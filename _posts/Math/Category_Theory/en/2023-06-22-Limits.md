## Definitions

We start with the following definition. For any two categories $$\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}$$, a functor $$\mathcal{A}\rightarrow \mathcal{B}$$ is called a *constant functor* if every object of $$\mathcal{A}$$ is sent to a fixed object $$B$$ of $$\mathcal{B}$$, and every morphism is sent to $$\id_B$$. By a slight abuse of notation, we sometimes write the above constant functor as $$B:\mathcal{A}\rightarrow \mathcal{B}$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> Consider a diagram[^1] $$F:\mathcal{I}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}$$ defined in a category $$\mathcal{A}$$. For an object $$A\in \mathcal{A}$$ and the constant functor $$A: \mathcal{I}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}$$, a natural transformation $$\lambda:A \Rightarrow F$$ is called a *cone over $$F$$*, and $$A$$ is called the *apex* of this cone, and each $$\lambda_i:A \rightarrow F(i)$$ is called a *leg*. A cone in $$\mathcal{A}^\op$$ is called a *cocone*.

</div>

Fix an arbitrary diagram $$F:\mathcal{I}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}$$. If a cone over $$F$$ with apex $$A$$ and a morphism $$A' \rightarrow A$$ are given, then we can naturally define a cone over $$F$$ with apex $$A'$$. Thus, if we define

$$\Cone(A, F)=\{\text{cones over $F$ with apex $A$}\}$$

then $$\Cone(-,F)$$ becomes a contravariant functor from $$\mathcal{A}$$ to $$\Set$$. When $$\mathcal{I}$$ is the category made from the poset $$(\mathbb{Z},\leq)$$, drawing [Definition 1](#def1) gives the following.

![Cone](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-1.png){:style="width:50em" class="invert" .align-center}

The fact that each triangle commutes follows from $$\lambda$$ being a natural transformation. Now if a morphism $$A' \rightarrow A$$ is given, then $$\Cone(A',F)$$ is defined as in the figure below.

![Functoriality_of_cone](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-2.png){:style="width:32em" class="invert" .align-center}

Therefore, when the contravariant functor $$\Cone(-,F): \mathcal{A}\rightarrow \Set$$ is representable, we define its representation as the limit of $$F$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def2">**Definition 2**</ins> The representation $$\lambda:\lim F \Rightarrow F$$ of the functor $$\Cone(-,F)$$ is called the *limit* of $$F$$.

</div>

Similarly, if a cocone over $$F$$ is given, we can define the covariant functor $$\Cone(F,-):\mathcal{A}\rightarrow \Set$$, and its representation is called a *colimit*.

## Universal Property of Limits

Meanwhile, in [§Representable Functors, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/category_theory/representable_functors#prop8) we saw how to represent a representation using a universal property. To apply this to our situation, consider the category of elements of the functor $$\Cone(-,F):\mathcal{A}\rightarrow \Set$$.

- The objects of $$\int\Cone(-,F)$$ are pairs $$(B, \mu)$$. Here $$B$$ is an object of $$\mathcal{A}$$, and $$\mu\in\Cone(B,F)$$.
- A morphism $$(B,\mu)\rightarrow (B',\mu')$$ in $$\int\Cone(-,F)$$ is a morphism $$f:B' \rightarrow B$$ satisfying the condition $$\Cone(f,F)(\mu)=\mu'$$.

That is, $$\int\Cone(-,F)$$ can be thought of as the category of (all possible apexes of) cones. Now, thinking of the dual version of [§Representable Functors, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/category_theory/representable_functors#prop8), the limit $$\lambda:\lim F\Rightarrow F$$ of $$F$$ is the terminal object of $$\int\Cone(-,F)$$. For instance, spelling this out a bit more for the previous example, given the following commutative diagram

![universal_property-1](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-3.png){:style="width:32em" class="invert" .align-center}

each time there must exist a unique $$f:B \rightarrow\lim F$$ making the following diagram commute.

![universal_property-2](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-4.png){:style="width:32em" class="invert" .align-center}

Of course, there is no guarantee that the limit (or colimit) of an arbitrary diagram exists in general.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> A diagram $$F:\mathcal{I}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}$$ is called *small* if the indexing category $$\mathcal{I}$$ is small. A category $$\mathcal{A}$$ in which every small diagram always has a limit is called a *complete category*. A category $$\mathcal{A}$$ in which every small diagram always has a colimit is called a *cocomplete category*. A functor preserving small limits is called a *continuous functor*, and a functor preserving small colimits is called a *cocontinuous functor*.

</div>

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex4">**Example 4**</ins> $$\Set$$ is a complete category. What is crucially used in proving this fact is the natural isomorphism of [§Representable Functors, ⁋Example 2](/en/math/category_theory/representable_functors#ex2)

$$A\cong\Hom_\Set(\ast, A)\qquad\text{for all $A\in\Set$}\tag{1}$$

Let an arbitrary (small) diagram $$F: \mathcal{I}\rightarrow \Set$$ be given. If the limit of $$F$$ exists, then

$$\Cone(A,F)\cong \Hom_\Set(A,\lim F)\qquad\text{for all $A\in\Set$}$$

and in particular $$\Cone(\ast,F)\cong\Hom_\Set(\ast,\lim F)\cong \lim F$$, so what remains is to find a natural transformation $$\lambda:\lim F\Rightarrow F$$ and show that this cone is universal. A natural transformation $$\lambda:\lim F\Rightarrow F$$ is determined by functions $$\lambda_i:\lim F\rightarrow F(i)$$ satisfying the commuting conditions, so for this we first need to think about the elements of $$\Cone(\ast,F)$$.

An element $$\mu:\ast\Rightarrow F$$ of $$\Cone(\ast,F)$$ is likewise a collection of legs $$\mu_i:\ast\rightarrow F(i)$$ satisfying the commuting conditions. But these $$\mu_i$$ can again be regarded as elements of $$F(i)$$ by equation (1), so for each $$i\in\mathcal{I}$$ defining

$$\Cone(\ast,F)\overset{\lambda_i}{\longrightarrow}F(i);\quad (\mu:\ast\Rightarrow F)\mapsto \mu_i\in F(i)\qquad\text{for all $i\in \mathcal{I}$}$$

and collecting them gives a natural transformation $$\lambda:\lim F\Rightarrow F$$ satisfying the desired condition.

</div>

## Examples of Limits

Depending on the shape of the category $$\mathcal{I}$$, limits are called by various names.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex5">**Example 5 (Inverse limit)**</ins> Consider the special case where $$\mathcal{I}=\omega^\op$$. Here $$\omega$$ is the category defined using the poset structure on the ordinal $$\omega$$, so a diagram indexed by this category has the following form

![inverse_limit](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-5.png){:style="width:14em" class="invert" .align-center}

The limit of this diagram is called an *inverse limit*; intuitively, drawing $$\lim F$$ gives the following figure

![inverse_limit-2](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-6.png){:style="width:24em" class="invert" .align-center}

and since $$\lim F$$ looks <em>small</em> compared to all the $$F(i)$$, it is worthy of being called a limit.

</div>

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex6">**Example 6 (Product)**</ins> A *discrete category* is a category that has no morphisms other than identity morphisms. Then for cones $$\lambda:A\Rightarrow F$$ over this diagram, the naturality of $$\lambda$$ imposes no conditions, so they are just families of morphisms $$(\lambda_i:A \rightarrow F(i))_{i\in \mathcal{I}}$$. The limit $$\pi:\lim F\Rightarrow F$$ of a diagram indexed by a discrete category is called a *product*, the $$\pi_i$$ are called *projections*, and in this case we use notation such as $$\prod F(i)$$ instead of $$\lim F$$.

</div>

Especially in the case of [Example 6](#ex6), rewriting the universal property of the limit cone $$\pi:\prod F(i)\Rightarrow F$$ gives the following.

> For any object $$A$$ of $$\mathcal{A}$$ and morphisms $$\lambda_i:A \rightarrow F(i)$$, there exists a unique $$A \rightarrow \prod F(i)$$ making the following diagram
>
> ![universal_property_of_product](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-7.png){:style="width:14em" class="invert" .align-center}
>
> commute.

To aid understanding, restricting this example to the case of limits of sets defined in [Example 4](#ex4), this universal property is exactly the same as the universal property of the product of sets defined in [\[Set Theory\] §Product of Sets, ⁋Theorem 3](/en/math/set_theory/product_of_sets#thm3).

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex7">**Example 7 (Equalizer)**</ins> Consider the following category

![equlizer_indexing_category](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-8.png){:style="width:4.5em" class="invert" .align-center}

Then a diagram indexed by this category has the form

![equalizer_diagram](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-9.png){:style="width:5.5em" class="invert" .align-center}

and saying that the following diagram

![equalizer_cone](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-10.png){:style="width:6em" class="invert" .align-center}

is a cone means the two conditions $$\lambda_C=f\circ\lambda_B$$, $$\lambda_C=g\circ\lambda_B$$ are satisfied, and thus $$\lambda_B\circ f=\lambda_B\circ g=\lambda_C$$. If $$A$$ is a cone, then $$\lambda_C$$ is defined from $$\lambda_B$$ in this way, so the only valid information is $$\lambda_B:A \rightarrow B$$ satisfying $$\lambda_B\circ f=\lambda_B\circ g$$. Then the limit of this diagram means the universal one among such cones.

</div>

Combining [Example 6](#ex6) and [Example 7](#ex7) appropriately, the limit of a diagram $$\mathcal{I}\rightarrow \Set$$ in $$\Set$$ can be written as a suitable equalizer diagram. As we saw in [Example 4](#ex4), an element $$\lambda\in\lim F=\Cone(\ast,F)$$ of the limit cone is a $$(\lambda_i)_{i\in\mathcal{I}}$$ making the following diagram commute for every morphism $$f$$ of $$\mathcal{I}$$:

![equalizer_seq](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-11.png){:style="width:16em" class="invert" .align-center}

That is, in other words, $$\lim F$$ is the collection of elements of $$\prod F(i)$$ satisfying the above condition, so define two functions $$a,b$$ from $$\prod_{i\in\mathcal{I}}F(i)$$ to $$\prod_{f\in\Hom(\mathcal{I})}F(\operatorname{cod}f)$$ by sending $$\lambda=(\lambda_i)_{i\in\mathcal{I}}\in\prod_{i\in\mathcal{I}}F(i)$$ to

- $$a(\lambda)$$ being the element $$(\lambda_{\operatorname{cod}f})_f$$ of $$\prod_{f\in\Hom(\mathcal{I})}F(\operatorname{cod}f)$$ whose $$f\in\Hom(\mathcal{I})$$-th component is $$\lambda_{\operatorname{cod}f}\in F(\operatorname{cod}f)$$,
- $$b(\lambda)$$ being the element $$(F(f)(\lambda_{\operatorname{dom}f}))_f$$ of $$\prod_{f\in\Hom(\mathcal{I})}F(\operatorname{cod}f)$$ whose $$f\in\Hom(\mathcal{I})$$-th component is $$F(f)(\lambda_{\operatorname{dom}f})\in F(\operatorname{cod}f)$$.

Then the commutativity of the above diagram exactly requires these two to be equal, and therefore $$\lim F$$ can be obtained through the following equalizer limit diagram

![equalizer_limit_diagram](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-12.png){:style="width:21em" class="invert" .align-center}

We did use properties of the category $$\Set$$ in this proof, but we can rewrite this in the language of category theory in a suitable way, and thus show that an arbitrary category having products and equalizers also has limits.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex8">**Example 8 (Fiber product)**</ins> Finally, consider the following category

![fiber_indexing_category](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-13.png){:style="width:4.8em" class="invert" .align-center}

Then a diagram indexed by this category has the following form

![fiber_diagram](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-14.png){:style="width:5.6em" class="invert" .align-center}

and the limit of this diagram is given as the universal $$A\overset{a}{\longleftarrow} X\overset{b}{\longrightarrow}B$$ among those satisfying the condition $$g\circ b=f\circ a$$. This is called a *fiber product* and denoted by $$A\times_C B$$. The following diagram representing this

![fiber_diagram_limit](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-15.png){:style="width:8.4em" class="invert" .align-center}

is called a *fiber diagram*, and to indicate that this is a fiber diagram, a corner symbol $$\lrcorner$$ is drawn on the fiber product side.

</div>

Replacing the above examples with colimits gives the notions of direct limit, coproduct, coequalizer, and fiber coproduct. In particular, a colimit can be represented as a suitable coequalizer diagram involving coproducts, and since $$\Set$$ has both coproducts $$\coprod$$ and coequalizers, we also see that $$\Set$$ is cocomplete.

## Limits and $$\Hom$$

Meanwhile, for any small diagram $$F: \mathcal{I}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}$$ and any $$A\in \mathcal{A}$$, write the composition

$$\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A,-)\circ F:\mathcal{I}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}\rightarrow \Set$$

as $$\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A,F-)$$. Then this is a diagram from $$\mathcal{I}$$ to $$\Set$$. By [Example 4](#ex4), since $$\Set$$ is a complete category, the limit $$\lim \Hom_\mathcal{A}(A,F-)=\Cone(\ast,\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A,F-))$$ of this diagram exists.

This consists of elements of $$\prod_{i\in \mathcal{I}}\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A, F(i))$$ satisfying the following compatibility condition for all $$f:i \rightarrow j$$

![compatibility](/assets/images/Math/Category_Theory/Limits-16.png){:style="width:12em" class="invert" .align-center}

so it is exactly $$\Cone(A,F)$$, and thus

$$\lim \Hom_\mathcal{A}(A, F-)\cong\Cone(A,F)$$

holds. Meanwhile, if $$F$$ has a limit then

$$\Cone(A,F)\cong\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A,\lim F)$$

holds, so we obtain the following theorem.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm9">**Theorem 9**</ins> If an arbitrary diagram $$F:\mathcal{I}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}$$ has a limit, then the following natural isomorphism

$$\lim\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A, F-)\cong\lim\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A, F-)$$

exists.

</div>

That is, $$\Hom_\mathcal{A}(A,-):\mathcal{A}\rightarrow\Set$$ is a continuous functor. The same claim holds for $$\Hom_\mathcal{A}(-, A)$$ and colimits.

## Associativity

Finally, we introduce the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop10">**Proposition 10**</ins> For any bifunctor $$F: \mathcal{I}\times \mathcal{J}\rightarrow \mathcal{A}$$, if both limits

$$\lim_{i\in \mathcal{I}}\lim_{j\in \mathcal{J}}F(i,j),\qquad \lim_{j\in \mathcal{J}}\lim_{i\in \mathcal{I}} F(i,j)$$

exist in $$\mathcal{A}$$, then they are isomorphic.

</div>

A similar theorem holds for colimits. That is, limits and colimits each satisfy associativity. However, in general the order of limits and colimits cannot be interchanged, and the best we can generally do is to use the universal property to obtain the following canonical morphism (which may not be an isomorphism)

$$\colim_{i\in \mathcal{I}}\lim_{j\in \mathcal{J}} F(i,j) \rightarrow \lim_{j\in \mathcal{J}}\colim_{i\in \mathcal{I}} F(i,j)$$
