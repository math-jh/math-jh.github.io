We now examine some additional properties of completion.

## Completion and Exact Sequences

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm1">**Theorem 1**</ins> Fix a Noetherian ring $$A$$ and an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$, and let $$\widehat{A}$$ be the $$\mathfrak{a}$$-adic completion of $$A$$. Then the following hold.

1. $$\widehat{A}$$ is Noetherian.
2. $$\widehat{A}/\mathfrak{a}^i\widehat{A}=A/\mathfrak{a}^i$$ for all $$i$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, since $$A$$ is Noetherian, $$A/\mathfrak{a}$$ is also Noetherian and $$\mathfrak{a}/\mathfrak{a}^2$$ is a finitely generated $$A/\mathfrak{a}$$-module. Hence $$\gr_\mathfrak{a}A$$ is generated by $$\mathfrak{a}/\mathfrak{a}^2$$ as an $$A/\mathfrak{a}$$-algebra, and therefore $$\gr_\mathfrak{a}A$$ is Noetherian by [##ref##](Hilbert_basis_thm). Now since $$\gr_{\widehat{\mathfrak{a}}}\widehat{A}=\gr_\mathfrak{a}A$$, we see that $$\gr_{\widehat{\mathfrak{a}}}\widehat{A}$$ is also Noetherian. On the other hand, for any ideal $$\widehat{\mathfrak{a}}\subseteq \widehat{A}$$, the initial ideal $$\initial(\widehat{\mathfrak{a}})$$ is generated by finitely many elements by the argument above, so the first result follows from [§Completion, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/commutative_algebra/completion#prop7).

For the second result, we again use [§Completion, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/commutative_algebra/completion#prop7) to see that $$\widehat{\mathfrak{a}}^i=\mathfrak{a}^i \widehat{A}$$ is equivalent to their initial ideals being equal, and thus we obtain the desired result.

</details>

The following lemma can be proved easily by examining the topological structure and base of the completion discussed in [§Completion, §§The $$\mathfrak{a}$$-adic Topology](/en/math/commutative_algebra/completion#the-mathfraka-adic-topology), and using [\[Topology\] §Bases of a Topological Space, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/topology/topological_bases#prop2) to determine when two bases define the same topology.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem2">**Lemma 2**</ins> Let two filtrations of a ring $$A$$

$$\mathcal{J}:\qquad A=\mathfrak{a}_0\supseteq \mathfrak{a}_1\supseteq \mathfrak{a}_2\supseteq\cdots$$

and

$$\mathcal{J}': \qquad A=\mathfrak{a}_0'\supseteq \mathfrak{a}_1'\supseteq \mathfrak{a}_2'\supseteq\cdots$$

be given. If for each $$\mathfrak{a}_i$$ there exists some $$\mathfrak{a}_j'$$ with $$\mathfrak{a}_j'\subseteq \mathfrak{a}_i$$, and for each $$\mathfrak{a}_i'$$ there exists some $$\mathfrak{a}_j$$ with $$\mathfrak{a}_j\subseteq \mathfrak{a}_i'$$, then $$\widehat{A}_\mathcal{J}\cong \widehat{A}_{\mathcal{J}'}$$.

</div>

On the other hand, by [\[Category Theory\] §Limits, ⁋Proposition 10](/en/math/category_theory/limits#prop10), taking completion is left exact. The following lemma shows that under an appropriate finiteness hypothesis, taking completion is also right exact.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem3">**Lemma 3**</ins> Fix a Noetherian ring $$A$$ and an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$. Then for any short exact sequence of finitely generated $$A$$-modules

$$0 \rightarrow A \rightarrow B \rightarrow C \rightarrow 0$$

the sequence

$$0 \rightarrow \varprojlim A/\mathfrak{a}^i A \rightarrow \varprojlim B/\mathfrak{a}^i B \rightarrow \varprojlim C/\mathfrak{a}^i C \rightarrow 0$$

is exact.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

By the preceding argument, we only need to show that $$\varprojlim B/\mathfrak{a}^i B \rightarrow \varprojlim C/\mathfrak{a}^i C$$ is surjective.

Let an element $$(c_i+\mathfrak{a}^i C)$$ of $$\varprojlim C/\mathfrak{a}^i C$$ be given, and let us find $$(b_i+\mathfrak{a}^iB)$$ satisfying the following two conditions:

- $$b_i\mapsto c_i\pmod{\mathfrak{a}^i C}$$,
- $$b_i\equiv b_j\pmod{\mathfrak{a}^iB}$$ for $$i<j$$.

It is clear that for fixed $$i$$ we can find $$b_i$$ satisfying the first condition, and that for the second condition it suffices to consider the case $$j=i+1$$. Thus suppose inductively that we have found $$b_1,\ldots, b_k$$ satisfying both conditions, and let us find $$b_{k+1}$$. First choose $$b_{k+1}'$$ such that $$b_{k+1}'\mapsto c_{k+1}\pmod{\mathfrak{a}^{k+1} C}$$. Then $$b_{k+1}'$$ and $$b_k$$ map to the same element in $$C/\mathfrak{a}^k C$$, so from the exact sequence

$$A/\mathfrak{a}^{k}A  \rightarrow B/ \mathfrak{a}^{k}B \rightarrow C/ \mathfrak{a}^{k}C \rightarrow 0$$

we can find suitable $$a\in A$$ mapping to $$b_k-b_{k+1}'$$ in $$B/\mathfrak{a}^kB$$. Now setting $$b_{k+1}=b_{k+1}'+\alpha_{k+1}(a_{k+1})$$ gives the desired result.

</details>

From this we obtain the following theorem.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm4">**Theorem 4**</ins> Fix a Noetherian ring $$A$$ and an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$, and let $$\widehat{A}$$ be the $$\mathfrak{a}$$-adic completion of $$A$$. Then the following hold.

1. For any finitely generated $$A$$-module $$M$$,

    $$\widehat{A}\otimes_A M \rightarrow\varprojlim_i M/\mathfrak{a}^iM$$

    is an isomorphism.
2. $$\widehat{A}$$ is a flat $$A$$-module.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since both $$\varprojlim$$ and $$\otimes$$ commute with finite direct sums, the first result is obvious when $$M$$ is a finitely generated free module. Now for any finitely generated $$A$$-module $$M$$, applying $$\widehat{A}\otimes_A-$$ to a free presentation

$$F \rightarrow G \rightarrow M \rightarrow 0$$

yields the desired result by [Lemma 3](#lem3) and [\[Homological Algebra\] §Diagram chasing, ⁋Proposition 1 (The four lemma)](/en/math/homological_algebra/diagram_chasing#prop1).

For the second result, by [§Flatness, ⁋Proposition 1](/en/math/commutative_algebra/flatness#prop1) it suffices to show that $$\widehat{\mathfrak{a}} \rightarrow \widehat{A}$$ is injective for any finitely generated ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$, and this is obvious from the left exactness of completion examined in [Lemma 3](#lem3).

</details>

## Hensel's Lemma

A typical example of a complete ring is the ring of formal power series $$A[[\x_i]]_{i\in I}$$ discussed in [§Completion, ⁋Example 4](). On the other hand, we saw in [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Algebras, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/algebraic_structures/algebras#prop8) that the ring of power series $$A[\x_i]_{i\in I}$$ plays the role of the free functor $$\Set \rightarrow \cAlg{A}$$, and a similar universal property holds for $$A[[\x_i]]_{i\in I}$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm5">**Theorem 5**</ins> Fix a ring $$A$$ and an $$A$$-algebra $$E$$, and suppose $$E$$ is complete with respect to some ideal $$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq E$$. Let $$\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_n\in \mathfrak{a}$$. Then the following hold.

1. There exists a unique $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$\phi:A[[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]]\rightarrow E$$ sending each $$x_i$$ to $$\alpha_i$$.
2. If $$A \rightarrow E/\mathfrak{a}$$ is an epimorphism and the $$\alpha_i$$ generate $$\mathfrak{a}$$, then $$\phi$$ is also an epimorphism.
3. If $$\gr\phi: R[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]\cong \gr_{(\x_1,\ldots, \x_n)}R[[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]] \rightarrow \gr_\mathfrak{a}E$$ is a monomorphism, then so is $$\phi$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The first assertion is almost identical to [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Algebras, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/algebraic_structures/algebras#prop8), and as in that proposition it holds for infinitely many variables as well.

For the second assertion, from the given hypothesis we know that

$$(\x_1,\ldots, \x_n)/(\x_1,\ldots, \x_n)^2 \rightarrow \mathfrak{a}/\mathfrak{a}^2$$

is surjective, and since $$\mathfrak{a}/\mathfrak{a}^2$$ generates $$\gr_\mathfrak{a}E$$, we see that $$\gr\phi$$ is surjective. Now for any $$y\in E$$, there exists a largest $$i$$ such that $$y\in \mathfrak{a}^i$$. Hence there exists $$x_1\in (\x_1,\ldots, \x_n)^i$$ whose initial form maps under $$\gr\phi$$ to the initial form of $$y$$, and then $$y-\phi(x_1)\in \mathfrak{a}^{i+1}$$. Repeating this process, we can find an infinite family $$(x_1,x_2,\ldots)$$ of elements of $$A[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$ such that

$$y=\sum_{j=1}^\infty \phi(x_j)=\phi\left(\sum_{j=1}^\infty x_j\right).$$

For the last assertion, if $$x$$ is a nonzero element of $$A[[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]]$$, then $$\initial(x)$$ is also nonzero, and hence $$(\gr\phi)(\initial(x))$$ is nonzero. Letting $$d$$ be the degree of $$\initial(x)$$,

$$x\equiv \initial(x)\pmod{(\x_1,\ldots, \x_n)^{d+1}}$$

so

$$\phi(x)\equiv(\gr\phi)(\initial(x))\pmod{\mathfrak{a}^{d+1}}$$

and thus $$\phi(x)\neq 0$$.

</details>

Then the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="cor6">**Corollary 6**</ins> Fix a power series $$f\in \x A[[\x]]$$ and define $$\phi: A[[\x]] \rightarrow A[[\x]]$$ by

$$\phi: A[[\x]] \rightarrow A[[\x]];\qquad \x\mapsto f.$$

Then $$\phi$$ is an isomorphism if and only if $$f'(0)$$ is a unit in $$A$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, the elements of $$A[[\x]]$$ not belonging to $$(\x)$$ are exactly those with nonzero constant term, and any $$\phi$$ of the given form preserves such elements. Now if $$\phi$$ is an isomorphism, then $$\phi((\x))=(\x)$$. Moreover $$\phi$$ must send a generator of $$(\x)$$ to a generator of $$(\x)$$, so we see that $$f+(\x^2)$$ must generate $$(\x)/(\x^2)$$, and since

$$f\equiv f'(0)\x\pmod{\x^2}$$

we conclude that $$f'(0)$$ must be a unit in $$A$$.

Conversely, suppose $$f'(0)$$ is a unit in $$A$$. Then $$\gr_{(\x)}A[[\x]]\cong \gr_{(\x)}A[\x]=A[\x]$$, and $$\gr\phi: A[\x] \rightarrow A[\x]$$ sends $$x$$ to $$ux$$ by definition. By the third result of [Theorem 5](#thm5), $$\phi$$ is injective, and writing $$f=u\x+h\x^2=(u+h\x)\x$$ for suitable $$h\in A[[\x]]$$, we see that $$f$$ generates $$(\x)$$. Hence by the second result of [Theorem 5](#thm5) we obtain the desired conclusion.

</details>

We thus obtain the following theorem, which is the central result of this section.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm7">**Theorem 7 (Hensel)**</ins> Let a ring $$A$$ be complete with respect to an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$, and let $$f(\x)\in A[\x]$$. If

$$f(a)\equiv 0\pmod{f'(a)^2 \mathfrak{a}}$$

then there exists $$b\in A$$ such that

$$f(b)=0,\qquad b\equiv a\pmod{f'(a)\mathfrak{a}}.$$

Moreover, if $$f'(a)$$ is a non-zerodivisor then such $$b$$ is uniquely determined.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For convenience write $$f'(a)=e$$. Then from

$$f(a+e\x)=f(a)+f'(a)e\x+\cdots$$

we can choose $$h$$ so that $$f(a+e\x)=f(a)+f'(a)e\x+h(x)(e\x)^2$$. Then

$$f(a+e\x)=f(a)+e^2(\x+\x^2h(\x))$$

and by the first result of [Theorem 5](#thm5), there exists a unique $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$\phi:A[[\x]] \rightarrow A[[\x]]$$ sending $$\x$$ to $$\x+\x^2h(\x)$$. On the other hand, by [Corollary 6](#cor6), $$\phi$$ is an isomorphism, and hence its inverse $$\phi^{-1}$$ exists. Applying $$\phi^{-1}$$ to the above equation yields

$$f(a+e\phi^{-1}(x))=f(a)+e^2x$$

and by the given hypothesis there exists $$\alpha\in \mathfrak{a}$$ such that $$f(a)=e^2\alpha$$. Using the first result of [Theorem 5](#thm5) again, let $$\psi: A[[\x]] \rightarrow A[[\x]]$$ be the $$A$$-algebra homomorphism sending $$\x$$ to $$-\alpha$$. Then from the above equation we obtain

$$f(a+e\psi\phi^{-1}(x))=0.$$

Thus setting $$b=e\psi\phi^{-1}$$ gives the desired result.

For uniqueness, assume $$e$$ is not a zero divisor and let $$b,b'$$ be two elements satisfying the given conditions. Then by definition they must be of the form $$a+er$$, $$a+er'$$. Applying the first result of [Theorem 5](#thm5), choose $$\beta,\beta': A[[\x]] \rightarrow A[[\x]]$$ sending $$\x$$ to $$r$$ and $$r'$$ respectively. Applying these gives

$$f(a)+e^2(r+r^2h(r))=f(a+er)=0=f(a+er')=f(a)+e^2(r'+(r')^2h(r'))$$

so we obtain $$\beta(\phi(\x))=\beta'(\phi(\x))$$. The desired result now follows from the fact that $$\phi$$ is an isomorphism and the uniqueness in [Theorem 5](#thm5).

</details>

Finally, we mention the following theorem and conclude.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm8">**Theorem 8 (Cohen structure theorem)**</ins> For a complete local Noetherian ring $$(A, \mathfrak{m})$$ with residue field $$\kappa$$, if $$A$$ contains some field then there exist suitable $$n$$ and ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ such that $$A\cong\kappa[[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]]/\mathfrak{a}$$.

</div>
