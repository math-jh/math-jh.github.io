## The Cayley-Hamilton Theorem

We begin by stating the generalized Cayley-Hamilton theorem.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm1">**Theorem 1**</ins> Fix a ring $$A$$ and an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq A$$, and let $$M$$ be an $$A$$-module generated by $$n$$ elements $$e_1,\ldots,e_n$$. Then for any $$u\in\End_\rMod{A}(M)$$, if $$u(M)\subseteq \mathfrak{a}M$$, there exists a monic polynomial

$$p(\x)=\x^n+p_1\x^{n-1}+\cdots+p_n,\qquad p_k\in \mathfrak{a}^k$$

such that $$p(u)=0$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

See [\[Multilinear Algebra\] §Determinants, ⁋Proposition 9](/en/math/multilinear_algebra/determinants#prop9); $$M$$ need not be a free module.

</details>

Then the following hold.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop2">**Proposition 2**</ins> For a ring $$A$$, suppose an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq A[\x]$$ is given, let $$B=A[\x]/\mathfrak{a}$$, and write $$b$$ for $$\x+\mathfrak{a}\in B$$. Then the following hold.

1. $$B$$ being generated by at most $$n$$ elements as an $$A$$-module is equivalent to $$\mathfrak{a}$$ containing a monic polynomial of degree at most $$n$$. In this case, $$B$$ is generated by $$1,b,\ldots,b^{n-1}$$.
2. $$B$$ being a free $$A$$-module is equivalent to $$\mathfrak{a}$$ being generated by a monic polynomial. In this case, $$1,b,\ldots,b^{n-1}$$ form a basis of $$B$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. One direction is obvious. Conversely, suppose $$B$$ is generated by $$n$$ elements as an $$A$$-module. Consider the $$A$$-module endomorphism $$b:B\rightarrow B$$ obtained by multiplication by $$b$$. Applying [Theorem 1](#thm1) to the ideal $$A$$, we see that this endomorphism satisfies a monic polynomial $$p(x)$$ of degree $$n$$, and substituting $$b$$ as an element must yield $$0$$. Therefore, by the definition of $$b$$, we know that $$p(\x)\in \mathfrak{a}$$.
2. First, suppose $$\mathfrak{a}$$ is generated by a monic polynomial of degree $$n$$. Then by the previous result, we know that $$B$$ is generated by $$1,b,\ldots,b^{n-1}$$. It remains to show that they are linearly independent. If $$\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i b^i=0$$ in the $$A$$-module $$B$$, then $$q(\x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}a_i\x^i$$ must belong to $$\mathfrak{a}$$, and because of the degree, $$q=0$$.  
Conversely, if $$B$$ is a free $$A$$-module of rank $$n$$, then $$B$$ can be generated by $$n$$ elements, and again by the previous result $$\mathfrak{a}$$ contains a monic polynomial $$p$$ of degree $$n$$, from which we can also deduce that $$1,b,\ldots,b^{n-1}$$ form a basis of $$B$$. It remains to show that $$p$$ generates $$\mathfrak{a}$$; for this, given any $$f\in \mathfrak{a}$$, consider the remainder $$r$$ upon dividing $$f$$ by $$p$$. Since both polynomials $$f$$ and $$p$$ belong to $$\mathfrak{a}$$, this remainder must also map to $$0$$ in $$B$$. But this is the same as substituting $$\x=b$$ into the polynomial $$r(\x)$$, and thinking of it as a linear combination of the basis $$1,\ldots,b^{n-1}$$ of $$B$$, we see that all coefficients of $$r$$ must be $$0$$.

</details>

## Integral Extensions

Previously, we saw that an $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ consists of the same data as a ring homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$. More precisely, a ring homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ gives the $$E$$-module $$E$$ an $$A$$-module structure $$\phi^\ast E$$ via restriction of scalars, and this $$A$$-module structure preserves the multiplicative structure of $$E$$ (by the commutativity of multiplication in $$E$$), so we may also view it as an $$A$$-algebra.

On the other hand, the composition of any ring homomorphisms is a ring homomorphism, and we know that for an $$A$$-algebra $$E$$, the ring homomorphism defined by the expression

$$A\overset{\phi}{\rightarrow}E\hookrightarrow E[\x]$$

gives $$E[\x]$$ an $$A$$-algebra structure. Therefore, by [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Algebras, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/algebraic_structures/algebras#prop8), a unique $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$\phi[\x]:A[\x]\rightarrow E[\x]$$ is defined, and concretely this is the function that takes any polynomial

$$p(\x)=a_n\x^n+\cdots+a_0\in A[\x]$$

and outputs the polynomial given by the expression

$$(\phi[\x](p))(\x)=\phi(a_n)\x^n+\cdots+\phi(a_0)\in E[\x].$$

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> Let a ring homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ be given.

1. If $$\phi$$ is injective, we call $$E$$ an *extension* of $$A$$.
2. An element $$x\in E$$ is called *integral* over $$\phi$$ if there exists a monic polynomial $$p\in A[\x]$$ such that $$(\phi[\x](p))(x)=0$$. If every element of $$E$$ is integral over $$\phi$$, we call $$\phi$$ an *integral homomorphism*. If an extension $$\phi$$ is an integral homomorphism, we call $$E$$ an *integral extension* of $$A$$.
3. The set of all elements of $$E$$ that are integral over $$\phi$$ is called the *integral closure* of $$A$$ in $$E$$, or the *normalization* of $$A$$ inside $$E$$. If $$A$$ is an integral domain, the integral closure of $$A$$ in $$\Frac(A)$$ is simply called the *normalization* of $$A$$. An integral domain $$A$$ is a *normal domain* if the normalization of $$A$$ is itself.
4. If the $$A$$-module $$\phi^\ast E$$ is a finitely generated $$A$$-module, we call $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ a *finite homomorphism*.
5. If the $$A$$-module $$\phi^\ast E$$ is finitely generated *as an $$A$$-algebra*, we call $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ a *finite type homomorphism*.

</div>

When the structure morphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ is clear from the context, we sometimes say that $$x$$ is integral over $$A$$ instead of integral over $$\phi$$. Similarly, in this case we say that $$E$$ is integral over $$A$$ instead of saying that $$\phi$$ is an integral homomorphism.

Any finite homomorphism is a finite type homomorphism by definition. On the other hand, for a finite homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$, the map $$x\times-:E\rightarrow E$$ is an $$A$$-algebra endomorphism, so applying [Theorem 1](#thm1) shows that $$x$$ is integral over $$\phi$$. That is, a finite homomorphism is also integral. The following lemma shows the converse.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem4">**Lemma 4**</ins> A ring homomorphism $$\phi$$ being finite is equivalent to $$\phi$$ being an integral homomorphism of finite type.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

One direction was shown above. For the converse, we must show that if $$E$$ is generated by finitely many integral elements, then $$E$$ is also finitely generated as an $$A$$-module. We use induction on the number of generators of $$E$$ as an $$A$$-algebra. Suppose $$E$$ is generated as an $$A$$-algebra by $$n$$ integral elements $$x_1,\ldots,x_n$$. We can consider the $$A$$-subalgebra $$E'$$ of $$E$$ generated by the $$n-1$$ elements $$x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}$$, which is finitely generated as an $$A$$-module by the inductive hypothesis. Let $$\{s_i\}$$ be generators of $$E'$$ as an $$A$$-module. Then since $$x_n$$ is integral over $$A$$, it is also integral over $$E'$$, and therefore $$E$$ is finitely generated as an $$E'$$-module. Let these elements be $$\{t_j\}$$; then we see that $$\{s_i t_j\}$$ finitely generate $$E$$ as an $$A$$-module.

</details>

Meanwhile, it is natural to expect a relationship between $$E$$ being integral over $$A$$ and each element of $$E$$ being integral. To this end, we first prove the following lemma.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem5">**Lemma 5**</ins> Let a ring homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ and an element $$x\in E$$ be given. Then $$x$$ being integral over $$A$$ is equivalent to the existence of an $$E$$-module $$N$$ and an $$A$$-submodule $$M$$ of $$N$$ such that $$M$$ is not annihilated by any nonzero element of $$E$$ and the inclusion $$xM\subseteq M$$ holds.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, suppose $$x$$ is integral over $$A$$. Then taking $$N=E$$, we know that $$M=A[x]$$ is finitely generated by [Proposition 2](#prop2). The converse is proved just as in the proof of [Proposition 2](#prop2), by viewing multiplication by $$x$$ as an endomorphism of $$M$$ and applying [Theorem 1](#thm1).

</details>

The following theorem is a property one would naturally expect, but it is almost impossible to prove directly from the definition without [Lemma 5](#lem5).

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm6">**Theorem 6**</ins> For an $$A$$-algebra $$E$$, the integral closure of $$A$$ in $$E$$ is again an $$A$$-algebra.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $$x,y\in E$$ be integral over $$A$$. We must show that $$x+y$$ and $$xy$$ are integral over $$A$$. Let $$M=A[x]$$ and $$M'=A[y]$$ be two submodules of $$E$$, and let $$MM'$$ be the subalgebra of $$E$$ generated by products $$xx'$$ of their elements. Since $$M$$ and $$M'$$ are each finitely generated, $$MM'$$ is also finitely generated. Now

$$(xx')MM'=(xM)(x'M)\subseteq MM',\qquad (x+x')MM'\subseteq xMM'+M(x'M')\subseteq MM',$$

so using [Lemma 5](#lem5) we obtain the desired result.

</details>

## Nakayama's Lemma

We now prove a very useful lemma. First, for any ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of a ring $$A$$, recall that the nilradical $$\sqrt{(0)}$$ of $$\mathfrak{a}$$ is given by the formula

$$\sqrt{(0)}=\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize$\mathfrak{p}$ prime} \mathfrak{p}.$$

([§Properties of Localization, ⁋Corollary 8](/en/math/commutative_algebra/properties_of_localization#cor8)) Similarly, we define the *Jacobson radical* of $$A$$ by the formula

$$J(A)=\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize$\mathfrak{m}$ maximal} \mathfrak{m}.$$

To prove Nakayama's lemma, we must first prove the following lemma.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem7">**Lemma 7**</ins> Let $$M$$ be a finitely generated $$A$$-module and $$\mathfrak{a}$$ an ideal of $$A$$ satisfying $$\mathfrak{a}M=M$$. Then there exists $$a\in \mathfrak{a}$$ such that $$(1-a)M=0$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

From the given condition we have $$M\subseteq \mathfrak{a}M$$, so by [Theorem 1](#thm1) there exists a monic polynomial

$$p(\x)=\x^n+p_1\x^{n-1}+\cdots+p_n,\qquad p_k\in \mathfrak{a}^k$$

such that $$p(\id_M)=0$$. That is,

$$(1+p_1+\cdots+p_n)M=0$$

and setting $$a=-(p_1+\cdots+p_n)$$ gives the desired result.

</details>

We can now finally state Nakayama's lemma.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem8">**Lemma 8 (Nakayama)**</ins> Suppose an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ contained in the Jacobson radical $$J(A)$$ of $$A$$ is given, and let $$M$$ be a finitely generated $$A$$-module. Then the following hold.

1. If $$\mathfrak{a}M=M$$, then $$M=0$$.
2. If the images of $$x_1,\ldots,x_n$$ in $$M/\mathfrak{a}M$$ generate $$M/\mathfrak{a}M$$ as an $$A$$-module, then $$x_1,\ldots,x_n$$ generate $$M$$ as an $$A$$-module.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

In the case of statement 1, we know that the element $$a\in \mathfrak{a}$$ obtained from [Lemma 7](#lem7) belongs to every maximal ideal by the assumption. In other words, $$1-a$$ cannot belong to any maximal ideal, so $$1-a$$ is a unit. Therefore we obtain the desired result.

In the case of statement 2, let $$N=M/\sum_i Ax_i$$. Then we can show that $$N/\mathfrak{a}N=0$$, and from statement 1 we know that $$N=0$$.

</details>

## Localization

We now examine some results related to localization.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop9">**Proposition 9**</ins> A unique factorization domain is a normal domain.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For any $$a/b\in \Frac(A)$$, suppose $$a$$ and $$b$$ are coprime and $$a/b$$ belongs to the normalization of $$A$$. Then there exists a monic polynomial such that

$$\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n+a_{n-1}\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)+a_0=0.$$

From this, we see that

$$\x^n+a_{n-1}b \x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1b^{n-1}\x+a_0b^n\in A[\x]$$

is a monic polynomial that vanishes at $$\x=a$$. That is, $$a^n$$ is divisible by $$b$$, and to avoid a contradiction we must have $$b=1$$; hence $$A$$ is a normal domain.

</details>

More generally, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop10">**Proposition 10**</ins> Suppose rings $$A\subseteq B$$ and a monic polynomial $$p\in A[\x]$$ are given. If monic polynomials $$q_1,q_2\in B[\x]$$ can be found such that $$p=q_1q_2$$ in $$B[\x]$$, then the coefficients of $$q_1$$ and $$q_2$$ are integral over $$A$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

By adjoining roots of the equations, we can find a ring $$C$$ containing $$B$$ such that in $$C[\x]$$ both $$q_1$$ and $$q_2$$ factor into products of the form $$\prod (x-\alpha_i)$$ and $$\prod(x-\beta_j)$$. Then by definition the $$\alpha_i$$ and $$\beta_j$$ are all integral over $$A$$, so the subring $$C'$$ of $$C$$ generated by them is an integral $$A$$-algebra. On the other hand, expanding $$p=q_1q_2$$ and looking at its coefficients, we see that they belong to $$C'$$.

</details>

Thus the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="cor11">**Corollary 11**</ins> For a normal domain $$A$$, any monic irreducible polynomial is prime.

</div>

Meanwhile, normalization commutes with localization, and the proof is also obvious.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop12">**Proposition 12**</ins> Fix rings $$A\subseteq B$$ and a multiplicative subset $$S$$ of $$A$$. Then for the integral closure $$A'$$ of $$A$$ in $$B$$, $$S^{-1}A'$$ is the integral closure of $$S^{-1}A$$ in $$S^{-1}B$$.

</div>

Another result related to localization is a slight strengthening of [§Properties of Localization, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/commutative_algebra/properties_of_localization#prop4). First, a ring $$A$$ is called a *semilocal ring* if it has only finitely many maximal ideals. Then the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop13">**Proposition 13**</ins> For a semilocal ring $$A$$ and finitely presented $$A$$-modules $$M,N$$, if $$M_\mathfrak{m}\cong N_\mathfrak{m}$$ holds for every maximal ideal $$\mathfrak{m}$$, then $$M\cong N$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, suppose the maximal ideals of $$A$$ are $$\mathfrak{m}_1,\ldots,\mathfrak{m}_n$$, and for each $$k$$ let $$u_k:M_{\mathfrak{p}_k}\rightarrow N_{\mathfrak{p}_k}$$ be given. Then $$A_{\mathfrak{m}}$$ is a flat $$A$$-module, and since $$M$$ is finitely presented by assumption, the following isomorphism

$$\Hom_{A_{\mathfrak{m}_k}}( M_{\mathfrak{m}_k}, N_{\mathfrak{m}_k}) \rightarrow \Hom_A(M,N)_{\mathfrak{m}_k}$$

exists. ([§Properties of Localization, ⁋Proposition 5](/en/math/commutative_algebra/properties_of_localization#prop5)) The image of $$u_k$$ under this isomorphism is obtained by putting an element of $$A\setminus \mathfrak{m}_k$$ into the denominator of an element of $$\Hom_A(M,N)$$; thus if necessary we may multiply $$u_k$$ by this denominator and regard $$v_k$$ as an element of $$\Hom_A(M,N)$$.

On the other hand, since $$\mathfrak{m}_k$$ is prime, if $$\bigcap_{l\neq k} \mathfrak{m}_l\subseteq \mathfrak{m}_k$$ then we would have $$\mathfrak{m}_l\subseteq \mathfrak{m}_k$$ for some $$l$$, which is a contradiction. Thus the formula

$$\bigcap_{l\neq k} \mathfrak{m}_l\not\subseteq \mathfrak{m}_k$$

holds, and from this there exists $$a_k\in\bigcap_{l\neq k}\mathfrak{m}_l$$ with $$a_k\not\in\mathfrak{m}_k$$. For the $$a_k$$ constructed in this way, define $$v=\sum_{k=1}^n a_kv_k$$; this becomes the desired isomorphism, and to prove it we need only look at the localization at each maximal ideal $$\mathfrak{m}_k$$ by [§Properties of Localization, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/commutative_algebra/properties_of_localization#prop4).

More generally, we show that for any local ring $$(B,\mathfrak{n})$$ and maps $$s,t:K\rightarrow L$$ between finitely generated $$B$$-modules, if $$s$$ is an isomorphism and $$t(K)\subseteq \mathfrak{n}L$$, then $$s+t$$ is also an isomorphism. Applying this result to the local ring $$(A_{\mathfrak{m}_k},\mathfrak{m}_kA_{\mathfrak{m}_k})$$ and the maps $$s=a_k v_k$$ and $$t=\sum_{l\neq k} a_l v_l$$ from $$M_{\mathfrak{m}_k}$$ to $$N_{\mathfrak{m}_k}$$ will complete the proof.

Let us prove this claim. First, $$t$$ can be viewed as the zero map from $$K$$ to $$L/\mathfrak{n}L$$, and $$s$$ can be viewed as an epimorphism from $$K$$ to $$L/\mathfrak{n}L$$; therefore $$s+t$$ can also be viewed as an epimorphism from $$K$$ to $$L/\mathfrak{n}L$$. Then by [Lemma 8](#lem8), the morphism $$s+t:K\rightarrow L$$ is also an epimorphism. Now take the inverse $$s^{-1}$$ of the isomorphism $$s$$ and consider the surjective endomorphism $$s^{-1}(s+t):K\rightarrow K$$. Then by [Theorem 1](#thm1), $$s^{-1}(s+t)$$ is also an isomorphism, and therefore $$s+t$$ is a monomorphism, giving the desired result.

</details>

Now let us examine a bit more the properties of ring homomorphisms from [Definition 3](#def3). We begin with the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop14">**Proposition 14**</ins> Let ring homomorphisms $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ and $$\rho:A\rightarrow A'$$ be given. If $$E'=A'\otimes_A E$$, then the following hold for $$\rho_!\phi:A'\rightarrow E'$$.

1. If $$\phi$$ is integral, then $$\rho_!\phi$$ is also integral.
2. If $$\phi$$ is finite, then $$\rho_!\phi$$ is also finite.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The proofs of both statements are similar, so we prove only the first. Assume $$\phi$$ is integral, and let $$x_i$$ be integral elements generating $$E$$. Then each $$x_i$$ satisfies a monic polynomial $$p_i\in A[\x]$$. Therefore each $$1\otimes x_i$$ generates $$E'$$ and satisfies $$(\rho\[\x\])(p_i)$$. From this we know that $$E'$$ is integral over $$A'$$.

</details>

Also, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop15">**Proposition 15**</ins> Let a ring homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ be given. If $$(a_1,\ldots,a_n)=A$$, then the following hold.

1. If each $$A_{a_i}\rightarrow E_{a_i}$$ is integral, then so is $$A\rightarrow E$$.
2. If each $$A_{a_i}\rightarrow E_{a_i}$$ is finite, then so is $$A\rightarrow E$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

As above, the proofs of the two statements are similar, so we prove only the first. For any $$x\in E$$, let $$\mathfrak{A}$$ be the ideal of polynomials in $$A[\x]$$ satisfying $$p(x)=0$$. Then we can check that the subset $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of $$A$$ consisting of the leading coefficients of these polynomials is an ideal of $$A$$.

On the other hand, from the assumption that each $$A_{a_i}\rightarrow E_{a_i}$$ is integral, the image of $$x$$ in $$E_{a_i}$$ is integral over $$A_{a_i}$$; thus writing its integral equation and clearing the powers of $$a_i$$ in the denominators, we know that for each $$i$$ there exists a suitable $$n_i$$ such that $$a_i^{n_i}\in\mathfrak{a}$$. Moreover, raising both sides of the equation

$$1=\sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i a_i$$

to a sufficiently high power, we see that these $$a_i^{n_i}$$ also generate the unit ideal, and therefore $$1\in\mathfrak{a}$$. Hence $$x$$ is integral by definition.

</details>

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop16">**Proposition 16**</ins> For a ring homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow E$$ and $$x\in E$$, $$x$$ being integral over $$\phi$$ is equivalent to the image of $$x$$ in $$E_\mathfrak{p}$$ being integral over $$\phi_\mathfrak{p}:A_\mathfrak{p}\rightarrow E_\mathfrak{p}$$ for every prime ideal $$\mathfrak{p}$$ of $$A$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

One direction follows using [Proposition 14](#prop14). For the converse, suppose that for every prime ideal $$\mathfrak{p}\subset A$$, the image of $$x$$ in $$E_\mathfrak{p}$$ is integral over $$A_\mathfrak{p}$$; we show that $$x$$ is integral. To do this, we take the $$A$$-subalgebra $$E'$$ of $$E$$ generated by $$\phi(A)$$ and $$x$$, and show that $$A\rightarrow E'$$ is integral.

First, from the assumption, there exist $$a_i\in A_\mathfrak{p}$$ such that in $$E_\mathfrak{p}$$ the equation

$$x^d+\phi_\mathfrak{p}(a_{d-1})x^{d-1}+\cdots+\phi_\mathfrak{p}(a_0)=0$$

holds. Now, taking a common denominator for the $$\phi_\mathfrak{p}(a_k)$$, we may view all the $$a_k$$ as belonging to $$A_f$$ for a suitable $$f\in A\setminus\mathfrak{p}$$; then the above equation holds in $$E_f$$, and from this we know that $$A_f\rightarrow E_f'$$ is a finite homomorphism.

</details>
