Given a connected reductive algebraic group $$G$$ and its Borel subgroup $$B$$, the most fundamental tool for understanding the structure of $$G$$ is the Bruhat decomposition

$$G=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}BwB$$

This decomposition indexes the $$B\times B$$-orbits of $$G$$ by elements of the Weyl group $$W$$, and naturally yields a cell decomposition of the flag variety $$G/B$$. In this post, after first understanding the Bruhat decomposition itself precisely, we examine the process of extending it to a parabolic subgroup $$P\supseteq B$$ to obtain a cell decomposition of the partial flag variety $$G/P$$. In particular, we introduce the concrete correspondence realizing the Grassmannian $$Gr_{n-k}(\mathbb{C}^n)$$ as $$GL_n/P_k$$, and the Schubert cells and Schubert varieties defined on it.

## Reductive Groups and Weyl Groups

In this post we mainly deal with a connected reductive algebraic group $$G$$ defined over an algebraically closed field. We saw the notion of Lie groups in [\[Lie Theory\] §Lie Groups](/en/math/lie_theory/Lie_groups), studied the structure of its Lie algebra in [\[Lie Theory\] §Root Systems](/en/math/lie_theory/root_systems), and introduced the Borel subgroups and flag varieties naturally obtained from these in [\[Lie Theory\] §Borel Subgroups and Flag Varieties](/en/math/lie_theory/borel_subgroup). In this section we briefly review these concepts in the context of algebraic groups.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> An algebraic group $$G$$ is called *reductive* if its unipotent radical is trivial. Equivalently, $$G$$ is given as an extension of a torus by a semisimple group.

</div>

Fixing a reductive group $$G$$, we fix a *Borel subgroup* $$B$$ of $$G$$ and a *maximal torus* $$T$$ contained in $$B$$. A Borel subgroup is a maximal connected solvable subgroup of $$G$$, and all Borel subgroups are conjugate to one another. A maximal torus $$T$$ is a maximal connected diagonalizable subgroup of $$B$$, and likewise all maximal tori are conjugate.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def2">**Definition 2**</ins> Denoting the normalizer of a maximal torus $$T$$ by $$N_G(T)$$, the *Weyl group* $$W$$ is defined as the following quotient group.

$$W=N_G(T)/T$$

</div>

The Weyl group $$W$$ naturally acts on the character lattice of $$T$$ by conjugation, and as we checked in [\[Lie Theory\] §Root Systems, ⁋Proposition 20](/en/math/lie_theory/root_systems#prop20), this action captures the symmetry of the root system $$\Phi$$. More importantly, $$W$$ carries the structure of a *Coxeter group*. Specifically, fixing the positive root system $$\Phi^+$$ determined by $$B$$ and the simple root system $$\Delta=\{\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_r\}$$, the finite group generated by the reflections $$s_i=s_{\alpha_i}$$ corresponding to each simple root $$\alpha_i$$ is precisely $$W$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop3">**Proposition 3**</ins> The Weyl group $$W$$ is generated by the simple reflections $$S=\{s_1,\ldots,s_r\}$$ and satisfies the following relations.

1. $$s_i^2=e$$ for all $$i$$.
2. $$(s_is_j)^{m_{ij}}=e$$ for $$i\neq j$$, where $$m_{ij}\in\{2,3,4,6\}$$.

Thus $$(W,S)$$ forms a Coxeter system.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

That $$W$$ is generated by reflections is part of the definition in [\[Lie Theory\] §Root Systems, ⁋Definition 17](/en/math/lie_theory/root_systems#def17), and its structure as a finite Coxeter group is obtained from the geometric properties of the root system. Specifically, the product $$s_is_j$$ of two simple reflections defines a rotation on the two-dimensional plane spanned by $$\alpha_i$$ and $$\alpha_j$$, and its angle is determined by the angle between the two roots. As we saw in [\[Lie Theory\] §Root Systems](/en/math/lie_theory/root_systems), the angle between two simple roots is one of $$90^\circ, 120^\circ, 135^\circ, 150^\circ$$, so $$m_{ij}$$ becomes $$2,3,4,6$$, respectively. That these relations alone completely determine $$W$$ follows from the general theory of Coxeter groups.

</details>

On a Coxeter system $$(W,S)$$, a *length function* $$\ell:W\rightarrow\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$$ is naturally defined. $$\ell(w)$$ is the minimum number of simple reflections needed to express $$w$$ as a product, and this coincides with the length of a *reduced expression* for $$w$$. Combinatorially, it is also given by $$\ell(w)=\lvert\Phi^+\cap w^{-1}\Phi^-\rvert$$. That is, $$\ell(w)$$ is the number of positive roots sent to negative roots by $$w$$.

## Bruhat Decomposition

The Bruhat decomposition is a fundamental theorem decomposing a reductive group $$G$$ into double cosets of a Borel subgroup $$B$$. This decomposition provides a cell decomposition of the flag variety $$G/B$$, and forms the basis for the Schubert varieties introduced later.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm4">**Theorem 4 (Bruhat Decomposition)**</ins> For a connected reductive algebraic group $$G$$, a Borel subgroup $$B$$, a maximal torus $$T\subset B$$, and the Weyl group $$W=N_G(T)/T$$, the following decomposition holds.

$$G=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}BwB$$

That is, $$G$$ is expressed as a disjoint union of double cosets $$BwB$$ of $$B$$, indexed by elements $$w$$ of the Weyl group $$W$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First we show that $$G=\bigcup_{w\in W}BwB$$. Thinking of $$B\times B$$ acting on $$G$$ by $$(b_1,b_2)\cdot g=b_1gb_2^{-1}$$, each $$BwB$$ becomes a single orbit. Since $$G$$ is an irreducible variety and the $$BwB$$ are locally closed subsets, one of maximal dimension among their closures must cover all of $$G$$. By the axioms of a BN-pair, $$G$$ is generated by $$B$$ and $$N=N_G(T)$$, and $$W=N/T$$ forms a complete set of representatives for the double cosets.

To show disjointness, assume $$BwB=BvB$$. Then $$w\in BvB$$, so $$w=b_1vb_2$$ for some $$b_1,b_2\in B$$. From this we get $$wBw^{-1}=b_1v(b_2Bb_2^{-1})v^{-1}b_1^{-1}=b_1vBv^{-1}b_1^{-1}$$. In particular, $$wBw^{-1}$$ and $$vBv^{-1}$$ are conjugate Borel subgroups. Meanwhile, since $$w\in N_G(T)$$, we have $$wTw^{-1}=T$$, and therefore $$T\subset wBw^{-1}\cap vBv^{-1}$$. If two Borel subgroups normalize each other and contain the same maximal torus $$T$$, then $$w^{-1}v$$ normalizes $$B$$. But the normalizer of $$B$$ is $$B$$ itself, so $$w^{-1}v\in B$$. Since also $$w^{-1}v\in N_G(T)$$, we have $$w^{-1}v\in B\cap N_G(T)=T$$. Thus $$w=v$$ in $$W=N_G(T)/T$$.

</details>

Each piece $$BwB$$ of the Bruhat decomposition is called a *Bruhat cell*, and its structure can be described more delicately as follows. Let $$U$$ be the unipotent radical of $$B$$, and let $$U^-$$ be the unipotent radical of the opposite Borel subgroup $$B^-$$. For each $$w\in W$$ define

$$U_w=U\cap wU^-w^{-1}$$

Then $$U_w$$ is given as a product of root subgroups $$U_\gamma$$, where $$\gamma$$ are the positive roots satisfying $$w^{-1}\gamma\in\Phi^-$$. That is,

$$U_w=\prod_{\gamma\in\Phi^+\,:\,w^{-1}\gamma\in\Phi^-}U_\gamma$$

and this is isomorphic to the affine space $$\mathbb{A}^{\ell(w)}$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop5">**Proposition 5**</ins> For each $$w\in W$$, the product map

$$U_w\times B\longrightarrow BwB,\qquad (u,b)\longmapsto u\dot{w}b$$

is an isomorphism of varieties, so that $$BwB\cong\mathbb{A}^{\ell(w)}\times B$$. Here $$\dot{w}$$ is a lift of $$w\in W=N_G(T)/T$$ in $$N_G(T)$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

$$B=U\rtimes T$$, and $$U$$ decomposes as a product of root subgroups. Decomposing $$U$$ as a product of $$U_w$$ and $$U_w'=U\cap wUw^{-1}$$, we have $$U=U_wU_w'$$ and $$U_w\cap U_w'=\{e\}$$. Since $$U_w'$$ corresponds to the roots sent from positive to positive by $$w^{-1}$$, we have $$w^{-1}U_w'w\subset U$$. Therefore $$BwB=UwB=U_w(wU_w'w^{-1})wB=U_wwB$$. Now if $$u_1w b_1=u_2w b_2$$, then $$w^{-1}u_2^{-1}u_1w=b_2b_1^{-1}\in B$$. Since $$u_2^{-1}u_1\in U_w$$, we have $$w^{-1}u_2^{-1}u_1w\in U^-$$, and thus $$w^{-1}u_2^{-1}u_1w\in B\cap U^-=\{e\}$$. Hence $$u_1=u_2$$ and $$b_1=b_2$$, so the map is bijective, and in fact an isomorphism of varieties. Since $$U_w$$ is a product of $$\ell(w)$$ root subgroups and each root subgroup is isomorphic to $$\mathbb{G}_a$$, we have $$U_w\cong\mathbb{A}^{\ell(w)}$$.

</details>

Projecting the Bruhat decomposition onto the flag variety $$\mathcal{F}=G/B$$ yields a cell decomposition. For each $$w\in W$$,

$$X_w^\circ=BwB/B\subset G/B$$

is a locally closed subset isomorphic to $$\mathbb{A}^{\ell(w)}$$, and their disjoint union covers $$G/B$$.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex6">**Example 6**</ins> Consider the case $$G=GL_n(\mathbb{C})$$. The Borel subgroup $$B$$ is the set of upper triangular matrices, and the maximal torus $$T$$ is the set of diagonal matrices. The Weyl group is $$W\cong S_n$$, and each $$w\in S_n$$ can be represented by a permutation matrix. The Bruhat decomposition $$GL_n=\bigsqcup_{w\in S_n}BwB$$ can be understood as a generalization of classical Gaussian elimination. Any invertible matrix $$g$$ can be transformed via elementary row and column operations into a ``canonical form'' centered at a permutation matrix $$w$$, and this $$w$$ determines the Bruhat cell to which $$g$$ belongs.

</div>

A similar decomposition exists for the opposite Borel subgroup $$B^-$$. This is called the *Birkhoff decomposition* or *opposite Bruhat decomposition*.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm7">**Theorem 7 (Birkhoff Decomposition)**</ins> Let $$B^-=w_0Bw_0$$ be the opposite Borel subgroup corresponding to $$B$$. Then the following holds.

$$G=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}B^-wB^-$$

More generally, letting $$B^+$$ and $$B^-$$ be two opposite Borel subgroups,

$$G=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}B^+wB^-$$

holds.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $$B^-=w_0Bw_0$$,

$$G=w_0Gw_0=w_0\left(\bigsqcup_{w\in W}BwB\right)w_0=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}(w_0Bw_0)(w_0ww_0)(w_0Bw_0)=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}B^-(w_0ww_0)B^-$$

Since $$w\mapsto w_0ww_0$$ is an automorphism of $$W$$, this gives the Bruhat decomposition of $$G$$ with respect to $$B^-$$.

To show a decomposition of the form $$B^+wB^-$$, substituting $$B^+=B$$ and $$B^-=w_0Bw_0$$ gives

$$BwB^-=Bww_0Bw_0=B(ww_0)Bw_0$$

so reindexing $$w\in W$$ by $$ww_0$$ yields the result. Thus $$G=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}BwB^-$$ holds.

</details>

## Parabolic Subgroups and Generalized Bruhat Decomposition

The Bruhat decomposition concerns Borel subgroups, but it can be extended to larger subgroups containing $$B$$, namely *parabolic subgroups*. A parabolic subgroup acts as the isotropy group of the *partial flag variety* $$G/P$$, which generalizes the flag variety, and plays a central role in realizing various homogeneous spaces—including Grassmannians—in a Lie-theoretic manner.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def8">**Definition 8**</ins> A subgroup $$P$$ of $$G$$ is called a *parabolic subgroup* if $$G/P$$ is a projective variety. Equivalently, $$P$$ is a connected closed subgroup containing some Borel subgroup.

</div>

The standard parabolic subgroups containing $$B$$ are in one-to-one correspondence with subsets $$I\subseteq\Delta$$ of the simple root system. Specifically, the parabolic subgroup $$P_I$$ corresponding to $$I$$ is defined by

$$P_I=BW_IB$$

where $$W_I=\langle s_i\mid\alpha_i\in I\rangle$$ is the *parabolic subgroup* of $$W$$ generated by the simple reflections in $$I$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop9">**Proposition 9**</ins> $$P_I=BW_IB=\bigsqcup_{w\in W_I}BwB$$ is a connected closed subgroup of $$G$$, obtained by adding to the Lie algebra of $$B$$ the negative root spaces corresponding to the simple roots in $$I$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $$B=U\rtimes T$$ and $$W_I\subset W$$, $$P_I$$ is the subgroup generated by $$B$$ and representatives of $$W_I$$. For each element $$w$$ of $$W_I$$, $$BwB$$ is a locally closed subset, and their union $$P_I$$ is a closed subset. In fact, $$P_I$$ admits a Levi decomposition $$P_I=L_I\ltimes U_I$$. Here the Levi factor $$L_I$$ is generated by $$T$$ and the root spaces corresponding to $$I$$, and the unipotent radical $$U_I$$ is generated by the root spaces of the positive roots not in $$I$$. Hence $$P_I$$ is a connected closed subgroup.

</details>

Given a parabolic subgroup $$P=P_I$$, to obtain the Bruhat decomposition on $$G/P$$ one must quotient the Weyl group $$W$$ by the parabolic subgroup $$W_I$$. The key players here are the *minimal length coset representatives*.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def10">**Definition 10**</ins> For a parabolic subgroup $$W_I\subseteq W$$, the *minimal length coset representatives* $$W^I$$ are defined as follows.

$$W^I=\{w\in W\mid\ell(ws)>\ell(w)\text{ for all simple reflections }s\in W_I\}$$

Equivalently, $$W^I$$ is the set of unique elements of minimal length in each left coset $$wW_I$$.

</div>

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop11">**Proposition 11**</ins> Each left coset $$wW_I$$ contains exactly one minimal length element. Therefore the natural projection $$W^I\rightarrow W/W_I$$ is a bijection.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For any $$w\in W$$, the existence of an element of minimal length in the coset $$wW_I$$ follows from the fact that the coset $$wW_I$$ of the finite group $$W_I$$ is nonempty. To show uniqueness, suppose $$u,v\in wW_I$$ both have minimal length. Then $$u=vw'$$ for some $$w'\in W_I$$. Since both $$u$$ and $$v$$ have minimal length, $$\ell(u)=\ell(v)\leq\ell(vs)$$ for all simple reflections $$s\in W_I$$. Meanwhile $$\ell(u)=\ell(vw')$$, and $$w'\in W_I$$ is expressed as a product of simple reflections. In a Coxeter group, $$\ell(vw')=\ell(v)+\ell(w')-2\cdot(\text{cancellation})$$, so for $$\ell(u)=\ell(v)$$ we must have $$\ell(w')=0$$, i.e. $$w'=e$$. Hence $$u=v$$.

</details>

Using minimal length coset representatives, we obtain a cell decomposition of $$G/P_I$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm12">**Theorem 12 (Generalized Bruhat Decomposition)**</ins> Let $$P=P_I$$ be a standard parabolic subgroup. Then the following holds.

$$G=\bigsqcup_{w\in W^I}BwP$$

Therefore the partial flag variety $$G/P$$ decomposes as

$$G/P=\bigsqcup_{w\in W^I}BwP/P$$

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

From the Bruhat decomposition $$G=\bigsqcup_{w\in W}BwB$$ and $$P=\bigsqcup_{v\in W_I}BvB$$ we get

$$G=\bigcup_{w\in W}BwP$$

$$G=\bigcup_{w\in W}BwP$$ is obtained directly from the Bruhat decomposition and $$P=\bigsqcup_{v\in W_I}BvB$$. Now suppose $$Bw_1P=Bw_2P$$ for $$w_1,w_2\in W^I$$. Then $$w_1\in Bw_2P$$, so $$w_1=b_1w_2p$$ for some $$b_1\in B$$, $$p\in P$$. Since $$p\in P$$, we have $$p=b'vb''$$ for some $$b',b''\in B$$, $$v\in W_I$$. Thus $$w_1\in Bw_2vB$$, and by the disjointness of the Bruhat decomposition, $$w_1=w_2v$$ in $$W$$. That is, $$w_1$$ and $$w_2$$ belong to the same coset $$wW_I$$. Since $$w_1,w_2\in W^I$$ and each coset contains a unique minimal length element, we have $$w_1=w_2$$.

</details>

Each cell $$BwP/P\subset G/P$$ is isomorphic to $$\mathbb{A}^{\ell(w)}$$, and their closures define *Schubert varieties*. We treat these more concretely in the next section.

## Grassmannian

The most representative example of the generalized Bruhat decomposition is the Grassmannian. Fix $$G=GL_n(\mathbb{C})$$, let the Borel subgroup $$B$$ be the set of upper triangular matrices, and let the maximal torus $$T$$ be the set of diagonal matrices. The Weyl group is $$W\cong S_n$$, and the simple reflections are the adjacent transpositions $$s_i=(i\;i+1)$$ ($$1\leq i\leq n-1$$).

<div class="definition"
