Now we examine the remaining results related to compactness and variations of this definition.

## Tychonoff's Theorem

An arbitrary product of compact spaces is again a compact space. If the product is finite, this result can be shown in a more intuitive way, but if the product is infinite, the following lemma is needed for this. This is a generalization of [§Compactness and Convergence of Filters, ⁋Proposition 5](/en/math/topology/filter_convergence#prop5) in the language of filters.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem1">**Lemma 1**</ins> A topological space $$X$$ is compact if and only if every ultrafilter converges.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, assume that $$X$$ is compact, and let an arbitrary ultrafilter $$\mathcal{F}$$ be given. Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that there is no limit point of $$\mathcal{F}$$. That is, for any $$x\in X$$, there exists an open neighborhood $$U_x$$ such that $$U_x\not\in \mathcal{F}$$. Then by the compactness of $$X$$, there exists a finite subcover $$U_{x_1},\ldots, U_{x_n}$$ of $$X$$.

On the other hand, by [[Set Theory] §Filters and Ideals, Galois Correspondence, ⁋Proposition 5](/en/math/set_theory/filter_and_ideal#prop5), $$\mathcal{F}$$ is prime. That is, for any subset $$A\subseteq X$$, exactly one of $$A\in \mathcal{F}$$ or $$X\setminus A\in \mathcal{F}$$ holds. Then for any $$A\in \mathcal{F}$$,

$$A=A\cap X=(A\cap U_{x_1})\cup \cdots\cup (A\cap U_{x_n})\in \mathcal{F}$$

and since $$U_{x_i}\not\in \mathcal{F}$$ by assumption, each $$A\cap U_{x_i}$$ also does not belong to $$\mathcal{F}$$, and since $$\mathcal{F}$$ is maximal, $$X\setminus (A\cap U_{x_i})\in \mathcal{F}$$ must hold. Then the finite intersection

$$X\setminus A=(X\setminus (A\cap U_{x_1}))\cap\cdots\cap (X\setminus (A\cap U_{x_n}))$$

must also belong to $$\mathcal{F}$$, which contradicts the assumption that $$\mathcal{F}$$ is maximal.

Conversely, suppose that for every ultrafilter $$\mathcal{F}$$, we can find a limit point $$x$$, and let $$\mathcal{A}$$ be a family of closed subsets of $$X$$ satisfying the finite intersection property. Then we can consider an ultrafilter $$\mathcal{F}$$ containing the filter generated by $$\mathcal{A}$$, and by assumption $$\mathcal{F}$$ has a limit point $$x$$. That is, $$\mathcal{N}(x)\subseteq \mathcal{F}$$, and thus for every $$F\in \mathcal{F}$$, there exists a neighborhood $$U$$ of $$x$$ such that $$U\cap F\neq\emptyset$$. In particular, for any $$A\in \mathcal{A}$$, there exists a neighborhood $$U$$ of $$x$$ such that $$A\cap U\neq\emptyset$$, and therefore $$x\in \cl(A)=A$$ always holds. From this we know that $$x\in\bigcap_{A\in \mathcal{A}}A$$, and thus we obtain the desired result by [§Compact Spaces, ⁋Proposition 11](/en/math/topology/compact_spaces#prop11).

</details>

Then the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm2">**Theorem 2 (Tychonoff)**</ins> The product $$X=\prod_{i\in I} X_i$$ of compact spaces $$(X_i)_{i\in I}$$ is compact. Conversely, if the product space $$X$$ is compact, then each $$X_i$$ is compact.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $$X$$ is compact, then the fact that each $$X_i$$ is compact is obvious from the continuity of $$\pr_i$$ and [§Compact Spaces, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/topology/compact_spaces#prop8).

For the converse direction, for any ultrafilter $$\mathcal{F}$$ defined on $$X$$, after verifying that $$\pr_i(\mathcal{F})$$ defines an ultrafilter base on $$X_i$$, we obtain a limit point $$x_i$$ of this ultrafilter from the assumption that $$X_i$$ is compact and [Lemma 1](#lem1), and since we can show that $$x=(x_i)_{i\in I}$$ is a limit point of $$\mathcal{F}$$, the proof is completed again by [Lemma 1](#lem1).

</details>

## Locally Compact Spaces

A space $$X$$ being compact is such a strong property that many of the spaces we deal with are not compact. For example, even considering just the real numbers $$\mathbb{R}$$, it is not compact if we consider the open cover consisting of intervals $$(n,n+2)$$. Therefore, it is necessary to weaken this condition somewhat.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> A topological space $$X$$ is *locally compact* at a point $$x\in X$$ if there exists a compact neighborhood $$A$$ of $$X$$ containing $$x$$. If $$X$$ is locally compact at every point, it is called a *locally compact space*.

</div>

Then by an argument similar to [§Compact Spaces, ⁋Lemma 6](/en/math/topology/compact_spaces#lem6), one can show that any locally compact Hausdorff space is regular. The following theorem gives important information about locally compact Hausdorff spaces.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm4">**Theorem 4 (Alexandroff)**</ins> Let a locally compact Hausdorff space $$X$$ be given. Then there exist a compact Hausdorff space $$X'$$, a point $$\ast_{X'}\in X'$$, and a homeomorphism $$f: X \rightarrow X'\setminus\{\ast_{X'}\}$$. Moreover, if another datum $$g: X \rightarrow X''\setminus\{\ast_{X''}\}$$ satisfying this condition is given, there exists a unique homeomorphism $$h: X' \rightarrow X''$$ such that $$g=h\circ f$$.

</div>

To make the proof a bit more intuitive, speaking in a slightly less rigorous way, if a locally compact Hausdorff space is given, we can add one point to it to make it a compact Hausdorff space, and this way of adding one point is unique. The new space obtained in this way is also called the *one-point compactification* of $$X$$.

<details class="proof--alone" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof of Theorem 4</summary>

First, let us show uniqueness. Naturally, it would be natural to define $$h$$ by sending $$f(x)$$ to $$g(x)$$ on $$X'\setminus\{\ast_{X'}\}$$ and $$\ast_{X'}$$ to $$\ast_{X''}$$. Then $$h$$ is continuous. To prove this, let $$V$$ be an arbitrary open subset of $$X''$$. If $$V$$ does not contain $$\ast_{X''}$$, then by definition

$$h^{-1}(V)=f(g^{-1}(V))$$

and since $$f$$ is a homeomorphism, $$h^{-1}(V)$$ is an open set in $$X'\setminus\{\ast_{X'}\}$$. On the other hand, since $$X'\setminus\{\ast_{X'}\}$$ is an open set because $$X'$$ is a Hausdorff space, by [§Subspaces, ⁋Lemma 2](/en/math/topology/subspaces#lem2), $$h^{-1}(V)$$ is an open set in $$X'$$.

If $$V$$ contains $$\ast_{X'}$$, then $$X''\setminus V$$ is a closed subset of $$X''$$, hence a compact subset, and thus also a compact subset of $$f(X)$$. Now since $$g$$ is a homeomorphism, $$g^{-1}(X''\setminus V)$$ is a compact subspace of $$X$$, and therefore the following set

$$h^{-1}(X''\setminus V)=f(g^{-1}(X''\setminus V))$$

is a compact subspace of $$X'$$, hence a closed set. ([§Compact Spaces, ⁋Corollary 5](/en/math/topology/compact_spaces#cor5)) Therefore, from the third condition of [§Continuous Functions, ⁋Theorem 4](/en/math/topology/continuous_functions#thm4), we know that $$h$$ is continuous.

From the above proof, if a topological space $$X'=X\cup \{\ast_{X'}\}$$ satisfying the given condition exists, then the open sets of $$X'$$ must be of the following two kinds

$$f(U)\quad\text{for $U$ open in $X$},\qquad X'\setminus f(C)\quad\text{for $C$ compact in $X$}$$

We know that they must be of these forms. Then we can easily show that this actually satisfies the conditions of [§Open Sets, ⁋Definition 1](/en/math/topology/open_sets#def1). It remains only to show that the topology given in this way is compact Hausdorff.

First, let us show that $$X'$$ is compact. If an arbitrary open covering $$(U_i)_{i\in I}$$ of $$X'$$ is given, this family must contain an open set $$U_j$$ containing $$\ast_{X'}$$, and by the topology defined above, this can be written as $$U_j=X'\setminus C$$ for some compact subset $$C$$ of $$X$$. On the other hand, consider the collection of open sets $$(f^{-1}(U_i))_{i\neq j}$$ of $$X$$. Then since $$(U_i)_{i\neq j}$$ must cover $$f(C)$$, $$(f^{-1}(U_i))_{i\neq j}$$ is an open covering of $$C$$, and from the assumption that $$C$$ is compact, we can choose a finite subcover and from this obtain a finite subcover of the open covering $$(U_i)_{i\in I}$$ of $$X'$$.

Now, to show that $$X'$$ is Hausdorff, let $$x,y\in X'$$ be any two points of $$X'$$. If both of them belong to $$f(X)$$, there is nothing to prove, so we may assume $$y=\ast_{X'}$$. Then there exists a compact neighborhood $$C$$ of $$X$$ containing $$x$$ and an open neighborhood $$U$$ of $$x$$ contained in $$C$$, so $$f(U)$$ and $$X'\setminus f(C)$$ become open sets separating $$x$$ and $$y$$.

</details>

## Topological Manifolds

Now let us weaken the finiteness required by compactness somewhat. For example, we can consider the situation where any open covering of a topological space $$X$$ has a *locally* finite open subcover. ([§Interior, Closure, and Boundary of Sets, ⁋Definition 3](/en/math/topology/other_concepts#def3)) However, by a slight play on words, one can show that this condition is in fact equivalent to the compactness of $$X$$, so this alone does not yield a new definition. Now, for two open covers $$(U_i)_{i\in I}$$ and $$(V_j)_{j\in J}$$ of a topological space, if there always exists a $$U_i$$ that entirely contains any $$V_j$$, we call $$(V_j)_{j\in J}$$ an *(open) refinement* of $$(U_i)_{i\in I}$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def5">**Definition 5**</ins> A topological space $$X$$ is *paracompact* if every open covering of $$X$$ has a locally finite open refinement.

</div>

If $$X$$ is compact, then a finite subcover of any open covering is immediately a locally finite open refinement, so any compact space is paracompact.

Almost all spaces we deal with are Hausdorff spaces. Although we will not prove it separately, one of the important properties characterizing a paracompact Hausdorff space is the existence of a *partition of unity*. ([Theorem 7](#thm7))

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def6">**Definition 6**</ins> Let a topological space $$X$$ be given. Then the set of continuous functions

$$\Phi=\{\phi:X \rightarrow [0,1]\mid \text{$\phi$ continuous}\}$$

is called a *partition of unity* if the following two conditions

1. For any $$x\in X$$, there exists an open neighborhood $$U$$ such that only finitely many $$\phi\in \Phi$$ satisfy $$\phi\vert_U\neq 0$$.
2. For any $$x\in X$$, $$\sum_{\phi\in \Phi} \phi(x)=1$$ holds.

hold. In particular, for an open covering $$(U_i)_{i\in I}$$ of $$X$$, a partition of unity $$\Phi=(\phi_i)_{i\in I}$$ satisfying $$\supp \phi_i\subseteq U_i$$ is called a *partition of unity subordinate to $$(U_i)$$*.

</div>

Thus the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm7">**Theorem 7**</ins> A topological space $$X$$ being a paracompact Hausdorff space is equivalent to the existence of a partition of unity subordinate to $$(U_i)$$ for any open cover $$(U_i)$$ of $$X$$.

</div>

The proof of this can be found on [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracompact_space). Instead of copying it verbatim, let us briefly look at how it is used. To do this, we first define a topological manifold. The model for a topological manifold is the space we know well, namely Euclidean space $$\mathbb{R}^m$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def8">**Definition 8**</ins> A topological space $$M$$ is *locally Euclidean of dimension $$m$$* if for every $$x\in M$$, there exists an open neighborhood $$U$$ of $$x$$ such that $$U$$ is homeomorphic to an open subset of $$\mathbb{R}^m$$.

</div>

This condition is the essence of a topological manifold, but since it is a weaker condition than one might think, we add some additional conditions.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def9">**Definition 9**</ins> A space that is second countable, Hausdorff, and locally Euclidean of dimension $$m$$ is called a *topological manifold of dimension $$m$$*.

</div>

Then there is another theorem we mention without proof.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm10">**Theorem 10**</ins> For any Hausdorff locally Euclidean topological space $$M$$, $$M$$ is second-countable if and only if $$M$$ is paracompact and has countably many connected components. ([§Connected Spaces, ⁋Definition 7](/en/math/topology/connected_spaces#def7))

</div>

We have not yet defined what a connected component is, but in any case, the above theorem logically implies that any topological manifold $$M$$ is paracompact Hausdorff, and therefore for any open cover $$(U_i)$$ of a topological manifold $$M$$, there exists a partition of unity subordinate to $$(U_i)$$. In particular, using the locally Euclidean condition, we can view the open cover $$(U_i)$$ as Euclidean spaces, and then a continuous function to $$\mathbb{R}$$ defined on it is an object we have known since learning calculus. Then by the formula

$$f=\sum \phi_i f_i$$

we can define a continuous function $$f$$ defined on the entire $$M$$, and conversely, if a continuous function $$f$$ defined on the entire $$M$$ is given, each $$\phi_i f$$ is a continuous function defined on $$U_i$$. In most cases, there is no particular problem in thinking of a topological manifold as having only one connected component, so in fact paracompactness can be considered an essential condition when dealing with manifolds.
