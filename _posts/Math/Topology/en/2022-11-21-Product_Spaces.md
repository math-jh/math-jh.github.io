## Definition and Properties of Product Spaces

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> Let $$(X_i)_{i\in I}$$ be a family of topological spaces. Their *product* is the topological space given by the product set $$X=\prod_{i\in I}X_i$$ equipped with the initial topology with respect to the projections $$\pr_i:X\rightarrow X_i$$.

</div>

Then by [§Initial and Final Topology, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/topology/initial_and_final_topology#prop2), the product topology on $$X$$ is the topology generated by taking the following collection as a subbase:

$$\mathcal{S}=\{\pr_i^{-1}(U_i)\mid U_i\text{ open in }X_i\}$$

In this case, the base $$\mathcal{B}$$ generated by $$\mathcal{S}$$ consists of sets of the form

$$\prod_{i\in I} U_i,\qquad \text{$U_i$ open in $X_i$, $U_i=X_i$ for all but finitely many $i$}$$

For the same reason as in [[Set Theory] §Properties of Products, ⁋Proposition 3](/en/math/set_theory/property_of_products#prop3), taking products of spaces satisfies the associative law, and similarly there is also a proposition concerning the commutative law. These are quite obvious, so we do not mention them separately.

Meanwhile, applying [§Initial and Final Topology, ⁋Proposition 3](/en/math/topology/initial_and_final_topology#prop3) yields the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop2">**Proposition 2**</ins> Let a product space $$X=\prod_{i\in I}X_i$$ and a topological space $$Y$$ be given, and let functions $$f_i:Y\rightarrow X_i$$ be given. Then the function $$f=(f_i): Y\rightarrow X$$ is continuous if and only if each $$f_i$$ is continuous.

</div>

From this we obtain the following two corollaries.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="cor3">**Corollary 3**</ins> Let two product spaces $$X=\prod_{i\in I}X_i$$ and $$Y=\prod_{i\in I}Y_i$$ with the same index set $$I$$ be given, and let $$f_i:X_i\rightarrow Y_i$$ be given. Then $$f:(x_i)\mapsto (f_i(x_i))$$ is continuous if and only if each $$f_i$$ is continuous.

</div>

For arbitrary sets $$X, Y$$ and a function $$f:X \rightarrow Y$$, the *graph* $$\Gamma(f)$$ of $$f$$ is the subset of $$X\times Y$$ given by

$$\Gamma(f)=\{(x,f(x)\mid x\in X\}\subseteq X\times Y$$

If both $$X$$ and $$Y$$ are topological spaces, then $$\Gamma(f)$$ inherits the subspace topology from the product space $$X\times Y$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="cor4">**Corollary 4**</ins> For topological spaces $$X,Y$$ and a function $$f:X\rightarrow Y$$, $$f$$ is continuous if and only if the function $$g:x\mapsto (x,f(x))$$ is a homeomorphism from $$X$$ onto $$\Gamma(f)$$.

</div>

Moreover, the obvious inverse of the function $$g$$ above is

$$\pr_X\vert_{\Gamma(f)}:\Gamma(f) \rightarrow X$$

In particular, the constant map sending every point of $$X$$ to $$y_0\in Y$$ is continuous, so by the above corollary we obtain a homeomorphism

$$X\rightarrow X\times\{y_0\}$$

On the other hand, consider an arbitrary subset $$A$$ of $$X\times Y$$. Then

$$A\cap (X\times \{y_0\})=\{(x,y)\mid (x,y)\in A,\quad y=y_0\}=\{(x,y_0)\mid (x,y_0)\in A\}$$

Since $$\Gamma(f)$$ carries the subspace topology from $$X\times Y$$, the above set is open in $$\Gamma(f)$$ if $$A$$ is open in $$X\times Y$$, and closed in $$\Gamma(f)$$ if $$A$$ is closed in $$X\times Y$$. Therefore, applying [Corollary 4](#cor4) again and mapping the above set to $$X$$, the corresponding set is also open or closed in $$X$$.

Let us denote this set by $$A(y_0)\subseteq X$$. Of course, by swapping the roles of $$X$$ and $$Y$$ in the same argument, we can also obtain a subset $$A(x_0)$$ of $$Y$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop5">**Proposition 5**</ins> For any open set $$U$$ in $$X\times Y$$, the projections $$\pr_X(U)$$ and $$\pr_Y(U)$$ are open in $$X$$ and $$Y$$, respectively.  

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

This is obvious from the above argument and the identities

$$\pr_X(U)=\bigcup_{y\in Y} U(y),\qquad \pr_Y(U)=\bigcup_{x\in X} U(x)$$

</details>

However, since an arbitrary union of closed sets need not be closed, the analogous statement for closed sets does not hold. Meanwhile, by [Corollary 4](#cor4) we also obtain the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop6">**Proposition 6**</ins> If a function $$f:X_1\times X_2 \rightarrow Y$$ is continuous at $$(a_1,a_2)\in X_1\times X_2$$, then the functions from $$X_1$$ to $$Y$$ and from $$X_2$$ to $$Y$$ defined by

$$x_1\mapsto f(x_1, a_2),\qquad x_2\mapsto f(a_1,x_2)$$

are continuous at $$x_1=a_1$$ and $$x_2=a_2$$, respectively. 

</div>

However, the converse of this proposition does not hold.

## Interior and Closure

Now we examine the relationship between product sets, interior, and closure. First, taking closures behaves well with respect to product sets. That is, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop7">**Proposition 7**</ins> Let $$X=\prod_{i\in I} X_i$$ be a product space, and let $$A_i$$ be arbitrary subsets of $$X_i$$. Then the following equality holds:

$$\prod_{i\in I} \cl A_i=\cl\left(\prod_{i\in I} A_i\right)$$

</div>
