## Rings

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> A monoid object in the symmetric monoidal category $$(\Ab,\otimes, \mathbb{Z})$$ is called a *ring*. ([§Abelian Groups, ⁋Definition 11](/en/math/algebraic_structures/abelian_groups#def11))

</div>

Following the convention for abelian groups, we write the operation of $$A$$ as $$+$$. In this situation, the multiplication $$\mu:A\otimes A \rightarrow A$$ of a ring $$A$$ is written as $$\cdot$$, and when there is no risk of confusion, we abbreviate $$\alpha\cdot \beta$$ simply as $$\alpha\beta$$. From

$$\Hom_\Ab(A\otimes A,A)\cong\Bilin(A,A;A)$$

we know that $$\mu$$ is bilinear. That is,

$$(\alpha+\beta)\gamma=\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma,\quad \alpha(\beta+\gamma)=\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma$$

holds. Also, $$\mu$$ is associative, and

$$\eta:\mathbb{Z}\rightarrow A$$

determines the (multiplicative) identity element $$1$$ of $$A$$ via the image of $$1\in \mathbb{Z}$$. Thus, a set $$A$$ is a ring precisely when there exist two binary operations $$+,\cdot$$ and elements $$0,1$$ satisfying the following conditions.

1. $$(A, +, 0)$$ is an abelian group.
2. $$(A,\cdot,1)$$ is a monoid.
3. Distributivity holds between $$\cdot$$ and $$+$$.


Depending on the author, the existence of a multiplicative identity may be omitted from the definition of a ring; we call such objects *pseudo-rings*, or *rng* (ring without the "i"). In any case, we will mostly use commutative rings (with unity).

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop2">**Proposition 2**</ins> For any elements $$\alpha,\beta$$ of a ring $$A$$, the following hold.

1. $$\alpha0=0\alpha=0$$,
2. $$\alpha(-\beta)=(-\alpha)\beta=-(\alpha\beta)$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. Since $$0$$ is the additive identity, from the equation
    
    $$0\alpha=(0+0)\alpha=0\alpha+0\alpha$$

    we obtain $$0\alpha=0$$. Similarly, we also obtain $$\alpha0=0$$.
2. By (1),
    
    $$0=\alpha0=\alpha(\beta+(-\beta))=\alpha\beta+\alpha(-\beta)$$

    and therefore $$-(\alpha\beta)=\alpha(-\beta)$$. Similarly, we also obtain $$(-\alpha)\beta=-(\alpha\beta)$$.

</details>

In general, we do not assume $$0\neq 1$$ in Definition 1, but if $$0=1$$, then by (1) of [Proposition 2](#prop2), for any $$\alpha\in A$$,

$$\alpha=\alpha\cdot 1=\alpha\cdot 0=0$$

holds, so we must have $$A=\{0\}$$. Such a ring is called the *zero ring*.

## Ring Homomorphisms

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> For two rings $$A,B$$, a function $$\phi:A \rightarrow B$$ is a *ring homomorphism* if for any $$\alpha,\beta$$,

$$\phi(\alpha+\beta)=\phi(\alpha)+\phi(\beta),\quad \phi(\alpha\beta)=\phi(\alpha)\phi(\beta),\quad \phi(1)=1$$

hold.

</div>

With these as morphisms, the categories

- $$\Ring$$ of rings,
- $$\Rng$$ of rngs,
- $$\cRing$$ of commutative rings

are defined, and so on. In $$\Ring$$ and $$\cRing$$, $$\mathbb{Z}$$ is an initial object and $$\{0\}$$ is a terminal object, whereas in $$\Rng$$, $$\{0\}$$ is a zero object.

By definition, a ring homomorphism $$\phi:A\rightarrow B$$ is also a group homomorphism between the abelian groups $$(A,+,0)$$ and $$(B,+,0)$$. The kernel $$\ker f$$ of a ring homomorphism $$\phi$$ is defined as the kernel of this group homomorphism. That is, $$\ker \phi=\phi^{-1}(0)$$. Then by [§Homomorphisms, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/algebraic_structures/group_homomorphisms#prop4), $$\phi$$ is injective if and only if $$\ker \phi=\{0\}$$.

## Free Ring on an Abelian Group

By definition, a ring is an abelian group, and a ring homomorphism is also a homomorphism of abelian groups. Thus there is a forgetful functor $$U:\Ring \rightarrow \Ab$$, which simply forgets the multiplicative structure. This functor is a right adjoint, and its left adjoint $$F: \Ab \rightarrow \Ring$$ is given by the graded abelian group

$$F(G)=\bigoplus_{n\geq 0} G^{\otimes n}$$

Here, an element $$\alpha_n$$ of $$G^{\otimes n}$$ can be written in the form

$$\alpha_n=\sum_{i\in I} \alpha^i_{n1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \alpha^i_{nn},\qquad \text{$\alpha_{nj}^i\in A$, $I$ finite}$$

and elements of $$F(G)$$ are of the form

$$(\alpha_0,\alpha_1,\ldots )=(\alpha_0,0,\ldots)+(0,\alpha_1,\ldots)+\cdots,\qquad \text{$\alpha_n=0$ for all but finitely many $n$}$$

But since each $$\alpha_n$$ can be identified by the number of tensor factors in its components $$\alpha^i_{n1}\otimes\cdots \alpha^i_{nn}$$, that is, by which $$G^{\otimes n}$$ it lies in, we may abuse notation and write $$(0,\ldots, 0, \alpha_n,0,\ldots)$$ as $$\alpha_n$$. Then any element of $$F(G)$$ can be written in the form

$$\sum_{i\in I} \alpha_{i1}\otimes \cdots\otimes \alpha_{in_i}$$

Now we must define multiplication on $$F(G)$$. If multiplication is well defined, then by distributivity

$$\left(\sum_{i\in I} \alpha_{i1}\otimes \cdots\otimes \alpha_{in_i}\right)\left(\sum_{j\in J} \beta_{j1}\otimes \cdots\otimes \beta_{jn_j}\right)=\sum_{(i,j)\in I\times J}(\alpha_{i1}\otimes\cdots \otimes \alpha_{in_i})(\beta_{j1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \beta_{jn_j})$$

must hold. Conversely, if we define only the products of $$\alpha_{i1}\otimes\cdots \otimes \alpha_{in_i}$$ and $$\beta_{j1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \beta_{jn_j}$$, then the product of all elements of $$F(G)$$ is defined via the above equation. And we define

$$(\alpha_{i1}\otimes\cdots \otimes \alpha_{in_i})(\beta_{j1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \beta_{jn_j})=\alpha_{i1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \alpha_{in_i}\otimes \beta_{j1}\otimes\cdots \beta_{jn_j}$$

By the coherence theorem, we see that this defines a ring structure on $$F(G)$$, with additive identity $$0=(0,0,\ldots)$$ and multiplicative identity $$1=(1,0,\ldots)$$. The functoriality of $$G\mapsto F(G)$$ can be proved without difficulty, and moreover, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop4">**Proposition 4**</ins> For the $$F$$ defined above and the forgetful functor $$U:\Ring \rightarrow \Ab$$, we have $$F\dashv U$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let an arbitrary ring $$A$$ and an abelian group $$G$$ be given. Then we must prove

$$\Hom_\Ring(F(G), A)\cong \Hom_\Ab(G, U(A))$$

For any ring homomorphism $$\phi: F(G) \rightarrow A$$, composing with the inclusion $$i:G\hookrightarrow F(G)$$ yields an abelian group homomorphism $$\phi\circ i:G \rightarrow U(A)$$.

Conversely, given an abelian group homomorphism $$f:G \rightarrow U(A)$$, the formula

$$\sum_{i\in I} \alpha_{i1}\otimes \cdots\otimes \alpha_{in_i}\mapsto \sum_{i\in I} f(\alpha_{i1})\otimes \cdots\otimes f(\alpha_{in_i})$$

defines a ring homomorphism $$\phi:F(G) \rightarrow A$$.

We can verify that the two functions $$\Hom_\Ring(F(G), A) \rightarrow\Hom_\Ab(G, U(A))$$ and $$\Hom_\Ab(G, U(A))\rightarrow \Hom_\Ring(F(G), A)$$ defined in this way are inverses of each other, and that this bijection is natural.

</details>

## Subrings and Ideals

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def5">**Definition 5**</ins> A subset $$S$$ of a ring $$(A,+,-,\cdot,0,1)$$ is a *subring* if $$(S,+,-,\cdot,0,1)$$ carries a ring structure.

</div>

On the other hand, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop6">**Proposition 6**</ins> For any ring homomorphism $$\phi:A \rightarrow B$$, $$\ker \phi$$ is a subring of $$A$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

We have already verified that $$\ker \phi$$ is a subgroup of the abelian group $$(A,+,0)$$, so it suffices to show that $$\ker \phi$$ is closed under multiplication. But for any $$\alpha,\beta\in\ker \phi$$,

$$\phi(\alpha\beta)=\phi(\alpha)\phi(\beta)=0\cdot 0=0$$

so $$\alpha\beta\in\ker \phi$$.

</details>

Examining the above proof carefully, we see that even if only *one* of the two elements $$\alpha,\beta$$ belongs to $$\ker \phi$$, $$\alpha\beta$$ still belongs to $$\ker \phi$$. We define this as follows.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def7">**Definition 7**</ins> Let a ring $$A$$ be given. Then a subset $$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq A$$ is a *left ideal* (resp. *right ideal*) if $$\mathfrak{a}$$ is a subgroup of $$(A,+,0)$$ and for any $$x\in\mathfrak{a}$$ and $$\alpha\in A$$, we have $$\alpha x\in\mathfrak{a}$$ (resp. $$x\alpha\in\mathfrak{a}$$).

If $$\mathfrak{a}$$ is both a left ideal and a right ideal, it is called a *two-sided ideal*.

</div>

For convenience, we will only prove statements about left ideals (or two-sided ideals) from now on; however, every statement about left ideals also holds for right ideals with appropriate modifications. Since most rings we will actually use are commutative, the distinction between left ideals, right ideals, and two-sided ideals is unnecessary.

It is easy to prove that the intersection of left ideals is a left ideal. On the other hand, for any element $$x$$ of $$A$$, the set

$$Ax=\{\alpha x\mid\alpha\in A\}$$

is a subgroup under addition, and moreover, for any $$\beta\in A$$ and $$\alpha x\in Ax$$, we have $$\beta(\alpha x)=(\beta\alpha)x\in Ax$$, so $$Ax$$ is a left ideal of $$A$$. These can be shown to coincide with the smallest left ideal containing $$x$$, that is, the intersection of all left ideals containing $$x$$, and the same argument applies to right ideals and two-sided ideals.

More generally, the sum $$\mathfrak{a}+\mathfrak{b}$$ of left ideals of $$A$$ is defined as the set

$$\mathfrak{a}+\mathfrak{b}=\{x+y\mid x\in \mathfrak{a},y\in \mathfrak{b}\}$$

and $$\mathfrak{a}+\mathfrak{b}$$ is again a left ideal; in fact, it is the smallest left ideal containing both $$\mathfrak{a}$$ and $$\mathfrak{b}$$. Then for any elements $$x_1,\ldots, x_n$$ of $$A$$, the ideal

$$Ax_1+\cdots+Ax_n$$

is the smallest of all left ideals containing $$x_1,\ldots, x_n$$. Similarly, we can define

$$x_1A+\cdots+x_nA,\qquad Ax_1A+\cdots +Ax_nA$$

and these are the smallest right ideal and two-sided ideal containing $$x_1,\ldots, x_n$$, respectively. If $$A$$ is commutative, the notions of left ideal, right ideal, and two-sided ideal all coincide, so we also write these collectively as $$(x_1,\ldots, x_n)$$.

On the other hand, for any left ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$, we have $$1\in\mathfrak{a}$$ if and only if $$\mathfrak{a}=A$$. Thus, for $$\mathfrak{a}\subsetneq A$$ we must have $$1\not\in\mathfrak{a}$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def8">**Definition 8**</ins> For a ring $$A$$ and an ideal $$\mathfrak{m}$$, if there is no ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ satisfying $$\mathfrak{m}\subsetneq\mathfrak{a}\subsetneq A$$, then $$\mathfrak{m}$$ is called a *maximal ideal*.

</div>

Let any ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of $$A$$ be given. Then we may consider the collection of ideals of $$A$$ containing $$\mathfrak{a}$$ but distinct from $$A$$ itself. This collection is an inductive set, so it has a maximal element. ([\[Set Theory\] §Axiom of Choice, ⁋Theorem 4](/en/math/set_theory/axiom_of_choice#thm4)) It is easy to see that this maximal element is a maximal ideal. Thus we obtain the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm9">**Theorem 9 (Krull)**</ins> For an ideal $$\mathfrak{a}\subsetneq A$$ of a ring $$A$$, there always exists a maximal ideal $$\mathfrak{m}$$ of $$A$$ containing $$\mathfrak{a}$$.

</div>
