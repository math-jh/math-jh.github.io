We now define $$A$$-modules over an arbitrary ring $$A$$ and examine their properties. More advanced topics on $$A$$-modules can be found in the [Multilinear Algebra](/en/math/multilinear_algebra) category.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> Fix a monoid object $$(A,\cdot, 1)$$ in the symmetric monoidal category $$(\Ab,\otimes, \mathbb{Z})$$. An object $$M\in\Ab$$ equipped with a left $$A$$-action is called a *left $$A$$-module*, and one equipped with a right $$A$$-action is called a *right $$A$$-module*.

</div>

Since a monoid object in the monoidal category $$(\Ab,\otimes, \mathbb{Z})$$ is precisely a ring $$A$$, the above definition translates as follows: $$M$$ is a *left $$A$$-module* if it is an abelian group under addition and is equipped with an action $$\cdot:A\times M \rightarrow M$$ of $$A$$ satisfying the following conditions:

1. For all $$\alpha\in A$$ and $$x,y\in M$$, $$\alpha\cdot(x+y)=\alpha\cdot x+\alpha\cdot y$$.
2. For all $$\alpha,\beta\in A$$ and $$x\in M$$, $$(\alpha+\beta)\cdot x=\alpha\cdot x+\beta\cdot x$$.
3. For all $$\alpha,\beta\in A$$ and $$x\in M$$, $$(\alpha\beta)\cdot x=\alpha\cdot(\beta\cdot x)$$.
4. For all $$x\in M$$, $$1\cdot x=x$$.

Here, rather than viewing $$\cdot$$ as a homomorphism from $$A\otimes M$$ to $$M$$, we have explicitly written out the bilinearity condition in conditions 1 and 2.


<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def2">**Definition 2**</ins> For an $$A$$-module $$M$$ and a family $$(x_i)_{i\in I}$$ of its elements, a *linear combination* of this family is an element of the form

$$\sum_{i\in I} \alpha_i x_i,\qquad\text{$(\alpha_i)$ finitely supported}$$

If there exists a family $$(x_i)_{i\in I}$$ of elements of $$M$$ such that every element of $$M$$ can be expressed as a linear combination of elements from this family, we say that $$(x_i)_{i\in I}$$ *generates* $$M$$. If this family can be chosen to be finite, then $$M$$ is called a *finitely generated $$A$$-module*.

</div>

## Submodules and Quotient Modules

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> For any $$A$$-module $$M$$ and its subset $$N$$, if $$N$$ itself carries an $$A$$-module structure, it is called a *submodule*.

</div>

Now, for an $$A$$-module $$M$$ and any submodule $$N$$ of $$M$$, the quotient group $$M/N$$ is well-defined. Moreover, since $$N$$ is closed under the $$A$$-action, we may equip $$M/N$$ with an $$A$$-module structure without difficulty.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def4">**Definition 4**</ins> For any $$A$$-module $$M$$ and its submodule $$N$$, the quotient $$M/N$$ is called the *quotient module*.

</div>

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex5">**Example 5**</ins> The multiplication map $$\mu:A\otimes A \rightarrow A$$ of a ring $$A$$ satisfies precisely all the properties required of an action. Hence any ring is always a module over itself. If we view $$A$$ as a left $$A$$-module, its submodules are exactly the left ideals of $$A$$; similarly, if we view $$A$$ as a right $$A$$-module, its submodules are exactly the right ideals of $$A$$.

On the other hand, reconsidering why only two-sided ideals were considered in defining quotient rings, we see that for a left ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$, the quotient module $$A/\mathfrak{a}$$ remains a left $$A$$-module (even though it does not carry a ring structure), and the same holds for right ideals.

</div>

## Linear Maps

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def6">**Definition 6**</ins> For two $$A$$-modules $$M$$ and $$N$$, an *$$A$$-linear map* from $$M$$ to $$N$$ is a function $$u$$ satisfying the two conditions

$$u(x+y)=u(x)+u(y),\qquad u(\alpha x)=\alpha u(x)$$

for all $$x,y\in M$$ and $$\alpha\in A$$. When no confusion can arise, we simply call it a linear map.

</div>

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop7">**Proposition 7**</ins> The composition of linear maps is a linear map. Moreover, a bijective linear map is always an isomorphism.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Clear.

</details>

We denote the category of left $$A$$-modules and $$A$$-linear maps by $$\lMod{A}$$, and the category of right $$A$$-modules and $$A$$-linear maps by $$\rMod{A}$$. We also write $$\Hom_\lMod{A}(M,N)$$ simply as $$\Hom_A(M,N)$$. These categories have full subcategories $$\lmod{A}$$ and $$\rmod{A}$$ consisting of finitely generated $$A$$-modules. The zero object of these four categories is $$\{0\}$$.

One special property of $$\lMod{A}$$ is that $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M,N)$$ carries an abelian group structure. Moreover, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8**</ins> For any $$M$$ and $$N$$, $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M,N)$$ is an abelian group. Moreover, for any $$A$$-linear map $$u:M \rightarrow M'$$, the map

$$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(u, N):\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M',N)\rightarrow \Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M,N)$$

is a homomorphism of abelian groups.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The sum $$v+w\in\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M,N)$$ of any two elements $$v,w\in\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M,N)$$ is defined by

$$(v+w)(x)=v(x)+w(x)\qquad\text{for all $x\in M$}$$

We need to verify that this is indeed an $$A$$-linear map, but this is clear.

That $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(u,N)$$ is a homomorphism of abelian groups follows from the identity

$$\left(\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(u, N)(v+w)\right)(x)=(v+w)(u(x))=v(u(x))+w(u(x))=\left(\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(u,N)(v)\right)(x)+\left(\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(u,N)(w)\right)(x)$$

</details>

A similar result holds for $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M, f)$$, and also for right $$A$$-modules. If $$A$$ is commutative, then for any $$u:M \rightarrow N$$, the operation $$\cdot$$ defined by

$$(\alpha\cdot u)(x):=\alpha\cdot u(x)\qquad\text{for all $x\in M$}$$

endows $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M,N)$$ with an $$A$$-module structure. If $$A$$ is not commutative, however, then for any $$\beta\in A$$ we have

$$(\alpha\cdot u)(\beta x)=\alpha\cdot u(\beta x)=\alpha\beta u(x)$$

and there is no natural way to rewrite this as $$\beta(\alpha u(x))=\beta\cdot(\alpha\cdot u)(x)$$; consequently, $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(M,N)$$ does not generally carry an $$A$$-module structure. On the other hand, if $$A$$ is commutative we can further show that the abelian group homomorphism from [Proposition 8](#prop8) is an $$A$$-linear map.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def9">**Definition 9**</ins> For an $$A$$-linear map $$u:M \rightarrow N$$, we define the kernel and image of $$u$$ respectively by

$$\ker u=\{x\in M\mid u(x)=0\},\qquad \im u=\{u(x)\in N\mid x\in M\}$$

</div>

The following is the isomorphism theorem we have used repeatedly; since its proof is the same as before, we omit it.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm10">**Theorem 10**</ins> Let $$u:M \rightarrow N$$ be an $$A$$-linear map.
