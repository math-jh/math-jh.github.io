We have not devoted much attention to the category $$\Ab$$ until now; in this article, we examine abelian groups.

## Sums of Abelian Groups

First, the universal property of the weak direct product established in [§Restricted Sums, ⁋Theorem 2](/en/math/algebraic_structures/restricted_sums#thm2) applies especially when the group $$H$$ is abelian.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm1">**Theorem 1**</ins> Let $$(G_i)$$ be a family of abelian groups, and consider $$\prod^w G_i$$ and the inclusion maps $$\iota_i$$. Then for any abelian group $$H$$ and group homomorphisms $$f_i:G_i\rightarrow H$$, there exists a unique group homomorphism $$f:\prod^wG_i\rightarrow H$$ satisfying $$f_i=f\circ\iota_i$$.

</div>

Thus, at least among abelian groups, the weak direct product $$\prod^w G_i$$ is a coproduct. We adopt the following terminology.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def2">**Definition 2**</ins> Let a family $$(G_i)$$ of abelian groups, their weak direct product $$\prod^w G_i$$, and the inclusion maps $$\iota_i$$ be given. We call $$\prod^w G_i$$ together with the $$\iota_i$$ the *direct sum* of the $$G_i$$, and denote it by $$\bigoplus G_i$$.  

</div>

By a slight abuse of notation, identifying $$\iota_i(G_i)$$ with $$G_i$$, any element of $$\bigoplus G_i$$ can be written as

$$x=\sum_{i\in I} x_i,\qquad\text{$x_i\in G_i$, $x_i=0$ for all but finitely many $i$}$$

In this situation, the subset of $$I$$ consisting of indices with $$x_i\neq 0$$ is called the *support* of $$x$$ and denoted by $$\supp(x)$$; a family $$(x_i)$$ satisfying the above condition is called *finitely supported*.

## Abelianization

Now we make the following definition.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> For any group $$G$$ and any two subgroups $$H_1,H_2$$ of $$G$$, define $$[H_1,H_2]$$ as the subgroup of $$G$$ generated by the *commutators*

$$[h_1,h_2]=h_1^{-1}h_2^{-1}h_1h_2,\qquad h_1\in H_1,h_2\in H_2$$

</div>

We are particularly interested in the case $$H_1=H_2=G$$. If $$G$$ is abelian, then for all $$x,y\in G$$ we have $$x^{-1}y^{-1}xy=e$$, so $$[G,G]=\{e\}$$. Therefore, $$[G,G]$$ can be thought of as measuring how far $$G$$ is from being abelian.

The following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop4">**Proposition 4**</ins> For any group $$G$$, the commutator subgroup $$[G,G]$$ is a normal subgroup of $$G$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For any $$x,y\in G$$ and $$g\in G$$,

$$g(x^{-1}y^{-1}xy)g^{-1}=(gx^{-1}g^{-1})(gy^{-1}g^{-1})(gxg^{-1})(gyg^{-1})=(gxg^{-1})^{-1}(gyg^{-1})^{-1}(gxg^{-1})(gyg^{-1})\in [G,G]$$

which is clear. 

</details>

Thus, $$G/[G,G]$$ is well-defined. This amounts to treating all elements of the form $$x^{-1}y^{-1}xy$$ as $$e$$, so $$G/[G,G]$$ becomes an abelian group. By our convention, the operation of an abelian group should be written as $$+$$, but since $$G/[G,G]$$ inherits its operation from $$G$$, doing so might cause confusion. Therefore, we write the operation in $$G/[G,G]$$ as multiplication rather than $$+$$.

Now, for any abelian group $$H$$ and any group homomorphism $$f:G\rightarrow H$$, the equality

$$e=f(x)^{-1}f(y)^{-1}f(x)f(y)=f(x^{-1}y^{-1}xy)$$

holds for all $$x,y\in G$$, and thus $$[G,G]\leq\ker f$$. By [§Group Isomorphisms, ⁋Proposition 3](/en/math/algebraic_structures/isomorphism_theorems#prop3), we obtain the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop5">**Proposition 5**</ins> Let $$G$$ be any group, and let $$p:G\rightarrow G/[G,G]$$ be the quotient homomorphism. Then for any abelian group $$H$$ and any group homomorphism $$f:G \rightarrow H$$, there exists a group homomorphism $$\bar{f}:G/[G,G]\rightarrow H$$ satisfying $$f=\bar{f}\circ p$$.

</div>

In particular, given any group homomorphism $$f:G\rightarrow H$$, the composition $$G\rightarrow H\rightarrow H/[H,H]$$ yields a group homomorphism from $$G$$ to the abelian group $$H/[H,H]$$, and by [Proposition 5](#prop5), this induces a group homomorphism from $$G/[G,G]$$ to $$H/[H,H]$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def6">**Definition 6**</ins> For any group $$G$$, the quotient group $$G/[G,G]$$ is called the *abelianization* of $$G$$ and denoted by $$G^\ab$$.

</div>

The preceding argument shows that this defines a functor $$\ab:\Grp\rightarrow\Ab$$. Moreover, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop7">**Proposition 7**</ins> For the forgetful functor $$U:\Ab \rightarrow \Grp$$ and the abelianization functor $$\ab:\Grp \rightarrow \Ab$$, there exists an adjunction $$\ab\dashv U$$.

</div>

The proof was already completed above.

## Free Abelian Groups

At the end of the previous article, we could interpret the free group $$F(X)$$ as the free product

$${\prod_{x\in X}}^\ast \mathbb{Z}$$

Since we already know that $$\Ab$$ has the coproduct $$\bigoplus$$, by the same argument we obtain the left adjoint $$F_\Ab:\Set\rightarrow \Ab$$ to the forgetful functor $$U:\Ab \rightarrow \Set$$ via the formula

$$F_\Ab(X)=F_\Ab\left(\coprod_{x\in X} \{x\}\right)\cong \coprod_{x\in X} F_\Ab(\ast)=\bigoplus_{x\in X} \mathbb{Z}$$

We define the resulting $$F_\Ab(X)$$ as the *free abelian group*. The following proposition then holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8**</ins> The forgetful functor $$U:\Ab \rightarrow \Set$$ has a left adjoint $$F_\Ab:\Set \rightarrow\Ab$$.

</div>

## The Abelian Group $$\Hom_\Ab(G,H)$$

For any abelian groups $$G,H$$, the set $$\Hom_\Ab(G,H)$$ is the set of group homomorphisms from $$G$$ to $$H$$. This set has an interesting property: $$\Hom_\Ab(G,H)$$ is itself an abelian group. This does not hold in $$\Grp$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop9">**Proposition 9**</ins> For any abelian groups $$G,H$$, the set $$\Hom_\Ab(G,H)$$ is an abelian group.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For any $$f,g:G \rightarrow H$$, we define $$f+g$$ by

$$(f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\qquad\text{for all $x\in G$}$$

</details>

$$\Hom_\Ab(-,-)$$ was originally defined as a bifunctor from $$\Ab^\op\times \Ab$$ to $$\Set$$, but by this proposition we can actually regard it as a bifunctor to $$\Ab$$. That is, we can think of $$\Hom_\Ab(-,-)$$ as something resembling an internal $$\Hom$$. However, with the language we have so far, this is impossible.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex10">**Example 10**</ins> $$\Ab$$ is a cartesian monoidal category with respect to $$\times$$. However, $$\Hom_\Ab(-,-)$$ cannot be regarded as an internal $$\Hom$$ with respect to this structure. That is,

$$\Hom_\Ab(G\times H, A)\cong \Hom_\Ab(G,\Hom_\Ab(H,A))$$

does not hold in general. For example, setting $$G=\mathbb{Z}$$ turns the above equation into

$$\Hom_\Ab(\mathbb{Z}\times H,A)\cong \Hom_\Ab(\mathbb{Z},\Hom_\Ab(H,A))\cong \Hom_\Ab(H,A)\tag{1}$$

which is false in most cases. (For instance, take $$H=\{e\}$$.)

</div>

Thus, to regard $$\Hom_\Ab(-,-)$$ as an internal $$\Hom$$, we must endow $$\Ab$$ with a new symmetric monoidal category structure. Moreover, equation (1) above suggests that $$\mathbb{Z}$$ should behave as the unit of this monoidal product.

## Tensor Products

The fundamental reason why the equation in [Example 10](#ex10) cannot hold is quite simple. In $$\Set$$, the above isomorphism holds because for any function $$f:A\times B \rightarrow C$$, fixing an element of $$A$$ or $$B$$ yields a function from $$B$$ or $$A$$ to $$C$$.

On the other hand, for a group homomorphism $$f:G\times H \rightarrow A$$, the only such map for which fixing the first or second component yields a group homomorphism is the zero map. Indeed, for any $$x\in G$$, if $$f(x, -)$$ is a group homomorphism then $$f(x,0)=0$$, and similarly $$f(0,y)=0$$ for any $$y\in H$$; substituting these into the condition that $$f$$ is a group homomorphism,

$$f(x+0,0+y)=f(x,0)+f(0,y)$$

we obtain $$f(x,y)=0$$ for all $$(x,y)\in G\times H$$.

Looking at the above argument, it seems quite natural to require that fixing one component of the source of $$f$$ yield a group homomorphism.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def11">**Definition 11**</ins> For two abelian groups $$G,H$$, a function $$f:G\times H \rightarrow A$$ is called *bilinear* if the two equations

$$f(x,y_1+y_2)=f(x,y_1)+f(x,y_2),\qquad f(x_1+x_2,y)=f(x_1,y)+f(x_2,y)$$

always hold.

</div>

Now, for fixed $$G,H\in\obj(\Ab)$$, define the set $$\Bilin(G,H;A)$$ by

$$\Bilin(G,H;A)=\{\text{bilinear maps from $G\times H$ to $A$}\}$$

By the above argument, replacing the left-hand side of the first equation in [Example 10](#ex10) with $$\Bilin(G,H;A)$$ yields an isomorphism

$$\Bilin(G,H;A)\cong \Hom_\Ab(G,\Hom_\Ab(H,A))$$

Moreover, we can verify that $$\Bilin(G,H;-)$$ is a representable functor from $$\Ab$$ to $$\Set$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm12">**Theorem 12**</ins> $$\Bilin(G,H;-)$$ is representable.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Define the subgroup $$S$$ of the free abelian group $$F_\Ab(G\times H)$$ by

$$S=\left\langle (x, y_1+y_2)-(x,y_1)-(x,y_2), (x_1+x_2,y)-(x_1,y)-(x_2,y)\mathop{\big\vert}x,x_1,x_2\in G, y,y_1,y_2\in H\right\rangle$$

Then by the universal property of the free abelian group, any function $$f:G\times H \rightarrow A$$ induces a group homomorphism $$\hat{f}:F_\Ab(G\times H)\rightarrow A$$, and if $$f$$ is bilinear, the kernel of $$\hat{f}$$ contains $$S$$; hence $$\hat{f}$$ defines a group homomorphism from $$F_\Ab(G\times H)/S$$ to $$A$$. 

The naturality of the isomorphism $$\Bilin(G,H;A)\cong\Hom_\Ab(F_\Ab(G\times H)/S,A)$$ still requires verification, but it is a straightforward computation, which we omit.

</details>

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def13">**Definition 13**</ins> The representation in [Theorem 12](#thm12) is called the *tensor product* of $$G$$ and $$H$$, and is denoted by $$A\otimes B$$.

</div>

Elements of $$A\otimes B$$ can be expressed as finite sums of elements of the form $$a\otimes b$$. We can then verify that $$\otimes$$ is a monoidal product with $$\mathbb{Z}$$ as the tensor unit.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm14">**Theorem 14**</ins> $$(\Ab,\otimes, \mathbb{Z})$$ is a symmetric monoidal category.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The associator $$\alpha$$ and symmetor $$\sigma$$ are obtained from the universal property of $$\otimes$$. That $$\mathbb{Z}$$ is the tensor unit follows from the fact that the isomorphism

$$\Hom_\Ab(\mathbb{Z}\otimes G, H)\cong\Bilin(\mathbb{Z},G;H)\cong\Hom_\Ab(\mathbb{Z},\Hom_\Ab(G,H))\cong\Hom_\Ab(G,H)$$

is natural.

</details>

In particular, for any $$f:A \rightarrow A'$$ and $$g:B \rightarrow B'$$, since $$\otimes$$ is a bifunctor there is a morphism $$f\otimes g:A\otimes B \rightarrow A'\otimes B'$$. This is the group homomorphism determined by sending elements of the form $$a\otimes b$$ to $$f(a)\otimes g(b)$$. Occasionally one considers the $$n$$-fold tensor product of an abelian group $$A$$, which we denote by

$$A^{\otimes n}=\underbrace{A\otimes\cdots\otimes A}_\text{$n$ times}$$

Then by the associativity of $$\otimes$$,

$$A^{\otimes m}\otimes A^{\otimes n}\cong A^{\otimes(m+n)}$$

holds. From this perspective, it is conventional to define $$A^{\otimes 0}$$ as $$\mathbb{Z}$$.

Thus, regarding $$(\Ab,\otimes, \mathbb{Z})$$ as a symmetric monoidal category, we have already verified the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm15">**Theorem 15 ($$\otimes\dashv\Hom$$)**</ins> There exists an adjunction

$$\Hom_\Ab(G\otimes H, A)\cong\Hom_\Ab(G,\Hom_\Ab(H, A))\cong\Hom_\Ab(H,\Hom_\Ab(G, A))$$

Therefore, $$\Hom_\Ab(-,-)$$ can be regarded as the internal $$\Hom$$ of $$(\Ab,\otimes,\mathbb{Z})$$.

</div>

## Graded Abelian Groups

For a family $$(G_i)$$ of abelian groups, the direct sum $$\bigoplus G_i$$ is well-defined. The following definition is particularly useful in other algebraic structures.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def16">**Definition 16**</ins> For a commutative monoid $$I$$, consider a family $$(G_i)_{i\in I}$$ of abelian groups indexed by $$I$$. This is called a *graded abelian group*.

</div>

At present, for a commutative monoid $$I$$, there is no difference between regarding $$I$$ as a set and taking the direct sum versus the graded abelian group defined above; thus this definition merely gives a new name to an existing concept. The reason for introducing it is to later specify the relationship between a new operation to be defined on abelian groups and the addition in $$I$$.
