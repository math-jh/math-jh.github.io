We now define the notion of an $$A$$-algebra. An algebra is a structure obtained by adding to an $$A$$-module $$M$$ a multiplication compatible with the addition and scalar multiplication of $$M$$; this is the same process as defining a multiplication on an abelian group $$G$$ compatible with its addition. However, using [§Rings, ⁋Definition 1](/en/math/algebraic_structures/rings#def1) verbatim presents two problems.

First, for a general ring $$A$$, neither $$\lMod{A}$$ nor $$\rMod{A}$$ is a monoidal category. This is resolved by assuming that $$A$$ is a commutative ring, for then $$(\lMod{A},\otimes_A, A)$$ becomes a symmetric monoidal category. ([§Direct Products, Direct Sums, and Tensor Products of Modules, §§Tensor Products of Modules over Commutative Rings](/en/math/algebraic_structures/operations_of_rings#가환환-위에서-정의된-가군의-텐서곱)) 

The second problem is historical: if we fix a commutative ring $$A$$ as usual and call a monoid object $$E$$ in the symmetric monoidal category $$(\lMod{A},\otimes_A,A)$$ an $$A$$-algebra, then the multiplication of $$E$$ must always satisfy associativity and possess an identity element, by the associativity and unit axioms of a monoid object. Yet many important examples studied to date often fail to satisfy these properties. To encompass all of them, we must abandon associativity and the existence of an identity element, which in turn means partially abandoning a definition like that of [§Rings, ⁋Definition 1](/en/math/algebraic_structures/rings#def1).

For these reasons, we henceforth always assume that $$A$$ is a commutative ring, and define an $$A$$-algebra as follows.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins>  If an $$A$$-bilinear map $$\mu:E\times E \rightarrow E$$ is given on an $$A$$-module $$E$$, then we call $$E$$ an *$$A$$-algebra*. Moreover, according to the properties satisfied by the multiplication $$\mu$$ of $$E$$, we make the following definitions.

- If the multiplication of $$E$$ satisfies commutativity, we call it a *commutative $$A$$-algebra*.
- If the multiplication of $$E$$ satisfies associativity, we call it an *associative $$A$$-algebra*.
- If the multiplication of $$E$$ has an identity element, we call it a *unital $$A$$-algebra*.

</div>

Thus a monoid object in $$(\lMod{A},\otimes_A,A)$$ is an associative unital $$A$$-algebra.

On the other hand, an associative unital $$A$$-algebra can be viewed as a multiplication defined on an $$A$$-module $$E$$, but also as a scalar multiplication defined on a ring $$E$$. Fix an arbitrary ring homomorphism $$\rho:A \rightarrow E$$, and assume that $$\rho(A)$$ is contained in the center $$Z(E)$$ of $$E$$. Then, viewing $$E$$ as a module over itself, we may consider the $$A$$-module $$\rho^\ast E$$ defined via restriction of scalars

$$\rho^\ast:\lMod{E} \rightarrow \lMod{A}$$

Then the multiplication of $$E$$ defines a map $$\mu:\rho^\ast E\times \rho^\ast E \rightarrow \rho^\ast E$$ on $$\rho^\ast E$$, and we have

$$\mu(\alpha\cdot x, y)=(\rho(\alpha)x)y=\rho(\alpha)xy$$

and since $$\rho(\alpha)\in Z(E)$$, we verify the identity

$$\alpha\cdot \mu(x,y)=\mu(\alpha\cdot x,y)=\mu(x,\alpha\cdot y)$$

In this case, the ring homomorphism $$\rho:A \rightarrow E$$ that gives $$E$$ an $$A$$-algebra structure is called the *structure morphism*.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def2">**Definition 2**</ins> Let $$E$$ and $$E'$$ be two $$A$$-algebras. An $$A$$-module homomorphism $$u: E \rightarrow E'$$ is called an *$$A$$-algebra homomorphism* if the identity

$$u(xy)=u(x)u(y)$$

holds for all $$x,y\in E$$.

</div>

The following proposition is straightforward.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop3">**Proposition 3**</ins> The composition of $$A$$-algebra homomorphisms is an $$A$$-algebra homomorphism. A bijective $$A$$-algebra homomorphism is always an isomorphism.

</div>

These data define the category $$\Alg{A}$$ of $$A$$-algebras. We also define the category $$\cAlg{A}$$ of commutative $$A$$-algebras.

## Examples of Algebras

Just as free rings are defined over abelian groups, free algebras are defined over any $$A$$-module. This process is exactly the same as the one constructed in [§Rings, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/algebraic_structures/rings#prop4); indeed, this is natural, for all that remains to be shown is that the multiplication defined here is compatible with the scalar multiplication structure of $$A$$, and this is already guaranteed because the requirement that $$A$$ be commutative ensures that the scalar multiplication structure is already defined on $$F(M)=\bigoplus_{n\geq 0}M^{\otimes n}$$.

In other words, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop4">**Proposition 4**</ins> For any $$A$$-module $$M$$, if we define

$$F(M)=\bigoplus_{n\geq 0} M^{\otimes n}$$

then $$F$$ is the left adjoint of the forgetful functor $$\Alg{A} \rightarrow \lMod{A}$$.

</div>

The $$F(M)$$ obtained from the above proposition retains the addition already defined on $$M$$ and acquires a new operation, multiplication. On the other hand, one can also construct an $$A$$-algebra from the multiplication defined on a suitable group $$G$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def5">**Definition 5**</ins> Fix a ring $$A$$ (not necessarily commutative) and an arbitrary group $$G$$. Then the *group ring* $$AG$$ is, as a set, the collection of finitely supported functions from $$G$$ to $$A$$; for two functions $$\alpha:G \rightarrow A$$ and $$\beta: G \rightarrow A$$, their operations are given by

$$\alpha+\beta: x\mapsto \alpha(x)+\beta(x),\qquad \alpha\beta:x\mapsto \sum_{uv=x}\alpha(u)\beta(v)$$

If $$A$$ is a (commutative) ring, then $$AG$$ also carries an $$A$$-module structure, hence becomes an $$A$$-algebra. In this case, $$AG$$ is called the *group algebra*.

</div>

For each $$x\in G$$, define $$\delta_x: G \rightarrow A$$ by the formula

$$\delta_x(y)=\begin{cases}1&\text{if $x=y$}\\0&\text{if $x\neq y$}\end{cases}$$

Then any element of $$AG$$ can be written as

$$\sum_{x\in G} \alpha_x\delta_x,\qquad\text{$\alpha_x=0$ for all but finitely many $x$}$$

for a family $$\alpha_x\in A$$ indexed by the elements of $$G$$. For convenience, writing the function $$\delta_x$$ as $$x$$, the multiplication in $$AG$$ can be written as

$$\left(\sum_{x\in G} \alpha_xx\right)\left(\sum_{y\in G} \beta_yy\right)=\sum_{x,y\in G}\alpha_x\beta_yxy=\sum_{z\in G}\left(\sum_{x\in G}\alpha_x\beta_{x^{-1}z}\right)z$$

Then the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop6">**Proposition 6**</ins> Consider the two functors

$$A-: \Grp \rightarrow \Alg{A},\qquad (-)^\times:\Alg{A} \rightarrow \Grp$$

Here $$(-)^\times$$ is the functor sending an arbitrary $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ to its group of units $$E^\times$$. Then $$A{-}\dashv (-)^\times$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

That is, for any group $$G$$ and any $$A$$-algebra $$E$$, we must establish the isomorphism

$$\Hom_{\Alg{A}}(AG, E)\cong \Hom_\Grp(G, E^\times)$$

First, suppose a group homomorphism $$f:G \rightarrow E^\times$$ is given. Then we can define $$\tilde{f}:AG \rightarrow E$$ by the formula

$$\tilde{f}:AG \rightarrow E;\quad \sum_{x\in G} \alpha_x\delta_x\mapsto \sum_{x\in G} \alpha_xf(x)$$

It is then obvious that $$\tilde{f}$$ preserves addition and scalar multiplication, and in the case of multiplication as well,

$$\begin{aligned}\tilde{f}\left(\left(\sum_{x\in G} a_xx\right)\left(\sum_{y\in G} b_yy\right)\right)&=\tilde{f}\left(\sum_{x,y\in G}a_xb_yxy\right)=\tilde{f}\left(\sum_{z\in G}\left(\sum_{x\in G}a_xb_{x^{-1}z}\right)\right)\\&=\sum_{z\in G}\left(\sum_{x\in G}a_xb_{x^{-1}z}\right)f(z)=\sum_{x,y\in G} a_xb_yf(xy)\\&=\left(\left(\sum_{x\in G} a_xf(x)\right)\left(\sum_{y\in G} b_yf(y)\right)\right)=\tilde{f}\left(\sum_{x\in G} a_xx\right)\tilde{f}\left(\sum_{y\in G} b_yy\right)\end{aligned}$$

so the desired identity holds. Conversely, suppose an arbitrary $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$u: AG \rightarrow E$$ is given. Then the function $$\bar{u}: G \rightarrow E$$ defined by the formula

$$\bar{u}: G \rightarrow E;\qquad x\mapsto u(\delta_x)$$

preserves multiplication. This is obvious, since for arbitrary $$x,y\in G$$,

$$\bar{u}(xy)=u(\delta_{xy})=u(\delta_x\delta_y)=u(\delta_x)u(\delta_y)=\bar{u}(\delta_x)\bar{u}(\delta_y)$$

and therefore the image of $$\bar{u}$$ lies in $$E^\times$$. That these two processes are inverses of each other is obvious.

</details>

The last example is already somewhat familiar.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def7">**Definition 7**</ins> For a ring $$A$$, the *polynomial algebra* $$A[\x]$$ is, as a set,

$$A[\x]=\{p(\x)=a_n\x^n+\cdots+a_1\x+a_0\mid a_i\in A\}$$

with addition and multiplication given by the usual polynomial operations, and scalar multiplication by $$A$$ given likewise by multiplication by a constant polynomial. More generally, for any set $$S$$, $$A[S]$$ can be defined in a similar manner.

</div>

In the case of multivariate polynomials $$A[S]$$, for notational convenience one usually chooses an index set $$I$$ with $$\lvert S\rvert=\lvert I\rvert$$ and denotes the elements of $$S$$ by $$\x_i$$. Then for arbitrary

$$\alpha=(\alpha_i)_{i\in I}\in \mathbb{N}_{\geq0}^I\qquad\text{finitely supported}$$

we write

$$\x^\alpha=\prod_{i\in I}\x_i^{\alpha_i}$$

and elements of $$A[\x_i]_{i\in I}$$ can be written in the form

$$\sum_{d=0}^n \sum_{\lvert\alpha\rvert=d} a_\alpha\x^\alpha$$

where $$\lvert\alpha\rvert=\sum_{i\in I}\alpha_i$$. 

Likewise, the polynomial algebra satisfies the following universal property.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8**</ins> The functor $$A[-]:\Set \rightarrow \cAlg{A}$$ sending a set $$S$$ to the polynomial algebra $$A[\x_i]_{i\in I}$$ with the elements of $$S$$ as variables is the left adjoint of the forgetful functor $$U: \cAlg{A} \rightarrow \Set$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

That is, we must establish the isomorphism

$$\Hom_{\cAlg{A}}(A[\x_i]_{i\in I}, E)\cong\Hom_\Set(S, UE)$$

First, suppose an arbitrary function $$f:S \rightarrow U(E)$$ is given. Then the map $$\tilde{f}: A[\x_i]_{i\in I} \rightarrow E$$ defined by the formula

$$\tilde{f}: \sum_{d=0}^n \sum_{\lvert\alpha\rvert=d} a_\alpha\x^\alpha\mapsto \sum_{d=0}^n\sum_{\lvert\alpha\rvert=d}a_\alpha\prod_{i\in I}f(\x_i)^\alpha_i$$

is an $$A$$-algebra homomorphism from $$A[\x_i]_{i\in I}$$ to $$E$$. Conversely, given an arbitrary $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$u:A[\x_i]_{i\in I} \rightarrow E$$, we define $$\bar{u}:S \rightarrow UE$$ by $$\x_i\mapsto u(\x_i)$$. It is easy to verify that these are inverses of each other.

</details>

Thus the polynomial algebra can be regarded as a kind of free commutative $$A$$-algebra. Comparing [Definition 5](#def5) and [Definition 7](#def7) reveals a slight similarity: if we generalize [Definition 5](#def5) to the notion of a *monoid ring*, then the polynomial ring of [Definition 7](#def7) becomes the monoid ring of the monoid consisting of elements of the form $$\x^\alpha$$.

## Subalgebras, Ideals, and Quotient Algebras

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def9">**Definition 9**</ins> A submodule $$F$$ of an $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ is called a *subalgebra* of $$E$$ if $$F$$ is closed under the multiplication of $$E$$.

</div>

Meanwhile, we define the ideal of an $$A$$-algebra in the same way as in [§Rings, ⁋Definition 7](/en/math/algebraic_structures/rings#def7).

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def10">**Definition 10**</ins> A subalgebra $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of an $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ is called a *left ideal* of $$E$$ if $$\alpha x\in \mathfrak{a}$$ holds for all $$x\in \mathfrak{a}$$ and $$\alpha\in E$$. Similarly we define a *right ideal*. An ideal that is simultaneously a left ideal and a right ideal is called a *two-sided ideal*.

</div>

In other words, an ideal of an $$A$$-algebra is nothing special: it is simply the ideal of $$E$$ when we forget the $$A$$-action on $$E$$ and regard $$E$$ merely as a ring. For example, the kernel of an $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$u:E \rightarrow F$$,

$$\ker u=\{x\in E\mid u(x)=0\}$$

is a two-sided ideal of $$E$$. We may then define the following.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def11">**Definition 11**</ins> For an arbitrary $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ and a two-sided ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of $$E$$, we call $$E/\mathfrak{a}$$ the *quotient algebra* of $$E$$ by $$\mathfrak{a}$$.

</div>

Of course, one should prove that $$E/\mathfrak{a}$$ is an $$A$$-algebra under this definition, but as this is the same as for rings, we omit it. Moreover, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop12">**Proposition 12**</ins> For an $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$u:E \rightarrow F$$ and its kernel $$\ker u$$, the following hold.

1. $$\ker u$$ is a two-sided ideal of $$E$$, and the assignment $$x+\ker u \mapsto u(x)$$ defines a well-defined isomorphism $$E/\ker u \rightarrow \im u$$.
2. For a subalgebra $$E'$$ of $$E$$, the set $$E'+\ker u=\{x'+y\mid x'\in E', y\in\ker u\}$$ is a subalgebra of $$E$$, the intersection $$E'\cap\ker u$$ is a two-sided ideal of $$E'$$, and there is an isomorphism $$(E'+\ker u)/\ker u\cong E'/(E'\cap \ker u)$$.
3. If two two-sided ideals $$\mathfrak{a}, \mathfrak{b}$$ of $$E$$ satisfy $$\mathfrak{b}\subseteq \mathfrak{a}$$, then $$\mathfrak{a}/\mathfrak{b}$$ is a two-sided ideal of $$E/\mathfrak{b}$$ and $$(E/\mathfrak{b})/(\mathfrak{a}/\mathfrak{b})\cong E/\mathfrak{a}$$.
4. For a two-sided ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of $$E$$, there exists an inclusion-preserving bijection between the set of two-sided ideals of $$E/\mathfrak{a}$$ and the set of ideals of $$E$$ containing $$\mathfrak{a}$$.

</div>
