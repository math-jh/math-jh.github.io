## Cotangent Space and Dimension

In the previous post we saw that $$T_pM$$ is an $$\mathbb{R}$$-vector space. Now we will show that $$T_pM$$ is in fact finite-dimensional; instead of proving this directly, we show that the dual space of $$T_pM$$ is finite-dimensional.

By [§Tangent Space, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/manifold/tangent_space#prop2), the descending chain of ideals in $$\mathcal{C}^\infty_p$$

$$\mathcal{C}^\infty_p\supset\mathfrak{m}_p\supset\mathfrak{m}_p^2\supset\cdots$$

is well-defined. In particular, we may view $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ as a vector space over $$\mathcal{C}^\infty_p/\mathfrak{m}_p\cong\mathbb{R}$$. 

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem1">**Lemma 1**</ins> For a manifold $$M$$ and any point $$p\in M$$, we have $$T_pM\cong(\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2)^\ast$$. 

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, suppose an arbitrary $$v\in T_pM$$ is given. Restricting this to the subset $$\mathfrak{m}_p$$, we have $$v\vert_{\mathfrak{m}_p}\in\Hom_\mathbb{R}(\mathfrak{m}_p,\mathbb{R})$$. Now, to show that $$v\vert_{\mathfrak{m}_p}$$ defines a linear map $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2\rightarrow\mathbb{R}$$ well, we must show that $$\mathfrak{m}_p^2\subset\ker (v\vert_{\mathfrak{m}_p})$$. For a suitable index set $$I$$, suppose $$\mathfrak{m}_p$$ is the ideal generated by the $$\mathbf{f}_i$$. Then $$\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ is the ideal generated by the $$\mathbf{f}_i\mathbf{f}_j$$. However,

$$v(\mathbf{f}_i\mathbf{f}_j)=\mathbf{f}_i(p)v(\mathbf{f}_j)+\mathbf{f}_j(p)v(\mathbf{f}_i)=0$$

so $$v$$ sends any generator of $$\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ to $$0$$, and thus $$\mathfrak{m}_p^2\subset\ker(v\vert_{\mathfrak{m}_p})$$; therefore for each $$v\in T_pM$$ we can associate a suitable element of $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$. That this correspondence is an $$\mathbb{R}$$-linear map is obvious.

Conversely, suppose an arbitrary $$L\in(\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2)^\ast$$ is given, and let us use it to construct a tangent vector $$v_L$$. A tangent vector is a linear map from $$C_p^\infty$$ to $$\mathbb{R}$$, so constructing $$v_L$$ amounts to specifying the value of $$v_L(\mathbf{f})$$ for arbitrary $$\mathbf{f}\in \mathcal{C}^\infty_p$$. If we let $$\mathbf{f(p)}$$ denote the constant function with function value $$f(p)$$, then $$\mathbf{f}-\mathbf{f(p)}$$ is an element of $$\mathfrak{m}_p$$, and hence 

$$v_L(\mathbf{f})=L((\mathbf{f}-\mathbf{f(p)})+\mathfrak{m}_p^2)$$

is well-defined. We must show that this $$v_L$$ is not only a linear map but also satisfies [§Tangent Space, ⁋Definition 3](/en/math/manifold/tangent_space#def3); thus we compute

$$\begin{aligned}
            v_L(\mathbf{f}\cdot\mathbf{g})&=L((\mathbf{fg}-\mathbf{f(p)g(p)})+\mathfrak{m}_p^2)\\
            &=L(((\mathbf{f}-\mathbf{f(p)})(\mathbf{g}-\mathbf{g(p)})+\mathbf{f(p)}(\mathbf{g}-\mathbf{g(p)})+(\mathbf{f}-\mathbf{f(p)})\mathbf{g(p)})+\mathfrak{m}_p^2)\\
            &=\mathbf{f}(p)L((\mathbf{g}-\mathbf{g(p)})+\mathfrak{m}_p^2)+\mathbf{g}(p)L((\mathbf{f}-\mathbf{f(p)})+\mathfrak{m}_p^2)\\
            &=\mathbf{f}(p)+v_L(\mathbf{g})+\mathbf{g}(p)v_L(\mathbf{f}).
        \end{aligned}
$$

It is easy to see that $$L\mapsto v_L$$ is also a linear map, and moreover one can verify that this correspondence is the inverse of the linear map from $$T_pM$$ to $$(\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2)^\ast$$ defined above.

</details>

Therefore, if $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ is finite-dimensional then so is $$T_pM$$, and in this case

$$(T_pM)^\ast\cong(\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2)^{\ast\ast}\cong\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$

so we call $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ the *cotangent space*. 

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm2">**Theorem 2**</ins> The $$\mathbb{R}$$-vector space $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ is finite-dimensional, and its dimension equals that of the manifold $$M$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

To show this, we use the following multivariate Taylor approximation

$$\begin{aligned}g(x)&=g(x_0)+\sum_{i=1}^m\frac{\partial g}{\partial r^i}\bigg|_{x_0}(r^i(x)-r^i(x_0))\\
&\phantom{phantom}+\sum_{i,j}(r^i(x)-r^i(x_0))(r^j(x)-r^j(x_0))\int_0^1(1-t)\frac{\partial^2g}{\partial r^i\partial r^j}\bigg|_{(x_0+t(x-x_0))}\mathop{dt}\end{aligned}$$

Let $$(U,\varphi)$$ be a coordinate system centered at $$p$$, and let $$\varphi=(x^i)_{i=1}^m$$. Also, suppose $$\mathbf{f}\in\mathfrak{m}_p$$ is given arbitrarily. 

Since the above formula holds in Euclidean space, set $$g=f\circ\varphi^{-1}$$ and regard the domain of $$g$$ as $$\varphi(U)$$. From the Taylor approximation centered at the origin, for arbitrary $$x\in\varphi(U)$$ we obtain the formula

$$g(x)=g(0)+\sum_{i=1}^m\frac{\partial g}{\partial r^i}\bigg|_0r^i(x)+\sum_{i,j}r^i(x)r^j(x)\int_0^1(1-t)\frac{\partial^2g}{\partial r^i\partial r^j}\bigg|_{tx}\mathop{dt}$$

Now if $$x=\varphi(q)$$, then

$$\begin{aligned}f(q)&=f(p)+\sum_{i=1}^m\frac{\partial (f\circ\varphi^{-1})}{\partial r^i}\bigg|_0r^i(\varphi(q))+\sum_{i,j}r^i(\varphi(q))r^j(\varphi(q))\int_0^1(1-t)\frac{\partial^2g}{\partial r^i\partial r^j}\bigg|_{t\varphi(q)}\mathop{dt}\\ &=f(p)+\sum_{i=1}^m\frac{\partial(f\circ\varphi^{-1})}{\partial r^i}\bigg|_0 x^i(q)+\sum_{i,j} x^i(q)x^j(q)\int_0^1(1-t)\frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial r^i\partial r^j}\bigg|_{t\varphi(q)}dt\end{aligned}$$

Examining the right-hand side, since $$\mathbf{f}\in\mathfrak{m}_p$$ we have $$f(p)=0$$, and moreover the integral on the right-hand side is a $$C^\infty$$ function of $$q$$. Now the $$x^i$$ are all functions satisfying $$x^i(p)=0$$, so passing to germs, the double sum on the right-hand side becomes an element of $$\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$. Collecting everything,

$$\mathbf{f}=\sum_{i=1}^m\frac{\partial(f\circ\varphi^{-1})}{\partial r^i}\bigg|_0\mathbf{x}^i\mod \mathfrak{m}_p^2$$

holds. Since $$\mathbf{f}$$ is arbitrary, we see that $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ is generated by the $$\mathbf{x}^i+\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$.

To complete the proof we must show that these $$m$$ elements $$\mathbf{x}^i+\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ are linearly independent. Suppose

$$\sum_{i=1}^m a_i\mathbf{x}^i=0\mod \mathfrak{m}_p^2$$

Then on $$U$$ this equation becomes

$$\sum_{i=1}^m a_i (x^i\circ\varphi^{-1})=0\mod \mathfrak{m}_0^2$$

(where $$\mathfrak{m}_0$$ is the maximal ideal corresponding to the point $$0\in\varphi(U)$$), and since $$x^i\circ\varphi^{-1}=r^i$$, 

$$\sum_{i=1}^m a_i\mathbf{r}^i=0\mod \mathfrak{m}^2_0$$

We have not yet defined $$\partial/\partial x^i$$, but we are well acquainted with directional derivatives in Euclidean space. Using this, applying $$\partial/\partial r^j$$ to both sides of the above equation, the $$0$$ on the right-hand side (that is, some element of $$\mathfrak{n}^2$$) becomes $$0$$ by the Leibniz rule, and hence this equation yields

$$a_j=\frac{\partial}{\partial r^j}\bigg|_0\sum a_i r^i=0$$

Therefore $$a_j=0$$ holds for all $$j$$, and the $$\mathbf{x}^i+\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ are linearly independent.

</details>

Looking carefully at the proof of this theorem, we obtain not only information about the dimension but also a basis of $$T_pM$$. We showed that the $$\mathbf{x}^i+\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$ form a basis of $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$, and since we know that the tangent space $$T_pM$$ is isomorphic to $$(\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2)^\ast$$, it is natural to take the dual basis of these as a basis of $$T_pM$$. That is,

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> Let a manifold $$M$$ and $$p\in M$$ be given. For a coordinate system $$(U,\varphi)$$ containing $$p$$ and the component functions $$x^i$$ of $$\varphi$$, the *directional derivative* in the $$x^i$$ direction is the tangent vector defined by the formula

$$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p\right)f=\frac{\partial(f\circ \varphi^{-1})}{\partial r^i}\bigg|_{\varphi(p)}$$

</div>

Of course all tangent vectors can be thought of as directional derivatives, but what is special about these vectors $$\partial/\partial x^i$$ is that they are *exactly* the derivatives in the directions parallel to the coordinate axes determined by the coordinate system. 

These $$m$$ tangent vectors are, as mentioned earlier, the dual of the basis of $$\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$, so it is obvious that they form a basis. Then, for arbitrary $$v\in T_pM$$, the way to express $$v$$ as a linear combination of the $$\partial/\partial x^i$$ must also exist uniquely. It is given by the formula

$$v=\sum_{i=1}^m v(x^i)\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p$$

To check that this formula is correct, it suffices to apply the tangent vectors on both sides of this equation to an arbitrary $$\mathbf{f}$$ and compare the results. Or more simply, since $$\mathbf{f}$$ is expressed as a linear combination of the $$\mathbf{x}^i+\mathfrak{m}_p^2$$, it is enough to apply them only to $$\mathbf{x}^j$$. Applying the right-hand side to $$\mathbf{x}^j$$,

$$\sum_{i=1}^m v(x^i)\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p x^j=\sum_{i=1}^m v(x^i)\delta_{ij}=v(x^j)$$

so the above formula holds. 

Now that we have defined tangent vectors in rigorous language, from the next post onward we will write elements of $$\mathcal{C}^\infty_p$$ simply as $$f$$ instead of germs $$\mathbf{f}$$. Here $$f\in \mathcal{C}^\infty_p$$ means that $$f$$ is a $$C^\infty$$ function defined on a suitable open neighborhood of $$p\in M$$.
