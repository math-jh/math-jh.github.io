Now we shall define determinants; to this end, we first define tensor algebras, symmetric algebras, and exterior algebras. Throughout this process, we always assume that $$A$$ is a commutative ring. Then in particular, $$A$$ has the IBN property. ([§Bases, ⁋Proposition 6](/en/math/multilinear_algebra/basis_of_free_modules#prop6))

## Definition of the Tensor Algebra

For any $$A$$-module $$M$$, we previously defined the free algebra $$F(M)$$ determined by $$M$$ via the formula

$$F(M)=\bigoplus_{n\geq 0} M^{\otimes n}$$

([\[Algebraic Structures\] §Algebras, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/algebraic_structures/algebras#prop4)). This is not merely an algebra, but naturally carries the structure of an $$\mathbb{N}_{\geq 0}$$-graded associative unital algebra. We name it as follows.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> We call the $$F(M)$$ defined above the *tensor algebra* of $$M$$, and denote it by $$\T(M)$$.

</div>

We denote each component $$M^{\otimes n}$$ by $$\T^n(M)$$. Then since $$\T^1(M)=M$$, there exists a canonical injection $$\iota: M \rightarrow \T(M)$$ from $$M$$ into $$\T(M)$$.

Now considering the adjunction $$T\dashv U$$, the map $$\iota$$ is the image of $$\id_{\T(M)}$$ under the following adjunction

$$\Hom_{\Alg{A}}(\T(M), \T(M))\cong \Hom_{\rMod{A}}(M, U\T(M))$$

; if we view $$\T(M)$$ as an $$\mathbb{N}$$-graded associative unital algebra, we only need to change the left-hand side to the appropriate category. Unwinding this adjoint as a universal property gives the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop2">**Proposition 2**</ins> Let an arbitrary $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ and an $$A$$-linear map $$u:M \rightarrow E$$ be given. Then there exists a unique $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$g: \T(M) \rightarrow E$$ such that $$f=g \circ\iota$$.

In addition, if $$E$$ is an $$\mathbb{N}$$-graded $$A$$-algebra and $$u(M)\subseteq E_1$$ holds, then the $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$g$$ obtained above becomes an $$\mathbb{N}$$-graded $$A$$-algebra homomorphism.

</div>

If the linear map $$u$$ above is surjective, then since $$\T(N)$$ is generated by $$\T^1(N)$$, we know that $$\T(u): \T(M) \rightarrow \T(N)$$ is surjective.

## Properties of Tensor Algebras

Now we examine how operations in $$\rMod{A}$$ behave when transferred via the functor $$T:\rMod{A} \rightarrow \Alg{A}$$. We are particularly interested in direct sums and extensions of scalars. As in [Proposition 2](#prop2), the discussion in this section remains valid even if we understand $$T$$ as a functor from $$\rMod{A}$$ to the category of associative unital $$\mathbb{N}$$-graded $$A$$-algebras; however, to avoid notational complexity, we shall write the target category as $$\Alg{A}$$.

First we examine the case of direct sums. Let $$M=\bigoplus_{i\in I} M_i$$ be the direct sum of $$A$$-modules $$M_i$$. Then, using the fact that $$\otimes$$ is a left adjoint of $$\Hom$$ and a little induction, we obtain the following isomorphism

$$\bigoplus_{(i_1,\ldots, i_n)\in I^n}M_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes M_{i_n}\cong \T^n(M)$$

and then $$\T(M)$$ is given by the direct sum

$$\T(M)\cong\bigoplus_{n\geq 0} \T^n(M)\cong\bigoplus_{n\geq 0}\bigoplus_{(i_1,\ldots, i_n)\in I^n}M_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes M_{i_n}$$

Although this looks complicated as a formula, it is basically nothing more than expanding the coproduct of the graded algebras on the right-hand side, since $$T$$ is a left adjoint.[^1]

In particular, for any free $$A$$-module $$M$$, let $$\mathcal{B}=(e_i)_{i\in I}$$ be a basis of $$M$$. Then

$$M=\bigoplus_{i\in I} Ae_i$$

and applying the above explanation yields the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop3">**Proposition 3**</ins> In the above situation, $$\T(M)$$ has as its basis the elements $$e_s$$ of the form

$$e_s=e_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes e_{i_n},\qquad\text{$s$ a finite sequence $(i_1,i_2,\ldots,i_n)$ in $I$}$$

</div>

This is because each $$\T^n(M)$$ has as its basis the $$e_s$$ defined using finite sequences $$s$$ of length $$n$$, and their direct sum is $$\T(M)$$. Meanwhile, we know that using the structure constants from [§Bases, ⁋Definition 9](/en/math/multilinear_algebra/basis_of_free_modules#def9), we can describe the multiplication of $$\T(M)$$; according to the above explanation, this is nothing other than concatenating sequences. That is, for two sequences

$$s=(i_1,\ldots, i_m),\qquad t=(j_1,\ldots, j_n)$$

if we define $$st$$ as the following sequence

$$st=(i_1,\ldots, i_m,j_1,\ldots, j_n)$$

then the equation defining the structure constant becomes

$$e_se_t=e_{st}$$

In the case of extension of scalars, let a ring homomorphism $$\phi: A \rightarrow B$$ be given, and let $$M$$ be an $$A$$-module. Then there exist the extension of scalars $$\phi_!: \rMod{A} \rightarrow\rMod{B}$$ and the two functors $$\T_A: \rMod{A} \rightarrow \Alg{A}$$, $$\T_B:\rMod{B} \rightarrow \Alg{B}$$; in the obvious way, $$\phi_!:\Alg{A} \rightarrow\Alg{B}$$ is also defined. Through this we obtain the following (graded) $$B$$-linear map

![extension_of_scalars](/assets/images/Math/Multilinear_Algebra/Tensor_Algebras-1.png){:style="width:16.8em" class="invert" .align-center}

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">
 
<ins id="prop4">**Proposition 4**</ins> The $$B$$-linear map $$\T_{B}(B\otimes_AM)\rightarrow B\otimes_A\T_A(M)$$ obtained above is an isomorphism.
 
</div> 
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>
 
It suffices to construct an inverse. To this end, first, from the adjunction

$$\Hom_\rMod{B}(\phi_!M,\phi_!M)\cong\Hom_\rMod{A}(M, \phi^\ast \phi_!M)$$

we obtain the $$A$$-linear map $$i: M \rightarrow \phi^\ast\phi_!M$$ corresponding to $$\id_{\phi_!M}$$. $$\iota_{\phi_!M}: \phi_!M \rightarrow \T_B(\phi_!M)$$

this is an $$A$$-linear map from the $$A$$-module $$M$$ to the $$A$$-module $$\phi^\ast \T_B(\phi_!M)$$ (more precisely, $$U\phi^\ast \T_B(\phi^\ast\phi_!M)$$). $$\Hom_{\Alg{A}}(\T_A(M), \phi^\ast \T_B(\phi_!M))\cong \Hom_\Alg{B}(\phi_! \T_A(M), \T_B(\phi_!M))$$

viewing this as a $$B$$-linear map $$\phi_!\T_A(M) \rightarrow \T_B(\phi_!M)$$, we can verify that this is the inverse of the above $$B$$-linear map.

## Definition of the Symmetric Algebra

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def5">**Definition 5**</ins> For any $$A$$-module $$M$$, consider the two-sided ideal

$$\mathfrak{I}=\langle x\otimes y-y\otimes x\mid x,y\in M\rangle$$

of the tensor algebra $$\T(M)$$. Then we call the quotient algebra $$\T(M)/\mathfrak{I}$$ the *symmetric algebra* of $$M$$ and write it as $$\S(M)$$.

</div>

From the definition, since $$\mathfrak{I}$$ is a homogeneous ideal, it is obvious that $$\T(M)/\mathfrak{I}$$ becomes a $$\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$$-graded algebra. Moreover, since each generator $$x\otimes y-y\otimes x$$ is an element of degree $$2$$, taking the quotient by $$\mathfrak{I}$$ does not affect $$\T^0(M)$$ and $$\T^1(M)$$ at all. That is, $$\S^0(M)\cong A$$ and $$\S^1(M)\cong M$$.

It is obvious from the definition that $$\S(M)$$ is a commutative unital associative algebra. This is because $$\S(M)$$ is generated by the elements of $$\S^1(M)$$, and for any $$x,y\in \S^1(M)\cong M$$ we have

$$x\otimes y\equiv y\otimes x\pmod{\mathfrak{I}}$$

It is customary to write the product of two elements of $$\S(M)$$ as $$xy$$, like multiplication.

Meanwhile, from the universal property of quotient algebras and [Proposition 2](#prop2), the following universal property is also obtained obviously.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop6">**Proposition 6**</ins> Let an arbitrary $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ and an $$A$$-linear map $$u:M \rightarrow E$$ satisfying the following condition

$$u(x)u(y)=u(y)u(x)\qquad\text{for all $x,y\in M$}$$

be given. Then there exists a unique $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$g: \S(M) \rightarrow E$$ such that $$f=g \circ\iota$$.

</div>

More generally, let us fix arbitrary $$A$$-modules $$M,N$$ and a natural number $$n\geq 1$$. A *symmetric linear map* from $$M^n$$ to $$N$$ is one satisfying the following condition

$$f(x_{\sigma(1)},x_{\sigma(2)},\ldots, x_{\sigma(n)})=f(x_1,x_2,\ldots, x_n),\qquad \sigma\in S_n$$

for all $$(x_i)\in M^n$$ and $$\sigma\in S_n$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop7">**Proposition 7**</ins> For two $$A$$-modules $$M,N$$ and any $$A$$-linear map $$g:\S^n(M) \rightarrow N$$, the function defined by the formula

$$(x_1,x_2,\ldots, x_n) \mapsto g(x_1x_2\cdots x_n)$$

is $$n$$-linear, and through this correspondence there is defined a bijective $$A$$-module homomorphism from $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(\S^n(M), N)$$ to the $$A$$-module of symmetric $$n$$-linear maps $$M^n \rightarrow N$$.

</div>

Here, we call the $$A$$-module $$\S^n(M)$$ the *$$n$$th symmetric power* of $$M$$. Then for any $$A$$-linear map $$u:M \rightarrow N$$, $$\S^n(u): \S^n(M) \rightarrow \S^n(N)$$ is induced, and taking their direct sum recovers $$\S(u)$$.

## Properties of Symmetric Algebras

We examined earlier how the functor $$T$$ and operations in $$\rMod{A}$$ behave. Now we prove that these results also hold for $$S$$.

First, let $$M=\bigoplus_{i\in I} M_i$$ be the direct sum of $$A$$-modules $$M_i$$. Then we obtain the following isomorphism

$$\S(M)\cong \bigotimes_{i\in I} \S(M_i)$$

This is because $$S$$ is the left adjoint of the forgetful functor $$U:\cAlg{A}\rightarrow \rMod{M}$$, so it preserves colimits, and colimits in $$\cAlg{A}$$ are given by $$\otimes_A$$ (just as coproducts in $$\cRing$$ are tensor products). In particular, as in [Proposition 3](#prop3), once we fix a basis $$(e_i)$$ of a free $$A$$-module, we obtain the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8**</ins> For a free $$A$$-module $$M$$ and its basis $$(e_i)_{i\in I}$$, let $$\alpha:I \rightarrow \mathbb{N}$$ be a finitely supported function. 

If we set

$$e^\alpha=\prod_{i\in I} e_i^{\alpha(i)}$$

then the collection of all such elements forms a basis of $$\S(M)$$.

</div>

Their multiplication is given by $$e^\alpha e^\beta=e^{\alpha+\beta}$$. That is, in this case $$\S(M)$$ is exactly the polynomial algebra $$A[\x_i]_{i\in I}$$.

The result corresponding to [Proposition 4](#prop4) is the following proposition, and its proof is also the same.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">
 
<ins id="prop9">**Proposition 9**</ins> The map $$\S_{B}(B\otimes_AM)\rightarrow B\otimes_A\S_A(M)$$ is an isomorphism.

</div>

## Definition of the Exterior Algebra

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def10">**Definition 10**</ins> For any $$A$$-module $$M$$, consider the two-sided ideal 

$$\mathfrak{J}=\langle x\otimes x\mid x\in M\rangle$$

of the tensor algebra $$\T(M)$$. Then we call the quotient algebra $$\T(M)/\mathfrak{J}$$ the *exterior algebra* of $$M$$ and write it as $$\bigwedge(M)$$.

</div>

It is customary to write the product of elements in $$\bigwedge(M)$$ as $$\wedge$$. Meanwhile, just as in the discussion after [Definition 5](#def5), it is obvious that $$\mathfrak{J}$$ is a homogeneous ideal and that the canonical inclusion $$\iota:M \hookrightarrow\bigwedge(M)$$ exists. Moreover, for the same reason as in [Proposition 6](#prop6), the following universal property holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop11">**Proposition 11**</ins> Let an arbitrary $$A$$-algebra $$E$$ and an $$A$$-linear map $$u:M \rightarrow E$$ satisfying the following condition

$$u(x)^2=0\qquad\text{for all $x\in M$}$$

be given. Then there exists a unique $$A$$-algebra homomorphism $$g: \bigwedge(M) \rightarrow E$$ such that $$f=g \circ\iota$$.

</div>

A property similar to [Proposition 7](#prop7) also holds for the exterior algebra. For any $$A$$-modules $$M,N$$ and integer $$n\geq 1$$, an *alternating linear map* $$f$$ from $$M^n$$ to $$N$$ is one for which the following condition

$$f(x_{\sigma(1)},x_{\sigma(2)},\ldots, x_{\sigma(n)})=\epsilon(\sigma)f(x_1,x_2,\ldots, x_n),\qquad \sigma\in S_n$$

holds for all $$(x_i)\in M^n$$ and $$\sigma\in S_n$$. This is equivalent to the condition that

$$f(x_1,\ldots, x_i, x,x,x_{i+1},\ldots, x_{n-1})=0$$

holds for any $$x_1,\ldots, x_{n-1}$$ and $$x$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop12">**Proposition 12**</ins> For two $$A$$-modules $$M,N$$ and any $$A$$-linear map $$g:\S^n(M) \rightarrow N$$, the function defined by the formula

$$(x_1,x_2,\ldots, x_n) \mapsto g(x_1\wedge x_2\wedge\cdots\wedge x_n)$$

is $$n$$-linear, and through this correspondence there is defined a bijective $$A$$-module homomorphism from $$\Hom_{\lMod{A}}(\bigwedge^n(M), N)$$ to the $$A$$-module of alternating $$n$$-linear maps $$M^n \rightarrow N$$.

</div>

## Properties of Exterior Algebras

Likewise, when $$M=\bigoplus_{i\in I} M_i$$ is the direct sum of $$A$$-modules $$M_i$$, we know from the fact that $$\bigwedge$$ is a left adjoint that $$\bigwedge(M)$$ must be the colimit of the $$\bigwedge(M_i)$$. To make this rigorous, we need to define colimits in the category of alternating algebras. This comes out as a tensor product, similarly to $$\cAlg{A}$$, but with the Koszul sign convention attached. This is nothing special; it simply reflects the fact that, by definition, when we multiply elements of degrees $$m$$ and $$n$$ in the exterior algebra, a sign $$(-1)^{mn}$$ appears. There is no need to write this out rigorously right now, so we only introduce the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop13">**Proposition 13**</ins> For a free $$A$$-module $$M$$ and its basis $$(e_i)_{i\in I}$$, fix a total ordering on $$I$$. For any finite subset $$J\subseteq I$$, if we set

$$e_J=e_{j_1}\wedge e_{j_2}\wedge\cdots\wedge e_{j_k},\qquad j_1<\cdots < j_k, \quad J=\{j_1,\ldots, j_k\}$$

then the collection of all such $$e_J$$ forms a basis of $$\bigwedge (M)$$.

</div>

For example, $$e_1\wedge e_2\wedge e_3$$ and $$e_1\wedge e_3\wedge e_2$$ become the same element up to a sign difference if we simply swap the positions of the last two elements; thus, by giving $$I$$ an arbitrary order and arranging elements accordingly as above, we can avoid meaningless duplication. The next proposition needs even less explanation.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop14">**Proposition 14**</ins> The map $$\bigwedge_{B}(B\otimes_AM)\rightarrow B\otimes_A\bigwedge_A(M)$$ is an isomorphism.

</div>
