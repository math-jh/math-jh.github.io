Consider a rational function $$f \in \mathbb{K}(X)$$ defined on a variety $$X$$. This function vanishes at some points and is undefined at others. Since rational functions are essentially ratios of regular functions, the points where it is undefined are a kind of pole, and we may speak of the order of such poles. To describe this information systematically, we introduce the notion of a *divisor*.

For this to work, for each codimension 1 irreducible subvariety $Y$ of a variety $X$, the stalk $\mathcal{O}_{X, \eta_Y}$ at its generic point $\eta_Y$ must be a discrete valuation ring. For this to be well defined, it suffices that $X$ be a normal domain ([\[Commutative Algebra\] §Integral Extensions, ⁋Definition 3](/en/math/commutative_algebra/integral_extension#def3)), or more generally that the $R_1$ condition of [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Regular Local Rings, ⁋Theorem 10](/en/math/commutative_algebra/regular_local_rings#thm10) hold. However, normality of a variety is not such a stringent condition, and if a variety is not normal we can circumvent this issue by passing to its normalization ([\[Commutative Algebra\] §Regular Local Rings, ⁋Definition 9](/en/math/commutative_algebra/regular_local_rings#def9)); therefore, in any context where divisors appear, we implicitly assume $$X$$ to be normal.

## Weil Divisors

We first examine the most intuitive definition of a divisor, the *Weil divisor*. This is a formal sum of codimension 1 closed subvarieties with integer coefficients, where the coefficient of each term records the order of the zero or pole along the corresponding closed subvariety.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> A *Weil divisor* on a variety $$X$$ is a formal $$\mathbb{Z}$$-linear combination of codimension 1 (irreducible) closed subvarieties of $$X$$

$$D = \sum_{i=1}^{r} n_i Y_i$$

We write $$\Div(X)$$ for the set of Weil divisors.

</div>

Then $$\Div(X)$$ forms an abelian group under addition.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def2">**Definition 2**</ins> A Weil divisor $$D = \sum_i n_i Y_i$$ is *effective* if all $$n_i \ge 0$$. We write this as $$D \ge 0$$.

</div>

As noted above, the basic idea of a Weil divisor is to encode the zeros and poles of a rational function $$f \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$. Such a divisor is called a *principal divisor*.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> The *principal divisor* $$\divisor(f)$$ of a rational function $$f \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$ is defined by

$$\divisor(f) = \sum_{Y} v_Y(f) \cdot Y$$

where the sum runs over all codimension 1 irreducible closed subvarieties $$Y$$ of $$X$$, and $$v_Y(f)$$ is the integer giving the order of the zero or pole of $$f$$ along $$Y$$.

</div>

If $$Y$$ is a smooth subvariety of codimension 1, then in a neighborhood of each point $$x$$ on $$Y$$, the subvariety $$Y$$ is defined by a single regular function $$\pi$$. We call this $$\pi$$ a *local equation* near $$P$$. Expanding the rational function $$f$$ near $$x$$ as $$f = \pi^{v_Y(f)} \cdot u$$, all information about the zeros and poles of $$f$$ is carried by $$\pi^{v_Y(f)}$$; thus $$u$$ is a function having neither zeros nor poles near $$x$$, and $$v_Y(f)$$ is the zero/pole order along $$Y$$.

To render this mathematically rigorous, the stalk $$\mathcal{O}_{X, \eta}$$ of the structure sheaf at the generic point $\eta$ of $Y$ must be a discrete valuation ring, and the local equation $$\pi$$ corresponds to a uniformizer of this discrete valuation ring. Then $$v_Y(f)$$ is precisely the valuation in this discrete valuation ring. For this to make sense, the stalk $$\mathcal{O}_{X, \eta}$$ at the generic point of $$Y$$ must be a discrete valuation ring, and this is exactly why we take $$X$$ to be a normal variety.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex4">**Example 4**</ins> Consider the regular function $$f(\x, \y) = \x^2 \y$$ on $$\mathbb{A}^2$$. This function has no poles, and its zeros lie only along the two irreducible closed subvarieties $$D_1=Z(\x)$$ and $$D_2=Z(\y)$$. Along $$D_1$$ the zero has order 2, and along $$D_2$$ the zero has order 1; hence the principal divisor corresponding to $$f$$ is

$$\divisor(f)=2D_1+D_2$$

</div>

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex5">**Example 5**</ins> Consider the rational function on $$\mathbb{A}^1$$

$$g(\x) = \frac{(\x-a_1)^{n_1} \cdots (\x-a_k)^{n_k}}{(\x-b_1)^{m_1} \cdots (\x-b_l)^{m_l}}$$

This function has zeros of order $$n_i$$ at the $$a_i$$ and poles of order $$m_j$$ at the $$b_j$$, so its principal divisor is

$$\divisor(g) = n_1(a_1) + \cdots + n_k(a_k) - m_1(b_1) - \cdots - m_l(b_l)$$

Here $$(a_i)$$ denotes the divisor corresponding to the point $$a_i$$; positive coefficients indicate zeros and negative coefficients indicate poles.

</div>

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop6">**Proposition 6**</ins> $$\divisor: \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast \to \operatorname{Div}(X)$$ is a group homomorphism.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For $$f, g \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$, we have

$$v_Y(fg) = v_Y(f) + v_Y(g)$$

for each $$Y$$, and therefore

$$\divisor(fg) = \sum_Y v_Y(fg) \cdot Y = \sum_Y (v_Y(f) + v_Y(g)) \cdot Y = \divisor(f) + \divisor(g)$$

</details>

Our goal is to extract properties of $$X$$ from $$\Div(X)$$. However, $$\Div(X)$$ is unnecessarily large for this purpose. Since we already understand the elements of $$\mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$ to a reasonable extent, we shall regard a divisor as equivalent to the one obtained from it by adding a divisor arising from an element of $$\mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$. That is, we make the following definition.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def7">**Definition 7**</ins> Two Weil divisors $$D_1, D_2$$ are *linearly equivalent* if there exists a rational function $$f \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$ such that $$D_1 - D_2 = \divisor(f)$$. We write this as $$D_1 \sim D_2$$.

</div>

Then the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8**</ins> Linear equivalence $$\sim$$ is an equivalence relation on $$\operatorname{Div}(X)$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, $$D - D = 0 = \divisor(1)$$, so $$D \sim D$$. Also, if $$D_1 \sim D_2$$, then there exists $$f$$ with $$D_1 - D_2 = \divisor(f)$$, and for this $$f$$ we have $$D_2 - D_1 = \divisor(f^{-1})$$. Finally, assume $$D_1 \sim D_2$$ and $$D_2 \sim D_3$$; that is, there exist $$f, g$$ such that $$D_1 - D_2 = \divisor(f)$$ and $$D_2 - D_3 = \divisor(g)$$. Then $$D_1 - D_3 = \divisor(fg)$$, so $$D_1 \sim D_3$$.

</details>

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def9">**Definition 9**</ins> The *divisor class group* $$\Cl(X)$$ of $$X$$ is defined as the quotient of $$\operatorname{Div}(X)$$ by linear equivalence:

$$\Cl(X) = \operatorname{Div}(X) / \{\divisor(f) : f \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast\}$$

</div>

To know which elements of $$\Div(X)$$ become equal in $$\Cl(X)$$, it suffices to examine which divisors are linearly equivalent to $$0$$. This is essentially the algebro-geometric analogue of [\[Algebraic Topology\] §Homotopy, ⁋Definition 9](/en/math/algebraic_topology/homotopy#def9). For a fixed space $$X$$ and a subspace $$A$$, $$A$$ is a *deformation retract* of $$X$$ if there exists a continuous map

$$H:[0,1]\times X\rightarrow X$$

satisfying (i) $$H(0, x) = x$$, (ii) $$H(1, x) \in A$$, (iii) if $$a \in A$$ then $$H(t, a) = a$$. In other words, at $$t=0$$ we have all of $$X$$, at $$t=1$$ we obtain $$A$$, and $$A$$ itself remains fixed throughout the deformation. To translate this notion into algebraic geometry, we use $$\mathbb{P}^1$$ as the parameter space in place of $$[0,1]$$. More concretely, viewing an element $$f \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$ as a rational map $$X \dashrightarrow \mathbb{P}^1$$, we may consider its graph

$$\Gamma_f =\{(x, t)\in X\times \mathbb{P}^1\mid f(x)=t\}\subset X \times \mathbb{P}^1$$

Then pulling back the coordinate of $$\mathbb{P}^1$$ via the canonical projection $$\pr_2:\Gamma_f\rightarrow \mathbb{P}^1$$ yields $$f$$ itself; consequently, $$\divisor(f)$$ measures, on $$\Gamma_f$$, the difference between the $$t=0$$ section and the $$t=\infty$$ section.

From this motivation we may say that $$\Cl(X)$$ is smaller than $$\Div(X)$$, yet still retains information about the properties of $$X$$.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex10">**Example 10**</ins> $$\Cl(\mathbb{A}^n) = 0$$. To verify this, let an arbitrary Weil divisor $$D=\sum n_i Y_i$$ be given. We must show that this is the principal divisor of some function. Since each $$Y_i$$ is an irreducible closed subvariety, it corresponds to a prime ideal $$I(Y_i)$$ in the coordinate ring $$\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$, and $$\codim Y_i=\codim I(Y_i)=1$$. Now $$\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$ is a unique factorization domain by [\[Ring Theory\] §Polynomial Rings, ⁋Theorem 16](/en/math/ring_theory/polynomial_rings#thm16); hence every height 1 prime ideal is necessarily principal. That is, there exists a function $$f_i \in \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$ such that $$I(Y_i)=(f_i)$$, and consequently each $$Y_i$$ is $$Z(f_i)$$.

</div>

Of course, to understand what kind of information $$\Cl(X)$$ carries, we must consider the case $$\Cl(X)\neq 0$$.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex11">**Example 11**</ins> $$\Cl(\mathbb{P}^n) \cong \mathbb{Z}$$. To verify this, fix a hyperplane class, for instance $$H=Z(\x_0)$$. We first show that the hyperplane $$Z(F)$$ defined by an arbitrary homogeneous polynomial $$F$$ of degree $$d$$ equals $$dH$$. Consider the function $$F/\x_0^d\in \mathbb{K}(\mathbb{P}^n)^\ast$$; then

$$\divisor(F/\x_0^d)=\divisor(F)-d\cdot \divisor(\x_0)=Z(F)-dH$$

Hence a Weil divisor $$D=\sum n_i Y_i$$ is determined by the orders $$n_i$$ and the degrees $$d_i$$ of the homogeneous polynomials defining the $$Y_i$$. Guided by this intuition, define

$$\deg: \Cl(\mathbb{P}^n) \rightarrow \mathbb{Z};\qquad D=\sum n_i Y_i \mapsto \sum n_i \deg(Y_i)$$

where $$\deg(Y_i)$$ is the degree of the homogeneous polynomial defining $$Y_i$$. Every hypersurface is the zero set of some homogeneous polynomial, so there is no ambiguity in this definition. Moreover, by [§Rational Maps, ⁋Example 4](/en/math/algebraic_varieties/rational_maps#ex4) we know that any rational function on $$\mathbb{P}^n$$ is expressed as a ratio $$F/G$$ of homogeneous polynomials $$F,G$$ of the same degree; therefore for any principal divisor $$\divisor(F)$$ we necessarily have $$\deg(\divisor(F))=0$$, which shows that this map is well-defined.

We claim that $$\deg$$ is an isomorphism. Surjectivity is obvious because the image of $$dH$$ is $$d$$. For injectivity we must show that any $$D$$ with $$\deg(D)=0$$ is a principal divisor. But we saw above that $$D \sim dH$$, so for this to map to $$0$$ under $$\deg$$ we must have $$d=0$$; that is, $$D$$ is linearly equivalent to $$0$$.

Intuitively, the reason this differs from $$\mathbb{A}^n$$ is that every global regular function on $$\mathbb{P}^n$$ is constant. Thus, if a function on $$\mathbb{P}^n$$ has a zero at some point, it must necessarily have a pole at another point, and the sum of the zero orders must equal the sum of the pole orders. The isomorphism $$\Cl(\mathbb{P}^n)\cong \mathbb{Z}$$ means that for $$d\neq 0$$, the divisors $$dH$$ are essentially all the non-principal divisors of $$\mathbb{P}^n$$, while the remaining divisors $$\sum n_i D_i$$ merely distribute this order $$d$$ among the individual $$D_i$$ via a rational function $$f$$.

</div>

## Cartier Divisors

A Weil divisor is geometrically intuitive, but it has the drawback of not working well on singular varieties. For example, on the cone $$X=Z(\x^2 + \y^2 - \z^2) \subset \mathbb{A}^3$$, imagine trying to define a principal divisor following the explanation after [Definition 3](#def3). At a smooth point of this cone, say $$(1,0,1)$$, consider a codimension $$1$$ subvariety such as $$X\cap Z(\y)$$; this subvariety is defined in a neighborhood of this point by the local equation $$\y=0$$, so for any given rational function $$f$$ we can extract the zero and pole information along $$\y$$. The problem arises at the singular point. Consider, for instance, the codimension $$1$$ subvariety $$L=(t,0,t)$$ near the origin $$(0,0,0)$$. To describe it we need both equations $$\y=0$$ and $$\x-\z=0$$, which means we cannot define $$v_L(f)$$ properly. More precisely, $$\mathcal{O}_{X,(0,0,0)}$$ fails to be a (one-dimensional) regular local ring and hence is not a discrete valuation ring, so we cannot define a valuation in the first place.

Thus, the core issue is that on a singular variety there can appear codimension $$1$$ subvarieties that are not expressed locally by a single equation. Therefore we simply restrict our attention to those objects that are locally expressed by a single equation. As usual, we define this by means of an appropriate gluing.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def12">**Definition 12**</ins> A *Cartier divisor* on a variety $$X$$ is the following data:

$$\{(U_i, f_i)\}_{i \in I}$$

where $$\{U_i\}$$ is an open cover of $$X$$, each $$f_i \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$ is a nonzero rational function, and for all $$i, j$$ the quotient $$f_i/f_j$$ is regular and non-vanishing (hence invertible) on $$U_i \cap U_j$$.

Two such data $$\{(U_i, f_i)\}$$ and $$\{(V_j, g_j)\}$$ represent the same Cartier divisor if there exists a common refinement $$\{W_k\}$$ of the two open covers on which each $$f_i/g_j$$ is regular and non-vanishing.

</div>

By definition a Cartier divisor is nothing but a collection of codimension 1 subvarieties that are *locally principal*. The data $$U_i$$ explicitly specifies the open set to which we localize and the single function defining the divisor on that open set. Consequently, the line $$L$$ examined above cannot be expressed by a single equation on any open set containing the origin, so it is not a Cartier divisor.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex13">**Example 13**</ins> In the example of the cone above, the line $$L'=(t,0,-t)$$ is not a Cartier divisor for the same reason as $$L$$. However, their sum $$L+L'$$ is a Cartier divisor, because $$L+L'$$ is defined by the zero set of $$\y$$.

</div>

As noted above, a Cartier divisor can be thought of as a codimension 1 subvariety satisfying the additional locally principal condition. Concretely, given a Cartier divisor $$\{(U_i, f_i)\}$$, we can consider the principal Weil divisor $$\divisor(f_i)$$ of $$f_i$$ on each $$U_i$$. Since $$f_i/f_j$$ is invertible on $$U_i \cap U_j$$, we have $$v_Y(f_i)=v_Y(f_j)$$ for every codimension $$1$$ subvariety $$Y$$, and therefore we can glue these together to define a Weil divisor.

We saw above that the converse does not hold in general; at the same time, in the smooth case the stalk is a regular local ring, so there is no difficulty. That is, the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop14">**Proposition 14**</ins> On a smooth variety $$X$$, Weil divisors and Cartier divisors are in natural one-to-one correspondence.

</div>

Essentially, a Weil divisor plays the role of a specific subset of the space $$X$$, analogous to a homology class, whereas a Cartier divisor plays the role of functions defined (locally) on $$X$$, analogous to a cohomology class. From this viewpoint, the proposition may be thought of as a kind of Poincaré duality. ([\[Algebraic Topology\] §Poincaré Duality, ⁋Theorem 11](/en/math/algebraic_topology/Poincare_duality#thm11))

We now define linear equivalence and the divisor class group for Cartier divisors, just as we did for Weil divisors. For this we first need the following.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def15">**Definition 15**</ins> For a rational function $$f \in \mathbb{K}(X)^\ast$$, the *principal Cartier divisor* $$\divisor(f)$$ is defined as $$\{(X, f)\}$$.

</div>

Then the following definition is the Cartier analogue of [Definition 8](#def8).

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def16">**Definition 16**</ins> Two Cartier divisors $$D_1, D_2$$ are *linearly equivalent* if $$D_1 - D_2$$ is a principal divisor.

</div>

We write $$\CaDiv(X)$$ for the group of Cartier divisors and $$\Prin(X)$$ for the subgroup generated by principal divisors. Then the *Cartier divisor class group* is

$$\CaCl(X) = \CaDiv(X) / \Prin(X)$$

By [Proposition 14](#prop14), on a smooth variety we have $$\CaCl(X) \cong \Cl(X)$$.
