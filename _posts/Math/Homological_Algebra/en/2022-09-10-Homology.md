## The Category $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$

In [\[Category Theory\] §Abelian Categories](/en/math/category_theory/abelian_categories) we mentioned that the category $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ of chain complexes exists and is an abelian category; in this section we examine this claim in somewhat more detail. First, morphisms in $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ are given as follows.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> Given two chain complexes $$C_\bullet, D_\bullet$$, a *chain map* $$f_\bullet:C_\bullet\rightarrow D_\bullet$$ between them is a collection of maps $$f_n:C_n\rightarrow D_n$$ making the following diagram

![chain_map](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-1.png){:style="width:22em" class="invert" .align-center}

commute.

</div>

In other words, saying that $$f_\bullet$$ is a chain map means that for every $$n$$

$$f_{n-1}\circ d_n^C=d_n^D\circ f_n$$

holds. When there is no danger of confusion, we write both $$d^C$$ and $$d^D$$ simply as $$d$$. Then we define the category $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ by

- the collection of chain complexes in $$\mathcal{A}$$ as objects $$\obj(\Ch(\mathcal{A}))$$,
- the collection of chain maps from $$C_\bullet$$ to $$D_\bullet$$ as morphisms $$\Mor_{\Ch(\mathcal{A})}(C_\bullet,D_\bullet)$$.

It is obvious that $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ becomes a category. Here, $$\id_{C_\bullet}$$ is the chain map $$\id_\bullet:C_\bullet\rightarrow C_\bullet$$ defined by $$\id_n:C_n\rightarrow C_n$$ equal to $$\id_{C_n}$$ for all $$n$$, and the composition of chain maps is given by

$$g_\bullet\circ f_\bullet=(g_n\circ f_n)_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}.$$

Moreover, the category $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ is an $$\Ab$$-category. Given two chain maps $$f_\bullet,g_\bullet:C_\bullet\rightarrow D_\bullet$$, if we define their sum by

$$f_\bullet+g_\bullet=(f_n+g_n)_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}$$

then $$\Mor_{\Ch(\mathcal{A})}(C_\bullet,D_\bullet)$$ becomes an abelian group. Also, given two chain complexes $$C_\bullet,D_\bullet$$, their product $$C_\bullet\oplus D_\bullet$$ is[^1] the following chain complex

$$C_\bullet\oplus D_\bullet:\quad \cdots \longrightarrow C_{n+1}\oplus D_{n+1}\overset{d_{n+1}^C\oplus d_{n+1}^D}{\longrightarrow}C_n\oplus D_n\overset{d_{n}^C\oplus d_{n}^D}{\longrightarrow}C_{n-1}\oplus D_{n-1}\longrightarrow\cdots$$

because it suffices to define it as such. The zero object is the chain complex

$$\cdots\rightarrow 0\rightarrow 0\rightarrow 0\rightarrow\cdots.$$

To show that $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ is an abelian category, it suffices to define the kernel and cokernel of an arbitrary chain map $$f_\bullet:C_\bullet \rightarrow D_\bullet$$. First, for each $$n$$, since $$f_n:C_n \rightarrow D_n$$ is a morphism in the abelian category $$\mathcal{A}$$, we have $$i_n:\ker f_n\rightarrow C_n$$ and $$p_n:D_n\rightarrow \coker f_n$$. Consider the following diagram

![kernel_of_chain_map](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-2.png){:style="width:10em" class="invert" .align-center}

Then since

$$f_{n-1}\circ d_n\circ i_n=d_n\circ f_n\circ i_n=0$$

a morphism $$\ker f_n\rightarrow \ker f_{n-1}$$ is naturally induced from the universal property of $$\ker f_{n-1}$$. Similarly $$\coker f_n\rightarrow \coker f_{n-1}$$ is also defined, and these data respectively assemble into two chain complexes

$$\ker(f_\bullet):\qquad \cdots\longrightarrow \ker f_{n+1}\longrightarrow \ker f_n\longrightarrow \ker f_{n-1}\longrightarrow\cdots$$

and

$$\coker(f_\bullet):\qquad \cdots\longrightarrow \coker f_{n+1}\longrightarrow \coker f_n\longrightarrow \coker f_{n-1}\longrightarrow\cdots.$$

To show that $$\ker(f_\bullet)$$ defined in this way is the kernel of $$f_\bullet$$ in $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$, we must verify the following universal property

![universal_property_of_kernel_in_Ch_category](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-3.png){:style="width:13.6em" class="invert" .align-center}

and to do this we first construct $$j_n:A_n\rightarrow \ker f_n$$ through the following diagram for each $$n$$

![degreewise_universal_property](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-4.png){:style="width:13.6em" class="invert" .align-center}

and then it suffices to show that $$j_\bullet$$ is a chain map. That is, we need only prove that the top square of the following diagram

![monomorphism_trick](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-5.png){:style="width:10em" class="invert" .align-center}

commutes. But kernels are always monomorphisms, so for this it suffices to show that

$$i_{n-1}\circ(j_{n-1}\circ d_n^A)=i_{n-1}\circ(d_n\circ j_n)$$

holds, and this is obvious by definition; hence $$\ker(f_\bullet)$$ is indeed the kernel of $$f_\bullet$$. Similarly one can check that $$\coker(f_\bullet)$$ is the cokernel of $$f_\bullet$$ in $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$.

Finally, we must show that any monomorphism $$f_\bullet$$ equals $$\ker(\coker f_\bullet)$$, and any epimorphism $$f_\bullet$$ equals $$\coker(\ker f_\bullet)$$. This is done by first proving that $$f_\bullet$$ is a monomorphism (resp. epimorphism) if and only if $$f_n$$ is a monomorphism (resp. epimorphism) for every $$n$$, and then using the abelian category condition degree by degree and assembling the results. Since this process is almost the same as above, we omit the detailed argument.

## Homology

Fix a chain complex $$C_\bullet$$ and define a new chain complex $$C_{\bullet-1}$$ by

$$C_{\bullet-1}:\qquad\cdots\longrightarrow\underbrace{C_n}_\text{\small degree $n+1$}\overset{d_n}{\longrightarrow}\underbrace{C_{n-1}}_\text{\small degree $n$}\overset{d_{n-1}}{\longrightarrow}\underbrace{C_{n-2}}_\text{\small degree $n-1$}\longrightarrow\cdots\tag{1}$$

In other words, $$C_{\bullet-1}$$ is the chain complex obtained by shifting $$C_\bullet$$ by one place. Now define the chain map $$d_\bullet:C_\bullet\rightarrow C_{\bullet-1}$$ by the following diagram

![differential_endomorphism](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-6.png){:style="width:30em" class="invert" .align-center}

Then $$\ker (d_\bullet)$$ is well defined, and considering the following diagram

![differential_of_Z](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-7.png){:style="width:11em" class="invert" .align-center}

we see from $$d_{n+1}\circ i_{n+1}=0$$ and the fact that $$i_n$$ is a monomorphism that the differential of $$\ker(d_\bullet)$$ is the zero map everywhere.

Likewise $$\coker(d_\bullet)$$ is also well defined, and the differentials between its components are also zero. Unlike the $$n$$th component of $$\ker(d_\bullet)$$, which is $$\ker d_n\rightarrow C_n$$ as examined above, the $$n$$th component of $$\coker(d_\bullet)$$ is $$C_{n-1}\rightarrow \coker(d_n)$$, which may cause some confusion; therefore we instead consider the chain complex $$\coker(d_{\bullet+1})$$ obtained by shifting the degrees back by one.

Now, thinking of

$$\im(d_{\bullet+1})=\ker(C_\bullet\rightarrow \coker(d_{\bullet+1}))$$

and using the universal property of $$\ker(d_\bullet)\rightarrow C_\bullet$$, we obtain a monomorphism

$$\im(d_{\bullet+1})\rightarrow\ker(d_\bullet)$$

and through this we obtain the new chain complex

$$H_\bullet(C)=\ker(d_\bullet)/\im(d_{\bullet+1}).$$

Explicitly, $$H_n(C)$$ is induced by the following diagram

![differential_of_H](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-8.png){:style="width:23em" class="invert" .align-center}

Now, $$\ker(d_n)\rightarrow H_n$$ is an epimorphism, and since $$\ker(d_n)\rightarrow\ker(d_{n-1})$$ is $$0$$, the map $$H_n(C)\rightarrow H_{n-1}(C)$$ is also a zero map. This shows that (just as with $$\ker(d_\bullet)$$ or $$\coker(d_\bullet)$$) $$H_\bullet(C)$$ is not very meaningful as a chain complex; instead, its individual components $$H_n(C)\in\obj(\mathcal{A})$$ are the objects of interest.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">
 
<ins id="def2">**Definition 2**</ins> For an arbitrary chain complex

$$C_\bullet:\quad\cdots\overset{d_{n+2}}{\longrightarrow} C_{n+1}\overset{d_{n+1}}{\longrightarrow} C_n\overset{d_n}{\longrightarrow} C_{n-1}\overset{d_{n-1}}{\longrightarrow}\cdots$$

the module of *$$n$$-cycles* of $$C_\bullet$$ is given by $$Z_n(C)=\ker (d_n)$$. Also, we call $$\im(d_{n+1})$$ the module of *$$n$$-boundaries* of $$C_\bullet$$, and write it as $$B_n(C)$$. The *$$n$$th homology* of $$C_\bullet$$ is then defined by $$H_n(C)=Z_n(C)/B_n(C)$$.
 
</div> 

Similarly, for a cochain complex one defines $$n$$-cocycles, $$n$$-coboundaries, and $$n$$th cohomology.

While $$H_\bullet$$ itself is a functor from $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ to $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$, each component $$H_n$$ defines a functor from $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ to $$\mathcal{A}$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop3">**Proposition 3**</ins> For every $$n$$, $$H_n$$ is a functor from $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$ to $$\mathcal{A}$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For an arbitrary chain map $$f_\bullet:C_\bullet\rightarrow D_\bullet$$, applying [§Diagram Chasing, ⁋Lemma 4](/en/math/homological_algebra/diagram_chasing#lem4) shows that $$f_n$$ induces $$Z_n(C)\rightarrow Z_n(D)$$ and $$B_n(C)\rightarrow B_n(D)$$. Therefore $$f_n$$ induces

$$H_n(f):H_n(C)\rightarrow H_n(D).$$

Checking that the data obtained in this way constitute a functor is straightforward.

</details>

## Double Complexes and Translation

Finally, we define double complexes. Intuitively, one can think of this as a chain complex made up of objects of $$\Ch(\mathcal{A})$$, i.e., an object of $$\Ch(\Ch(\mathcal{A}))$$. The chain complex $$C_{\bullet-1}$$ in (1) above and the chain map below it are examples of this.

However, when performing calculations later, one sometimes obtains cleaner results if the signs of the rows are changed.[^2] Adopting this sign convention, we define it as follows.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def4">**Definition 4**</ins> A *double complex* made up of objects of an abelian category $$\mathcal{A}$$ is given by two maps

$$d^h:C_{p,q}\rightarrow C_{p-1,q},\qquad d^v:C_{p,q}\rightarrow C_{p,q-1}$$

satisfying

$$(d^h)^2=(d^v)^2=d^vd^h+d^hd^v=0.$$

![double_complex](/assets/images/Math/Homological_Algebra/Homology-9.png){:style="width:24em" class="invert" .align-center}

</div>

The condition $$d^vd^h+d^hd^v=0$$ means $$d^vd^h=-d^hd^v$$, so double complexes are not objects of $$\Ch(\Ch(\mathcal{A}))$$; however, from $$d^v$$ we define the formula

$$f_{p,q}=(-1)^pd_{p,q}^v:C_{p,q}\rightarrow C_{p,q-1}$$

and then we can treat it as an object of $$\Ch(\Ch(\mathcal{A}))$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def5">**Definition 5**</ins> The *total complex* $$\Tot(C)_\bullet$$ of a double complex $$C_{p,q}$$ is defined in degree $$n$$ by

$$ (\Tot(C))_n = \bigoplus_{p+q=n} C_{p,q} $$

and its differential is given by

$$ d = d^h + (-1)^p d^v : (\Tot(C))_n \rightarrow (\Tot(C))_{n-1} $$

(Equivalently, one may also write $$d^v + (-1)^q d^h$$.)

</div>

For convenience of discussion we have written the differential in a somewhat abbreviated form, but unpacking it reveals that actual computations are somewhat more complicated than the formula above. For example, suppose a double complex $$C_{p,q}$$ is given, and for convenience assume that this complex is nonzero only where $$p,q\geq 0$$. Then in the total complex $$\Tot(C)_\bullet$$ of this double complex, to compute the differential $$d:\Tot(C)_2\rightarrow \Tot(C)_1$$ we must proceed as follows. First, an element of $$\Tot(C)_2$$ has the form $$(x_{0,2}, x_{1,1}, x_{2,0})$$,[^3] and the differential of this element is the following element containing all of this information:

$$(d^v(x_{0,2})+d^h(x_{1,1}), -d^v(x_{1,1})+d^h(x_{2,0}))\in\Tot(C)_1=C_{0,1}\oplus C_{1,0}$$

In other words, $$\Tot(C)$$ packages the double complex $$C_{p,q}$$ into a single complex, taking into account simultaneously all components having total degree $$p+q=n$$.

To show that this is a complex, we must verify $$d^2=0$$, which is obtained from the formula

$$ (d^h + (-1)^p d^v)^2 = (d^h)^2 + (-1)^p d^vd^h + (-1)^{p-1} d^hd^v + (d^v)^2 $$

Here $$(d^h)^2=0$$ and $$(d^v)^2=0$$, and from the double complex condition $$d^vd^h + d^hd^v=0$$ we have $$d^vd^h = -d^hd^v$$, so the remaining two terms cancel as well.

Meanwhile, since we used the above sign convention in defining double complexes, the *$$p$$-th translation* $$C[p]$$ of $$C$$ is also the chain complex given by the formula

$$C[p]_n=C_{n+p}$$

and in this case the differential must be defined as $$(-1)^pd$$. For instance, $$C[-1]$$ means the same as $$C_{\bullet-1}$$ defined above, but denotes the chain complex with the signs of the differentials reversed.

In any case, regardless of these newly introduced signs,

$$H_n(C[p])=H_{n+p}(C)$$

is obvious; moreover, if $$f:C\rightarrow D$$ is a chain map, we can define $$f[p]$$ by the formula

$$f[p]_n=f_{n+p}$$

and thereby make $$f[p]:C[p]\rightarrow D[p]$$. Thus translation is a functor from $$\Ch(\mathcal{C})$$ to itself.

## Truncation

Finally, let an arbitrary chain complex $$C_\bullet$$ be given. For any integer $$n$$, the chain complex $$(\tau_{\geq n}C)_\bullet$$ defined by the formula

$$(\tau_{\geq n}C)_i=\begin{cases}0&\text{if $i < n$}\\ Z_n&\text{if $i=n$}\\ C_i&\text{if $i >  n$}\end{cases}$$

and with the same differential as $$C_\bullet$$ is called the *intelligent truncation*. Then

$$H_i(\tau_{\geq n}C)=\begin{cases}0&\text{if $i < n$}\\ H_i(C)&\text{if $i\geq n$}\end{cases}$$

Similarly one can also define $$(\tau_{< n}C)_\bullet$$. On the other hand, the *brutal truncation* is defined by the formula

$$(\sigma_{\geq n}C)_i=\begin{cases}0&\text{if $i \leq n$}\\ C_i&\text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$

Although the definition of $$(\sigma_{\geq n}C)_\bullet$$ may appear more natural, examining the $$n$$th homology shows that the intelligent truncation yields a better operation.
