The properties of infinite sequences discussed in [§Limits of Sequences](/en/math/calculus/sequences) are particularly important in our situation when dealing with *infinite series*.

## Partial Sums and Infinite Series

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> If the sequence of *partial sums* $$s_N = \sum_{n=1}^N a_n$$ formed from a sequence $$(a_n)$$ converges to $$S$$, we say that the *infinite series* $$\sum a_n$$ *converges* to $$S$$ and write $$\sum a_n = S$$. If $$(s_N)$$ diverges, we say the series *diverges*.

</div>

From this perspective, the convergence or divergence of a series reduces to the convergence or divergence of the sequence $$s_N$$ called the <em-ko>partial sums</em-ko>, so we can use the tools from [§Limits of Sequences](/en/math/calculus/sequences) directly. The following are partial sums of two particularly useful series.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex2">**Example 2**</ins>

- The *geometric series* $$\sum r^n$$ is the infinite series defined by the partial sum
    
    $$s_N = \sum_{n=0}^N r^n = \frac{1 - r^{N+1}}{1-r} \qquad (r \neq 1)$$
  
    and the above formula can be verified through $$(1-r)s_N = (1-r)(1 + r + \cdots + r^N) = 1 - r^{N+1}$$. If $$\lvert r\rvert < 1$$, then $$r^{N+1} \to 0$$, so we know this infinite sum converges to $$1/(1-r)$$. If $$\lvert r\rvert \geq 1$$, we can prove that this sequence is not bounded below or oscillates, hence diverges.
- The *$$p$$-series* $$\sum_n 1/n^p$$ converges if $$p > 1$$ and diverges if $$p \leq 1$$. If $$p \leq 0$$, the general term $$1/n^p = n^{-p}$$ diverges to infinity, so in particular the partial sums are not bounded above and thus diverge. Let us consider the case $$p > 0$$. Excluding the first term of the partial sum and grouping $$2^k$$ terms at a time $$(k=1,\dots,m)$$, we examine up to the $$2^{m+1}-1$$-th term:
    
    $$s_{2^{m+1}-1} = 1 + \left(\frac{1}{2^p}+\frac{1}{3^p}\right) + \left(\frac{1}{4^p}+\cdots+\frac{1}{7^p}\right) + \cdots + \left(\frac{1}{(2^m)^p}+\cdots+\frac{1}{(2^{m+1}-1)^p}\right)$$
    
    Here, the $$k$$-th parentheses contains $$2^k$$ terms, and every term inside is less than or equal to $$1/(2^k)^p$$, so
    
    $$s_{2^{m+1}-1} \leq \sum_{k=0}^m 2^k \cdot \frac{1}{2^{kp}} = \sum_{k=0}^m (2^{1-p})^k$$
    
    If $$p > 1$$, then $$2^{1-p} < 1$$, so the right-hand side is a partial sum of a geometric series and is bounded; applying [§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7), we can show that the $$p$$-series converges. On the other hand, if $$0 < p \leq 1$$, then the sum of each parentheses is at least as large as the smallest term $$1/(2^{k+1}-1)^p$$ among the $$2^k$$ terms, so

    $$\frac{2^k}{(2^{k+1}-1)^p} \geq \frac{2^k}{2^{k+1}-1} \geq \frac{1}{2}$$

    Therefore, from
    
    $$s_{2^{m+1}-1} \geq 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2} \geq 1 + \frac{m}{2}$$
    
    we can verify that the partial sums diverge to infinity.

</div>

The perspective of [Definition 1](#def1) that a series reduces to the convergence problem of a sequence of partial sums implies that the properties of limits of sequences carry over directly to series. The most basic is linearity: the termwise sum and scalar multiple of two convergent series again preserve convergence. This is the linearity of limits at the level of partial sums ([§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop2)).

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop3">**Proposition 3 (Linearity of Series)**</ins> If $$\sum a_n = A$$ and $$\sum b_n = B$$ converge and $$\lambda, \mu$$ are constants, then $$\sum (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n)$$ also converges and its sum is $$\lambda A + \mu B$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let the partial sums be $$s_N = \sum_{n=1}^N a_n$$ and $$t_N = \sum_{n=1}^N b_n$$. Since these are finite sums,

$$\begin{aligned}
\sum_{n=1}^N (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n) &= \lambda \sum_{n=1}^N a_n + \mu \sum_{n=1}^N b_n \\
&= \lambda s_N + \mu t_N
\end{aligned}$$

Since $$s_N \to A$$ and $$t_N \to B$$, by linearity of convergent sequences the right-hand side converges to $$\lambda A + \mu B$$. Hence the partial sums on the left also converge to the same value, yielding the conclusion.

</details>

## Convergence Tests

In most cases of series, finding the exact sum is too ambitious, and often only the convergence behavior is needed. The most basic yet powerful tool for determining this is the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop4">**Proposition 4 (Term Test)**</ins> If the series $$\sum a_n$$ converges, then $$a_n \to 0$$. Therefore, if $$a_n \not\to 0$$, the series diverges.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $$s_N \to S$$, then $$s_{N-1} \to S$$ as well, so $$a_N = s_N - s_{N-1} \to S - S = 0$$.

</details>

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex5">**Example 5 (Harmonic Series)**</ins> The *harmonic series*

$$\sum \frac{1}{n} = 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \cdots$$

has terms going to $$0$$ but diverges. This is the special case $$p=1$$ from [Example 2](#ex2); similarly, grouping terms in blocks of $$2^k$$,

$$1 + \frac{1}{2} + \left(\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{4}\right) + \left(\frac{1}{5}+\cdots+\frac{1}{8}\right) + \cdots$$

we see that the sum of each parentheses is at least $$1/2$$, so the partial sums grow without bound.

</div>

As the harmonic series shows, $$a_n \to 0$$ is only a necessary condition for convergence, not sufficient. On the other hand, for a series with positive terms, the partial sums are monotone increasing, so applying [§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7), the question of convergence reduces to checking boundedness of the sums.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm6">**Theorem 6**</ins> For a series with positive terms, the following hold.

1. If $$0 \leq a_n \leq b_n$$ and $$\sum b_n$$ converges, then $$\sum a_n$$ also converges.
2. (Ratio Test) If $$a_{n+1}/a_n \to L$$, then the series converges if $$L < 1$$ and diverges if $$L > 1$$.
3. ([§Improper Integrals, ⁋Example 8](/en/math/calculus/improper_integrals#ex8)) If $$f$$ is a positive decreasing continuous function and $$a_n = f(n)$$, then $$\sum a_n$$ and $$\int_1^\infty f$$ converge or diverge simultaneously.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

1: The partial sums $$(s_N)$$ of $$\sum a_n$$ are increasing and bounded above by $$s_N \leq \sum_n b_n$$, so by the monotone convergence theorem ([§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7)) they converge.

2: If $$L < 1$$, choose $$r$$ with $$L < r < 1$$; then for large $$n$$ we have $$a_{n+1} \leq r a_n$$, hence $$a_{N+k} \leq r^k a_N$$, and the series is compared to a convergent geometric series. If $$L > 1$$, the terms increase and do not go to $$0$$, so the series diverges by the term test.

3: Since $$f$$ is decreasing, $$\int_k^{k+1} f \leq f(k) \leq \int_{k-1}^k f$$, and summing over $$k$$ sandwiches the partial sums between integrals from above and below, so the two converge or diverge together.

</details>

Observe that in the ratio test, the case $$L = 1$$ is inconclusive. For instance, all $$p$$-series have $$L = 1$$, yet their convergence behavior depends on $$p$$; for this reason, when testing convergence of a series, it is important to choose the appropriate test.

The comparison test is the most powerful among the above examples, since it can be applied to any series provided one can find a suitable inequality, but it carries the burden of having to bound the terms directly by those of another series. In practice, it is often clear that the terms of a series are of the <em-ko>same order of magnitude</em-ko> as some standard series, yet a clean inequality is hard to obtain. In this case, the following variant is convenient: it suffices to look at the limit of the ratio of terms.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop7">**Proposition 7 (Limit Comparison Test)**</ins> For positive-term series $$\sum a_n$$ and $$\sum b_n$$, if $$a_n/b_n \to c$$ with $$0 < c < \infty$$, then the two series converge or diverge together.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

From the definition of the limit, for sufficiently large $$n$$ we can arrange

$$\frac{c}{2} < \frac{a_n}{b_n} < 2c$$

Multiplying both sides by $$b_n > 0$$,

$$\begin{aligned}
\frac{c}{2}\,b_n &< a_n < 2c\,b_n
\end{aligned}$$

If $$\sum b_n$$ converges, then $$\sum 2c\,b_n$$ also converges, so by the right-hand inequality and the comparison test ([Theorem 6](#thm6)), $$\sum a_n$$ converges. Conversely, if $$\sum a_n$$ converges, then by the left-hand inequality $$b_n < (2/c) a_n$$ and the comparison test, $$\sum b_n$$ converges. The divergence case follows by contrapositive.

</details>

The utility of the limit comparison test lies in replacing terms mixed with polynomials and powers by the dominant term governing convergence. If the term is a fraction, one need only keep the fastest-growing terms in the numerator and denominator as a power for the standard $$b_n$$, and check whether the limit of $$a_n/b_n$$ lies in $$(0, \infty)$$, eliminating the labor of matching inequalities by hand.

Another effective test is the following *root test*.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8 (Root Test)**</ins> For a positive-term series $$\sum a_n$$, if $$\sqrt[n]{a_n} \to L$$, then the series converges if $$L < 1$$ and diverges if $$L > 1$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $$L < 1$$, we can choose $$r$$ with $$L < r < 1$$, and for sufficiently large $$n$$ we have $$\sqrt[n]{a_n} < r$$, i.e., $$a_n < r^n$$. The right-hand side is a convergent geometric series with common ratio $$r < 1$$, so by the comparison test $$\sum a_n$$ converges. If $$L > 1$$, then for sufficiently large $$n$$ we have $$a_n > 1$$, so the terms do not go to $$0$$, and by the term test ([Proposition 4](#prop4)) the series diverges.

</details>

In general, the comparison test may seem easier to apply to sequences, but the root test can handle more cases where convergence is to be determined. It is particularly powerful for series whose terms are given in the form of an $$n$$-th power; for example, in $$\sum (n/(2n+1))^n$$ we immediately read $$\sqrt[n]{a_n} = n/(2n+1) \to 1/2 < 1$$, so convergence follows. However, the root test, like the ratio test, gives no conclusion when $$L = 1$$, so in this case another method must be tried.

## Absolute Convergence and Alternating Series

For a positive-term series, the partial sums are monotone increasing, so leaning on [§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7), it is easy to determine convergence using [Theorem 6](#thm6) and related tools; on the other hand, positive-term series are harder to converge than series with mixed signs. In this section we examine series in which the signs alternate.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def9">**Definition 9**</ins> If $$\sum \lvert a_n\rvert$$ converges, we say that $$\sum a_n$$ is *absolutely convergent*.

</div>

An absolutely convergent series always converges. Since $$0 \leq a_n + \lvert a_n\rvert \leq 2\lvert a_n\rvert$$, by [Theorem 6](#thm6) the series $$\sum (a_n + \lvert a_n\rvert)$$ converges, and therefore $$\sum a_n = \sum (a_n + \lvert a_n\rvert) - \sum \lvert a_n\rvert$$ also converges. However, the converse does not hold. When positive and negative terms are mixed, the partial sums can cancel each other, so the original series may converge even though the series of absolute values diverges. The following test is the most basic tool guaranteeing such convergence.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop10">**Proposition 10 (Alternating Series Test)**</ins> If a positive sequence $$b_n \geq 0$$ is monotone decreasing and $$b_n \to 0$$, then the *alternating series* $$\sum (-1)^{n+1} b_n$$ formed from it converges. Moreover, the error between its sum $$S$$ and the partial sum $$s_N$$ is controlled by $$\lvert S - s_N\rvert \leq b_{N+1}$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The even-indexed partial sums

$$s_{2m} = (b_1 - b_2) + \cdots + (b_{2m-1} - b_{2m})$$

are increasing since each parentheses is nonnegative, and

$$s_{2m} = b_1 - (b_2 - b_3) - \cdots - b_{2m} \leq b_1$$

so they are bounded above. Hence $$s_{2m}$$ converges, and since $$s_{2m+1} = s_{2m} + b_{2m+1}$$ with $$b_{2m+1} \to 0$$, the odd-indexed partial sums also converge to the same limit $$S$$. Since the partial sums alternate around $$S$$, the claim about the error also follows.

</details>

A series that converges but is not absolutely convergent is called *conditionally convergent*. For example, the alternating harmonic series $$\sum (-1)^{n+1}/n = \ln 2$$ converges by the alternating series test, but taking absolute values gives the harmonic series which diverges, so it is conditionally convergent.
