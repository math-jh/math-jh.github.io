The properties of infinite sequences discussed in [§Limits of Sequences](/en/math/calculus/sequences) are especially important for our present purpose of studying *infinite series*.

## Partial Sums and Infinite Series

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> If the sequence of *partial sums* $$s_N = \sum_{n=1}^N a_n$$ formed from a sequence $$(a_n)$$ converges to $$S$$, we say that the *infinite series* $$\sum a_n$$ *converges* to $$S$$ and write $$\sum a_n = S$$. If $$(s_N)$$ diverges, we say the series *diverges*.

</div>

From this perspective, the convergence or divergence of a series reduces to the convergence or divergence of the sequence of partial sums $$s_N$$, so we may apply the tools of [§Limits of Sequences](/en/math/calculus/sequences) directly. The following are two particularly useful partial sums.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex2">**Example 2**</ins>

- The *geometric series* $$\sum r^n$$ is the infinite series whose partial sums are given by
    
    $$s_N = \sum_{n=0}^N r^n = \frac{1 - r^{N+1}}{1-r} \qquad (r \neq 1)$$
  
    This formula is verified by observing that $$(1-r)s_N = (1-r)(1 + r + \cdots + r^N) = 1 - r^{N+1}$$. If $$\lvert r\rvert < 1$$, then $$r^{N+1} \to 0$$, so the infinite sum converges to $$1/(1-r)$$. If $$\lvert r\rvert \geq 1$$, one can show that the sequence is either unbounded below or oscillatory, hence divergent.
- The *$$p$$-series* $$\sum_n 1/n^p$$ converges if $$p > 1$$ and diverges if $$p \leq 1$$. If $$p \leq 0$$, the general term $$1/n^p = n^{-p}$$ diverges to infinity, so in particular the partial sums are not bounded above and the series diverges. Consider the case $$p > 0$$. Grouping the terms of the partial sum in blocks of $$2^k$$ terms (for $$k=1,\dots,m$$), excluding the first term, and looking up to the $$2^{m+1}-1$$st term, we have
    
    $$s_{2^{m+1}-1} = 1 + \left(\frac{1}{2^p}+\frac{1}{3^p}\right) + \left(\frac{1}{4^p}+\cdots+\frac{1}{7^p}\right) + \cdots + \left(\frac{1}{(2^m)^p}+\cdots+\frac{1}{(2^{m+1}-1)^p}\right)$$
    
    The $$k$$th block contains $$2^k$$ terms, each of which is at most $$1/(2^k)^p$$, so
    
    $$s_{2^{m+1}-1} \leq \sum_{k=0}^m 2^k \cdot \frac{1}{2^{kp}} = \sum_{k=0}^m (2^{1-p})^k$$
    
    If $$p > 1$$, then $$2^{1-p} < 1$$, so the right-hand side is a partial sum of a geometric series and hence bounded; applying [§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7) shows that the $$p$$-series converges. On the other hand, if $$0 < p \leq 1$$, then each block contains at least the smallest term $$1/(2^{k+1}-1)^p$$, so

    $$\frac{2^k}{(2^{k+1}-1)^p} \geq \frac{2^k}{2^{k+1}-1} \geq \frac{1}{2}$$

    Therefore
    
    $$s_{2^{m+1}-1} \geq 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2} \geq 1 + \frac{m}{2}$$
    
    and we see that the partial sums diverge to infinity.

</div>

The viewpoint of [Definition 1](#def1), that a series reduces to the convergence problem of the sequence of its partial sums, implies that the properties of limits of sequences carry over directly to series. The most basic of these is linearity: the termwise sum and scalar multiple of two convergent series again converge. This follows from the linearity of limits at the level of partial sums ([§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop2)).

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop3">**Proposition 3 (Linearity of Series)**</ins> If $$\sum a_n = A$$ and $$\sum b_n = B$$ converge and $$\lambda, \mu$$ are constants, then $$\sum (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n)$$ also converges, and its sum is $$\lambda A + \mu B$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let the partial sums be $$s_N = \sum_{n=1}^N a_n$$ and $$t_N = \sum_{n=1}^N b_n$$. Since these are finite sums,

$$\begin{aligned}
\sum_{n=1}^N (\lambda a_n + \mu b_n) &= \lambda \sum_{n=1}^N a_n + \mu \sum_{n=1}^N b_n \\
&= \lambda s_N + \mu t_N
\end{aligned}$$

Since $$s_N \to A$$ and $$t_N \to B$$, the linearity of convergent sequences implies that the right-hand side converges to $$\lambda A + \mu B$$. Hence the partial sums on the left converge to the same value, giving the conclusion.

</details>

## Convergence Tests

For most series, computing the exact sum is too ambitious; often we need only determine convergence. The most basic yet powerful tool for this is the following proposition.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop4">**Proposition 4 (Term Test)**</ins> If the series $$\sum a_n$$ converges, then $$a_n \to 0$$. Consequently, if $$a_n \not\to 0$$, the series diverges.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $$s_N \to S$$, then also $$s_{N-1} \to S$$, so $$a_N = s_N - s_{N-1} \to S - S = 0$$.

</details>

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex5">**Example 5 (Harmonic Series)**</ins> The *harmonic series*

$$\sum \frac{1}{n} = 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \cdots$$

has terms tending to $$0$$, yet it diverges. This is the special case $$p=1$$ of [Example 2](#ex2); grouping the terms in blocks of $$2^k$$,

$$1 + \frac{1}{2} + \left(\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{4}\right) + \left(\frac{1}{5}+\cdots+\frac{1}{8}\right) + \cdots$$

we see that each block sums to at least $$1/2$$, so the partial sums grow without bound.

</div>

As the harmonic series shows, $$a_n \to 0$$ is a necessary but not sufficient condition for convergence. Meanwhile, for a series with positive terms the partial sums are monotone increasing, so by [§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7) the question of convergence reduces to checking whether the sums are bounded.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm6">**Theorem 6**</ins> For a series with positive terms, the following hold.

1. ([§Improper Integrals, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/calculus/improper_integrals#prop4)) If $$0 \leq a_n \leq b_n$$ and $$\sum b_n$$ converges, then $$\sum a_n$$ also converges.
2. (Ratio Test) If $$a_{n+1}/a_n \to L$$, then the series converges when $$L < 1$$ and diverges when $$L > 1$$.
3. ([§Improper Integrals, ⁋Example 8](/en/math/calculus/improper_integrals#ex8)) If $$f$$ is a positive decreasing continuous function and $$a_n = f(n)$$, then $$\sum a_n$$ and $$\int_1^\infty f$$ converge or diverge together.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

1: The partial sums $$(s_N)$$ of $$\sum a_n$$ are increasing and bounded above by $$\sum_n b_n$$, so they converge by the monotone convergence theorem ([§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7)).

2: If $$L < 1$$, choose $$r$$ with $$L < r < 1$$; then for sufficiently large $$n$$ we have $$a_{n+1} \leq r a_n$$, whence $$a_{N+k} \leq r^k a_N$$, and comparison with a convergent geometric series applies. If $$L > 1$$, the terms increase and do not tend to $$0$$, so the series diverges by the term test.

3: Since $$f$$ is decreasing, $$\int_k^{k+1} f \leq f(k) \leq \int_{k-1}^k f$$; summing over $$k$$ sandwiches the partial sums between integrals, so the two converge or diverge together.

</details>

Observe that the ratio test is inconclusive when $$L = 1$$. For instance, all $$p$$-series have $$L = 1$$, yet their convergence depends on $$p$$; for this reason it is important to choose the appropriate test when determining convergence.

The comparison test is the most powerful among the above, applicable to any series provided one can find a suitable inequality, but it carries the burden of bounding the terms directly against another series. In practice, the terms of a series are often clearly of the same order as those of a standard series, yet a clean inequality is hard to obtain. In such cases the following variant is convenient: it suffices to look at the limit of the ratio of the terms.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop7">**Proposition 7 (Limit Comparison Test)**</ins> For positive-term series $$\sum a_n$$ and $$\sum b_n$$, if $$a_n/b_n \to c$$ with $$0 < c < \infty$$, then the two series converge or diverge together.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

By the definition of the limit, for sufficiently large $$n$$ we can arrange

$$\frac{c}{2} < \frac{a_n}{b_n} < 2c$$

Multiplying by $$b_n > 0$$ gives

$$\begin{aligned}
\frac{c}{2}\,b_n &< a_n < 2c\,b_n
\end{aligned}$$

If $$\sum b_n$$ converges, then $$\sum 2c\,b_n$$ converges, so the right-hand inequality and the comparison test ([Theorem 6](#thm6)) imply that $$\sum a_n$$ converges. Conversely, if $$\sum a_n$$ converges, the left-hand inequality $$b_n < (2/c) a_n$$ and the comparison test imply that $$\sum b_n$$ converges. The divergence case follows by contraposition.

</details>

The utility of the limit comparison test lies in replacing terms that are mixtures of polynomials and powers with the dominant term governing their convergence. When the general term is a fraction, one takes as the standard $$b_n$$ the power obtained by keeping only the fastest-growing terms in numerator and denominator, and merely checks that the limit of $$a_n/b_n$$ lies in $$(0, \infty)$$; the labor of matching inequalities by hand disappears.

Another effective test is the *root test*.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8 (Root Test)**</ins> For a positive-term series $$\sum a_n$$, if $$\sqrt[n]{a_n} \to L$$, then the series converges when $$L < 1$$ and diverges when $$L > 1$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $$L < 1$$, we can choose $$r$$ with $$L < r < 1$$; then for sufficiently large $$n$$ we have $$\sqrt[n]{a_n} < r$$, i.e. $$a_n < r^n$$. The right-hand side is a convergent geometric series with ratio $$r < 1$$, so the comparison test implies that $$\sum a_n$$ converges. If $$L > 1$$, then $$a_n > 1$$ for sufficiently large $$n$$, so the terms do not tend to $$0$$ and the series diverges by the term test ([Proposition 4](#prop4)).

</details>

In general, the comparison tests may appear easier to apply to specific series, but the root test can decide convergence in more cases. It is especially powerful for series whose terms are given in the form of an $$n$$th power; for example, in $$\sum (n/(2n+1))^n$$ we read off immediately that $$\sqrt[n]{a_n} = n/(2n+1) \to 1/2 < 1$$, so convergence follows. However, like the ratio test, the root test gives no conclusion when $$L = 1$$, and one must try another method in that case.

## Absolute Convergence and Alternating Series

For positive-term series the partial sums are monotone increasing, so relying on [§Limits of Sequences, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/calculus/sequences#prop7) we can easily test convergence using [Theorem 6](#thm6) and related tools; at the same time, such series are harder to converge than series with mixed signs. In this section we examine series whose terms alternate in sign.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def9">**Definition 9**</ins> If $$\sum \lvert a_n\rvert$$ converges, we say that $$\sum a_n$$ is *absolutely convergent*.

</div>

Every absolutely convergent series converges. Indeed, since $$0 \leq a_n + \lvert a_n\rvert \leq 2\lvert a_n\rvert$$, [§Improper Integrals, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/calculus/improper_integrals#prop4) implies that $$\sum (a_n + \lvert a_n\rvert)$$ converges, and therefore $$\sum a_n = \sum (a_n + \lvert a_n\rvert) - \sum \lvert a_n\rvert$$ converges as well. The converse does not hold. When positive and negative terms are mixed, the partial sums can cancel each other, so the original series may converge even though the series of absolute values diverges. The following test is the most basic tool guaranteeing such convergence.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop10">**Proposition 10 (Alternating Series Test)**</ins> If a sequence of positive terms $$b_n \geq 0$$ is monotone decreasing and $$b_n \to 0$$, then the *alternating series* $$\sum (-1)^{n+1} b_n$$ formed from it converges. Moreover, the error between its sum $$S$$ and the partial sum $$s_N$$ is bounded by $$\lvert S - s_N\rvert \leq b_{N+1}$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The even partial sums

$$s_{2m} = (b_1 - b_2) + \cdots + (b_{2m-1} - b_{2m})$$

are increasing since each parenthesis is nonnegative, and

$$s_{2m} = b_1 - (b_2 - b_3) - \cdots - b_{2m} \leq b_1$$

so they are bounded above. Hence $$s_{2m}$$ converges, and since $$s_{2m+1} = s_{2m} + b_{2m+1}$$ with $$b_{2m+1} \to 0$$, the odd partial sums converge to the same limit $$S$$. Because the partial sums alternate around $$S$$, the error bound follows as well.

</details>

A series that converges but not absolutely is called *conditionally convergent*. For example, the alternating harmonic series $$\sum (-1)^{n+1}/n = \ln 2$$ converges by the alternating series test, but taking absolute values yields the harmonic series, which diverges; hence it is conditionally convergent.
