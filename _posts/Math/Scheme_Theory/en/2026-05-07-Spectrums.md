<div class="remark" markdown="1">

In every post in this category, "ring" means a commutative ring (with unity).

</div>

In this post we define the spectrum, the most fundamental object in algebraic geometry. The spectrum is a topological space carrying a suitable structure sheaf; in this post we first define it as a set, and then equip it with a topology. In the next post we will define the structure sheaf on $$\Spec A$$.

## $$\Spec A$$ as a Set

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> For a ring $$A$$, $$\Spec A$$ is the set of all prime ideals of $$A$$, called the *spectrum* of $$A$$.

</div>

Now suppose a ring homomorphism $$\phi: A \rightarrow B$$ is given. Then by [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Field of Fractions, ⁋Proposition 9](/en/math/algebraic_structures/field_of_fractions#prop9), the function

$$\Spec\phi: \Spec B \rightarrow \Spec A;\qquad \mathfrak{q}\mapsto \phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})$$

is well-defined.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop2">**Proposition 2**</ins> The map $$\Spec: \cRing^\op \rightarrow \Set$$ defined above is a functor.

</div>

That is, $$\Spec(\phi\circ\psi)=(\Spec\psi)\circ(\Spec\phi)$$ and $$\Spec(\id_A)=\id_{\Spec A}$$, and the proof is not difficult.

## $$\Spec A$$ as a Topological Space

We now equip $$\Spec A$$ with an appropriate topology.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> Fix a ring $$A$$ and its spectrum $$\Spec A$$. For any non-empty subset $$S$$ of $$A$$, we define the subset $$Z(S)$$ of $$\Spec A$$ by

$$Z(S)=\{\mathfrak{p}\in\Spec A\mid S\subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}$$

</div>

Several basic facts are immediate. First, $$Z$$ is inclusion-reversing: if two subsets $$S_1\subseteq S_2$$ of $$A$$ are given, then

$$Z(S_1)=\{\mathfrak{p}\in\Spec A\mid S_1\subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}\supseteq \{\mathfrak{p}\in\Spec A\mid S_2\subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}=Z(S_2)$$

This containment, however, need not be strict.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop4">**Proposition 4**</ins> Fix a ring $$A$$ and its spectrum $$\Spec A$$. For any subset $$S$$ of $$A$$ and the ideal $$(S)$$ generated by $$S$$, $$Z(S)=Z((S))$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $$S\subseteq (S)$$ trivially, the containment $$Z((S))\subseteq Z(S)$$ is immediate from the discussion above, so it suffices to prove the reverse direction. Let $$\mathfrak{p}$$ be any element of $$Z(S)$$; that is, $$S\subseteq \mathfrak{p}$$. Since the ideal generated by a subset $$\mathfrak{p}$$ of $$A$$ that is itself an ideal is $$\mathfrak{p}$$ itself, we obtain $$(S)\subseteq (\mathfrak{p})=\mathfrak{p}$$.

</details>

Moreover, even for two *ideals* $$\mathfrak{a}_1\subseteq \mathfrak{a}_2$$ of $$A$$, the containment $$Z(\mathfrak{a}_1)\supseteq Z(\mathfrak{a}_2)$$ may in general be an equality.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop5">**Proposition 5**</ins> Fix a ring $$A$$ and its spectrum $$\Spec A$$. For any ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of $$A$$ and its radical $$\sqrt{\mathfrak{a}}$$, $$Z(\mathfrak{a})=Z(\sqrt{\mathfrak{a}})$$ holds.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Again, the containment $$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq \sqrt{\mathfrak{a}}$$ immediately yields $$Z(\sqrt{\mathfrak{a}})\subseteq Z(\mathfrak{a})$$. Conversely, for any $$\mathfrak{p}\in Z(\mathfrak{a})$$, applying [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Properties of Localization, ⁋Corollary 8](/en/math/commutative_algebra/properties_of_localization#cor8) gives

$$\sqrt{\mathfrak{a}}=\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize$\mathfrak{q}$ a prime containing $\mathfrak{a}$}\mathfrak{q}\subseteq \mathfrak{p}$$

so $$\mathfrak{p}\in Z(\sqrt{\mathfrak{a}})$$.

</details>

The crucial fact in defining a topology on $$\Spec A$$ is the following lemma.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem6">**Lemma 6**</ins> The following hold.

1. For any ideals $$\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{b}$$ of $$A$$, $$Z(\mathfrak{ab})=Z(\mathfrak{a})\cup Z(\mathfrak{b})$$.
2. For any family $$\{\mathfrak{a}_i\}$$ of ideals of $$A$$, $$Z(\sum \mathfrak{a}_i)=\bigcap Z(\mathfrak{a}_i)$$.
3. For any ideals $$\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{b}$$ of $$A$$, $$Z(\mathfrak{a})\subseteq Z(\mathfrak{b})\iff \sqrt{\mathfrak{a}}\supseteq \sqrt{\mathfrak{b}}$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. Any prime ideal $$\mathfrak{p}$$ that contains $$\mathfrak{a}$$ or $$\mathfrak{b}$$ clearly also contains the smaller ideal $$\mathfrak{ab}$$, so it suffices to show the reverse containment. Assume $$\mathfrak{p}\supset \mathfrak{ab}$$. If $$\mathfrak{p}\not\supseteq \mathfrak{b}$$, we can find an element $$b\in\mathfrak{b}$$ with $$b\not\in \mathfrak{p}$$. Then for any $$a\in \mathfrak{a}$$, $$ab\in \mathfrak{ab}\subseteq \mathfrak{p}$$, and since $$b\not\in \mathfrak{p}$$ we must have $$a\in \mathfrak{p}$$, so $$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq \mathfrak{p}$$.
2. This is immediate, since $$\sum \mathfrak{a}_i$$ is by definition the smallest ideal containing each $$\mathfrak{a}_i$$.
3. [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Properties of Localization, ⁋Corollary 8](/en/math/commutative_algebra/properties_of_localization#cor8).

</details>

Then by [\[Topology\] §Interior, Closure, Boundary, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/topology/other_concepts#prop2), there exists a unique topology $$\mathcal{T}$$ having the sets $$Z(\mathfrak{a})$$ as its closed sets, making $$\Spec A$$ into a topological space.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def7">**Definition 7**</ins> The topology on $$\Spec A$$ defined above is called the *Zariski topology*.

</div>

The Zariski topology is not the kind of topology one usually regards as nice. For example, for any integral domain $$A$$, $$(0)$$ is itself a prime ideal, and the only non-empty subset of $$A$$ contained in $$(0)$$ is $$(0)$$ itself. So the only closed set containing $$(0)\in\Spec A$$ is $$Z(0)=\Spec A$$, and hence unless $$A$$ is a field the singleton $$\{(0)\}$$ is not closed. In particular, the Zariski topology is not in general Hausdorff. As the posts in this category develop, however, we will discover familiar geometric notions in this structure.

We saw in [Proposition 2](#prop2) that $$\Spec$$ can be regarded as a functor $$\Spec: \cRing^\op \rightarrow \Set$$. Moreover, $$\Spec$$ is also a functor from $$\cRing^\op$$ to $$\Top$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8**</ins> When the spectrum $$\Spec A$$ of a ring $$A$$ is equipped with the topology of [Definition 7](#def7), the functor $$\Spec: \cRing^\op \rightarrow \Top$$ from [Proposition 2](#prop2) is a functor.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

What remains to show beyond [Proposition 2](#prop2) is that for any ring homomorphism $$\phi: A \rightarrow B$$, the map $$\Spec \phi: \Spec B \rightarrow \Spec A$$ is continuous. It therefore suffices to show that the preimage under $$\Spec\phi$$ of any closed subset of $$\Spec A$$ is closed in $$\Spec B$$. ([\[Topology\] §Interior, Closure, Boundary, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/topology/other_concepts#prop2))

Now, any closed subset of $$\Spec A$$ has the form $$Z(\mathfrak{a})$$ and any closed subset of $$\Spec B$$ has the form $$Z(\mathfrak{b})$$, so it suffices to show that for every ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ of $$A$$ there exists an ideal $$\mathfrak{b}$$ of $$B$$ with

$$(\Spec\phi)^{-1}(Z(\mathfrak{a}))=Z(\mathfrak{b})$$

Our claim is that

$$(\Spec\phi)^{-1}(Z(\mathfrak{a}))=Z(\phi(\mathfrak{a}))$$

so the ideal generated by $$\phi(\mathfrak{a})$$ satisfies the requirement and the proof is complete.

First, suppose $$\mathfrak{q}\in\Spec B$$ lies in the left-hand side, i.e. $$(\Spec\phi)(\mathfrak{q})=\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})\in Z(\mathfrak{a})$$. Then from $$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq \phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})$$ we get $$\phi(\mathfrak{a})\subseteq \mathfrak{q}$$, so $$\mathfrak{q}\in Z(\phi(\mathfrak{a}))$$.

Conversely, suppose $$\mathfrak{q}\in\Spec B$$ lies in the right-hand side. Then $$\phi(\mathfrak{a})\subseteq \mathfrak{q}$$ yields the containment

$$\mathfrak{a}\subseteq \phi^{-1}(\phi(\mathfrak{a}))\subseteq\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})=(\Spec\phi)(\mathfrak{q})$$

which is precisely $$(\Spec\phi)(\mathfrak{q})\in Z(\mathfrak{a})$$, i.e. $$\mathfrak{q}\in (\Spec\phi)^{-1}(Z(\mathfrak{a}))$$.

</details>

Two important ring homomorphisms we will use are the quotient $$\pi:A \rightarrow A/\mathfrak{a}$$ and the localization $$\epsilon: A \rightarrow S^{-1}A$$. ([\[Commutative Algebra\] §Basic Notions, ⁋Proposition 11](/en/math/commutative_algebra/basic_notions#prop11) and [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Localization, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/commutative_algebra/localization#prop8))

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop9">**Proposition 9**</ins> For the maps $$\pi:A \rightarrow A/\mathfrak{a}$$ and $$\epsilon: A \rightarrow S^{-1}A$$ defined above, the following hold.

1. Both $$\Spec\pi$$ and $$\Spec\epsilon$$ are injective, and each defines a homeomorphism onto its image.
2. The image of $$\Spec\pi$$ in $$\Spec A$$ is closed.
3. If $$S=\{1,f,f^2,\ldots\}$$ for some $$f\in A$$, then the image of $$\Spec \epsilon$$ in $$\Spec A$$ is open.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The first claim is a consequence of the two propositions cited above, [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Basic Notions, ⁋Proposition 11](/en/math/commutative_algebra/basic_notions#prop11) and [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Localization, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/commutative_algebra/localization#prop8). For the second, the image of $$\Spec\pi$$ in $$\Spec A$$ is exactly $$Z(\mathfrak{a})$$. Finally, for the third, the elements of $$\Spec\epsilon$$ are the prime ideals not containing $$f$$, and these can be written as $$\Spec A\setminus Z(f)$$.

</details>

It is clear that $$\Spec A$$ as a topological space admits a basis, but we would like this basis to bear some relation to the algebraic structure of $$A$$. We make the following definition.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def10">**Definition 10**</ins> For any element $$f\in A$$ of a ring $$A$$, we write $$D(f)$$ for the complement $$\Spec A\setminus Z(f)$$ of $$Z(f)$$ in $$\Spec A$$. An open set of this form is called a *principal open set*.

</div>

Now for any $$f\in A$$, set $$S_f=\{1,f,f^2,\ldots\}$$ and let $$A_f=S_f^{-1}A$$. Then by [Proposition 9](#prop9), the image of $$\Spec A_f$$ in $$\Spec A$$ under $$\Spec \epsilon$$ is open. That this open set coincides with $$D(f)$$ as a set is immediate from

$$\mathfrak{p}\not\in Z(f)\iff (f)\not\subseteq \mathfrak{p} \iff f\not\in \mathfrak{p}\iff f^k\not\in \mathfrak{p}\text{ for all $k\geq 0$}\iff S_f\cap \mathfrak{p}=\emptyset$$

together with [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Localization, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/commutative_algebra/localization#prop8). Moreover, that $$D(f)$$ carries the same topology as $$\Spec A_f$$ also follows from the fact that the correspondence in [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Localization, ⁋Proposition 8](/en/math/commutative_algebra/localization#prop8) preserves containment.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem11">**Lemma 11**</ins> The collection of principal open sets is a basis for $$\Spec A$$. ([\[Topology\] §Topological Bases, ⁋Definition 1](/en/math/topology/topological_bases#def1))

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For any open set $$\Spec A \setminus Z(S)$$, using the second part of [Lemma 6](#lem6) we compute

$$\Spec A\setminus Z(S)=\Spec A\setminus Z\left(\sum_{f\in S} (f)\right)=\Spec A\setminus\left(\bigcap_{f\in S}Z(f)\right)=\bigcup_{f\in S} (\Spec A\setminus Z(f))=\bigcup_{f\in S} D(f)$$

</details>

A similar computation shows that $$D(fg)=D(f)\cap D(g)$$. This uses the first part of [Lemma 6](#lem6), so in general it cannot be extended to infinite index sets.

Separately, one can show that $$\Spec A$$ always satisfies the condition of [\[Topology\] §Compact Spaces, ⁋Definition 1](/en/math/topology/compact_spaces#def1). However, many of the properties one expects of a compact space typically require the Hausdorff condition as well ([\[Topology\] §Compact Spaces, §§Compact Hausdorff Spaces](/en/math/topology/compact_spaces#compact-hausdorff-spaces)), and since the Zariski topology is in general not Hausdorff, we will instead call it *quasi-compact*.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem12">**Lemma 12**</ins> $$\Spec A$$ is quasi-compact as a topological space.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

By [Lemma 11](#lem11), it suffices to show that whenever a family $$\{D(f_i)\}_{i\in I}$$ of principal open sets covers $$\Spec A$$, there exists a finite subset $$J$$ of $$I$$ for which $$\Spec A=\bigcup_{j\in J} D(f_j)$$ still holds.

From the assumption $$\Spec A=\bigcup_{i\in I} D(f_i)$$ we have

$$\emptyset=\Spec A\setminus\bigcup_{i\in I} D(f_i)=\bigcap_{i\in I}(\Spec A\setminus D(f_i))=\bigcap_{i\in I} Z(f_i)$$

On the other hand, by [Lemma 6](#lem6),

$$\emptyset=\bigcap_{i\in I} Z(f_i)=Z\left(\sum_{i\in I}(f_i)\right)$$

and if $$\sum (f_i)$$ were a proper ideal of $$A$$, any maximal ideal containing it would be an element of $$\bigcap_{i\in I} Z(f_i)$$, so the equation above is equivalent to $$\sum(f_i)=(1)$$. Since $$1\in\sum(f_i)$$, there exist a finite subset $$J\subseteq I$$ and a family $$(a_j)_{j\in J}$$ of elements of $$A$$ with $$\sum_{j\in J} a_jf_j=1$$. That is,

$$Z\left(\sum_{j\in J} (f_j)\right)=\emptyset$$

and reversing the computations above gives the desired conclusion.

</details>


## Galois Correspondence

By definition, $$Z$$ is a function that takes an ideal of $$A$$ and returns a closed subset of $$\Spec A$$. Conversely, we define the following.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def13">**Definition 13**</ins> For any subset $$T\subseteq \Spec A$$, set

$$I(T)=\{f\in A\mid\text{$f\in \mathfrak{p}$ for all $\mathfrak{p}\in T$}\}=\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize$\mathfrak{p}$ a prime in $T$} \mathfrak{p}$$

</div>

Then $$I(T)$$ is an intersection of ideals and is therefore itself an ideal. Moreover, if $$T_1\subseteq T_2$$, then trivially $$I(T_2)\subseteq I(T_1)$$.

Combining [Definition 3](#def3) with [Definition 12](#def12), we have defined two maps between the ordered sets $$\mathcal{P}(A)$$ and $$\mathcal{P}(\Spec A)$$:

$$Z: \mathcal{P}(A) \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(\Spec A);\quad S\mapsto Z(S),\qquad I: \mathcal{P}(\Spec A) \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(A);\quad T\mapsto I(T)$$

For any $$S\in \mathcal{P}(A)$$ and any $$T\in \mathcal{P}(\Spec A)$$,

$$T\subseteq Z(S)\iff\text{$\mathfrak{p}\in Z(S)$ for all $\mathfrak{p}\in T$}\iff\text{$f\in \mathfrak{p}$ for all $f\in S$ and all $\mathfrak{p}\in T$}\iff S\subseteq I(T)$$

so $$(Z, I)$$ defines an antitone Galois connection between $$\mathcal{P}(A)$$ and $$\mathcal{P}(\Spec A)$$. ([\[Set Theory\] §Filters and Ideals, Galois Connection, ⁋Definition 6](/en/math/set_theory/filter_and_ideal#def6)) Consequently,

$$Z(I(Z(S)))=Z(S),\qquad I(Z(I(T)))=I(T)$$

hold for every $$S\in \mathcal{P}(A)$$ and every $$T\in \mathcal{P}(\Spec A)$$.

Now consider the two closure operators $$IZ: \mathcal{P}(A) \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(A)$$ and $$ZI: \mathcal{P}(\Spec A) \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(\Spec A)$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop14">**Proposition 14**</ins> For the closure operators $$IZ: \mathcal{P}(A) \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(A)$$ and $$ZI: \mathcal{P}(\Spec A) \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(\Spec A)$$, the following hold.

1. $IZ(S)=\sqrt{(S)}$
2. $ZI(T)=\cl(T)$

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. This is immediate from
    
    $$I(Z(S))=I(Z((S)))=\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize$\mathfrak{p}$ a prime in $Z((S))$} \mathfrak{p}=\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize$\mathfrak{p}$ a prime containing $(S)$} \mathfrak{p}=\sqrt{(S)}$$

2. This is immediate from
    
    $$\cl(T)
    =\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize $Z(S)\supseteq T$} Z(S)
    =\bigcap_\text{\scriptsize $S\subseteq I(T)$} Z(S)
    =Z\left(\sum_\text{\scriptsize $S\subseteq I(T)$}(S)\right)
    =Z(I(T))$$

</details>

In particular, we obtain the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm15">**Theorem 15**</ins> There is a Galois correspondence between the radical ideals of a ring $$A$$ and the closed subsets of $$\Spec A$$.

</div>

A potentially confusing point in this correspondence is that since every prime ideal of $$A$$ is a radical ideal, the collection of radical ideals of a ring $$A$$ always (as a set) contains $$\Spec A$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop16">**Proposition 16**</ins> There is a Galois correspondence between the prime ideals of a ring $$A$$ and the irreducible closed subsets of $$\Spec A$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

We must show: for any prime ideal $$\mathfrak{p}$$, $$Z(\mathfrak{p})$$ is irreducible; and for any irreducible closed subset $$Y$$, $$I(Y)$$ is a prime ideal.

First, suppose $$Z(\mathfrak{p})$$ is not irreducible. Then there exist two proper closed subsets $$Z(\mathfrak{a}), Z(\mathfrak{b})$$ of $$Z(\mathfrak{p})$$ with $$Z(\mathfrak{p})=Z(\mathfrak{a})\cup Z(\mathfrak{b})$$. ([\[Topology\] §Dimension, ⁋Definition 6](/en/math/topology/dimension#def6)) Now

$$Z(\mathfrak{p})=Z(\mathfrak{a})\cup Z(\mathfrak{b})=Z(\mathfrak{ab})\iff \mathfrak{p}=\sqrt{\mathfrak{p}}=\sqrt{\mathfrak{ab}}=\sqrt{\mathfrak{a}\cap \mathfrak{b}}$$

while

$$\sqrt{\mathfrak{a}\cap \mathfrak{b}}\supseteq \mathfrak{a}\cap \mathfrak{b}\supseteq \mathfrak{a}\mathfrak{b}$$

Combining the two equations we must have $$\mathfrak{p}\supseteq \mathfrak{a}$$ or $$\mathfrak{p}\supseteq \mathfrak{b}$$, hence $$Z(\mathfrak{p})=Z(\mathfrak{a})$$ or $$Z(\mathfrak{p})=Z(\mathfrak{b})$$, a contradiction.

Conversely, we must show that $$I(Y)$$ is a prime ideal for any irreducible closed subset $$Y$$. Since $$Y$$ is closed, by [Proposition 15](#prop15) and [Proposition 5](#prop5) there exists a radical ideal $$\mathfrak{a}$$ with $$Y=V(\mathfrak{a})$$. It then suffices to show that $$\mathfrak{a}=IZ(\mathfrak{a})=I(Y)$$ is prime.

To this end, suppose $$fg\in \mathfrak{a}$$ and consider the two non-empty open sets $$D(f), D(g)$$ of $$\Spec A$$. Then

$$(D(f)\cap Y)\cap (D(g)\cap Y)=D(f)\cap D(g)\cap Y=D(fg)\cap Y$$

must be empty. But $$D(f)\cap Y$$ and $$D(g)\cap Y$$ are two open subsets of the irreducible closed set $$Y$$, so for the above to hold one of them must be empty. ([\[Topology\] §Dimension, ⁋Proposition 7](/en/math/topology/dimension#prop7)) From this we conclude $$f\in \mathfrak{a}$$ or $$g\in \mathfrak{a}$$, hence $$\mathfrak{a}$$ is a prime ideal.

</details>

In particular, if a prime ideal $$\mathfrak{p}$$ has no prime ideal $$\mathfrak{q}\subsetneq \mathfrak{p}$$ — that is, if $$\mathfrak{p}$$ is a *minimal* prime ideal — then $$Z(\mathfrak{p})$$ is the largest among the irreducible components, i.e. an irreducible component itself. Hence we obtain the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="cor17">**Corollary 17**</ins> There is a Galois correspondence between the minimal prime ideals of a ring $$A$$ and the irreducible components of $$\Spec A$$.

</div>

In particular, the spectrum of an integral domain — the example considered above — is irreducible.

## Classical Algebraic Geometry

Before defining the structure sheaf in the next post, it is helpful to briefly review classical algebraic geometry. For the remainder we fix an algebraically closed field $$\mathbb{K}$$. Then the (classical) *affine $$n$$-space* over $$\mathbb{K}$$ is the set

$$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n=\{(x_1,\ldots, x_n)\mid x_1,\ldots, x_n\in \mathbb{K}\}$$

Each element $$x=(x_1,\ldots, x_n)$$ of $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n$$ is called a *point*, and each $$x_i$$ is called the *$$i$$-th coordinate* of $$x$$.

Now consider the polynomial ring $$A=\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots,\x_n]$$ with coefficients in $$\mathbb{K}$$. For any $$x=(x_1,\ldots, x_n)\in \mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n$$ we may define the ideal $$\mathfrak{m}_x$$ of $$A$$ by

$$\mathfrak{m}_x=(\x_1-x_1,\ldots, \x_n-x_n)\subseteq \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$

and in [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Nullstellensatz](/en/math/commutative_algebra/nullstellensatz) we showed that these are precisely *all* maximal ideals of $$A=\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$. Hence, writing $$\mSpec A$$ for the collection of maximal ideals of $$A$$, we have $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n= \mSpec A$$.

Moreover, any $$f\in A$$ may be regarded as a function from $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n$$ to $$\mathbb{K}$$ via

$$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{K};\qquad (x_1,\ldots, x_n)\mapsto f(x_1,\ldots, x_n)$$

Then, analogously to [Definition 3](#def3), for any set $$S$$ of $$A$$ we define

$$Z(S)=\{(x_1,\ldots, x_n)\mid \text{$f(x_1,\ldots, x_n)=0$ for all $f\in S$}\}$$

so that $$Z(S)$$ is the common zero set on $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n$$ of the family of functions $$S$$, and a computation analogous to [Lemma 6](#lem6) endows $$\mSpec A$$ with a topology whose closed sets are the sets $$Z(S)$$.

On the other hand, in analogy with [Definition 12](#def12), for a subset $$T$$ of $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n$$, setting

$$I(T)=\{f\in A\mid \text{$f(x_1,\ldots, x_n)=0$ for all $x\in T$}\}$$

we see, as in [Theorem 14](#thm14), that $$I$$ and $$Z$$ define an antitone Galois connection. The analogue of [Proposition 13](#prop13) for $$\mSpec A$$ follows from [\[Commutative Algebra\] §Nullstellensatz, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/commutative_algebra/nullstellensatz#prop4), since $$\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$ is Jacobson.

We saw above that elements of $$A$$ — i.e. polynomials — behave like functions on $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n$$. More generally, on a principal open set $$D(g)$$, $$f/g$$ is also well-defined, and in algebraic geometry we are interested in such functions. Functions of this form are not in general well-defined on the whole of $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\text{classical}}^n$$, and for that reason we will examine functions that, at every point, can be written in the form $$f/g$$ on some suitable neighborhood.
