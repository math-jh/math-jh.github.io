## Definition of Schemes

Now we can define what a scheme is.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> A locally ringed space $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$ is called a *scheme* if for every $$x\in X$$, there exists an open neighborhood $$U$$ of $$x$$ such that $$(U, \mathscr{O}_X\vert_U)$$ is an affine scheme. The notions of morphisms and isomorphisms between schemes are defined as those of locally ringed spaces.

</div>

Given any point $$x\in X$$ of a scheme $$X$$, choose an affine open neighborhood $$U$$ of $$x$$. Then by definition there exists a suitable ring $$A$$ such that $$(U, \mathscr{O}_X\vert_U)\cong (\Spec A, \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A})$$ as locally ringed spaces. In particular, letting $$\mathfrak{p}_x$$ denote the point of $$\Spec A$$ corresponding to $$x\in U$$ via the above isomorphism, we know that

$$\mathscr{O}_{X,x}=\varinjlim_{V\ni x} \mathscr{O}_X(V)=\varinjlim_{U\supseteq V\ni x} \mathscr{O}_X(V)=\mathscr{O}_{U, x}\cong \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A, \mathfrak{p}_x}\tag{$\ast$}$$

In general, $$(U, \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A}\vert_U)$$ obtained by restricting an affine scheme $$(\Spec A, \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A})$$ to an *arbitrary* open subset is not always an affine scheme. ([Example 6](#ex6)) However, a principal open subset $$D(f)$$ of an affine scheme $$\Spec A$$ is always an affine scheme via the isomorphism

$$(D(f), \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A}\vert_{D(f)})\cong (\Spec A_f, \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f})\tag{$\ast\ast$}$$

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem2">**Lemma 2**</ins> For an affine scheme $$\Spec A$$ and $$f\in A$$, the set $$D(f)$$ is always an affine scheme via the above isomorphism ($$\ast\ast$$).

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

From the third result of [§Spectra, ⁋Proposition 9](/en/math/scheme_theory/spectrums#prop9), we know that the injective continuous map $$\Spec\epsilon: \Spec A_f \rightarrow A$$ between topological spaces gives a homeomorphism $$D(f)\cong \Spec A_f$$, but to justify the above isomorphism we must justify this as a morphism of schemes.

First, the ring homomorphism $$\epsilon: A \rightarrow A_f$$ induces a scheme morphism

$$(\Spec \epsilon, (\Spec\epsilon)^\sharp): (\Spec A_f, \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f}) \rightarrow (\Spec A, \mathscr{O}_A)$$

and the homeomorphism between $$D(f)$$ and $$\Spec A_f$$ arises from the following decomposition, considering the canonical inclusion $$\iota: D(f)\hookrightarrow \Spec A$$:

![decomposition](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Varieties/Schemes-1.png){:style="width:11em" class="invert" .align-center}

Now, for the above isomorphism ($$\ast\ast$$) to be an isomorphism of schemes, we must define the corresponding morphism $$(\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})^\sharp$$ in $$\Sh(D(f), \cRing)$$ for the homeomorphism $$\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)}$$. Then by [[Topology] §Sheaves, ⁋Example 12](/en/math/topology/sheaves#ex12),

defining

$$(\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})^\sharp: \mathscr{O}_{D(f)} \rightarrow (\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})_\ast \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f}$$

is exactly the same as defining

$$(\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})^{\sharp}: \iota^{-1}\mathscr{O}_{\Spec A} \rightarrow (\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})_\ast \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f}$$

and again by [[Topology] §Sheaves, ⁋Lemma 11](/en/math/topology/sheaves#lem11),

$$\begin{aligned}\Hom_{\Sh(D(f), \cRing)}(\iota^{-1}\mathscr{O}_{\Spec A}, (\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})_\ast\mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f})&\cong \Hom_{\Sh(\Spec A, \cRing)}(\mathscr{O}_{\Spec A}, \iota_\ast(\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})_\ast \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f})\\&\cong \Hom_{\Sh(\Spec A, \cRing)}(\mathscr{O}_{\Spec A}, (\iota\circ\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})_\ast \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f})\\&=\Hom_{\Sh(\Spec A, \cRing)}(\mathscr{O}_{\Spec A}, (\Spec\epsilon)_\ast \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f})\end{aligned}$$

so we can define $$(\Spec\epsilon\vert^{D(f)})^\sharp$$ via $$(\Spec\epsilon)^\sharp:\mathscr{O}_{\Spec A} \rightarrow (\Spec \epsilon)_\ast \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A_f}$$, and that this is an isomorphism follows essentially from the fact that $$\Spec A_f$$ is an open subset of $$\Spec A$$, as can be seen by following the proof of that lemma.

</details>

In particular, by definition a scheme can be covered by affine schemes, and since these have a base consisting of principal open sets, any scheme has a base of affine open subsets.

The following lemma shows, by a similar argument, that any open subset of a scheme is always a scheme.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem3">**Lemma 3**</ins> Let $$U$$ be an arbitrary open subset of a scheme $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$. Then $$(U, \mathscr{O}_X\vert_U)$$ is also a scheme.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $$x$$ be an arbitrary point of the open subset $$U$$. Since $$X$$ is a scheme, there exist a suitable open neighborhood $$V$$ of $$x$$ (in $$X$$) and a ring $$A$$ such that $$(V, \mathscr{O}_X\vert_V)\cong(\Spec A, \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A})$$. Also, since $$U$$ is open, $$U\cap V$$ is an open subset of $$V$$. Now by [§Spectra, ⁋Lemma 11](/en/math/scheme_theory/spectrums#lem11), there exist $$f_i\in A$$ satisfying

$$U\cap V=\bigcup_{i\in I} D(f_i)\qquad\text{in $\Spec A$}$$

so that $$x\in D(f_i)$$ for some $$i$$. Since $$(D(f_i), \mathscr{O}_X\vert_{D(f_i)})$$ is an affine scheme and $$x\in D(f_i)\subseteq V$$, we obtain the desired result.

</details>

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def4">**Definition 4**</ins> For an arbitrary open subset $$U$$ of a scheme $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$, we call the scheme $$(U, \mathscr{O}_X\vert_U)$$ an *open subscheme* of $$X$$.

</div>

## Algebraic Functions Defined on Schemes

In [§Affine Schemes, §§Algebraic Functions on $$\Spec A$$](/en/math/scheme_theory/affine_schemes#algebraic-functions-on-spec-a), we decided to think of the value of a function $$f\in A$$ at an arbitrary point $$\mathfrak{p}$$ as the image of $$f$$ in $$A/\mathfrak{p}$$. Applying this directly to a general scheme $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$ is somewhat tricky, because the above procedure is not clear in $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$, and moreover, even if we repeat the above discussion using an affine open neighborhood of $$x\in X$$, it is not obvious that this does not depend on the choice of affine open neighborhood.

To resolve this, we use a small trick and define the following.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def5">**Definition 5**</ins> Consider a scheme $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$, an arbitrary point $$x\in X$$, and the local ring $$(\mathscr{O}_{X, x}, \mathfrak{m}_x)$$. Then the *residue field* of $$X$$ at $$x$$ is defined as $$\mathscr{O}_{X,x}/\mathfrak{m}_x$$, and we denote this by $$\kappa(x)$$. For a function $$f\in \mathscr{O}_X(U)$$ defined on an open subset $$U$$ containing the point $$x$$, the *value* of $$f$$ at $$x$$ is defined as the image of $$f$$ in $$\kappa(x)$$.

</div>

Choose an arbitrary point $$x\in X$$ of a scheme $$X$$ and an affine open neighborhood $$(U, \mathscr{O}_X\vert_U)\cong(\Spec A, \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A})$$ of $$x$$. Then, writing $$\mathfrak{p}_x$$ for the point of $$\Spec A$$ corresponding to $$x\in U$$, we checked from the isomorphism ($$\ast$$) above that $$\mathscr{O}_{X,x}\cong \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A, \mathfrak{p}_x}$$, and then from [§Affine Schemes, ⁋Lemma 8](/en/math/scheme_theory/affine_schemes#lem8) we obtain the isomorphism

$$\mathscr{O}_{X,x}\cong \mathscr{O}_{\Spec A, \mathfrak{p}_x}\cong A_{\mathfrak{p}_x}$$

and in particular this isomorphism corresponds the maximal ideal $$\mathfrak{p}_xA_{\mathfrak{p}_x}$$ of the local ring $$A_{\mathfrak{p}_x}$$ to $$\mathfrak{m}_x$$. Therefore, thinking of the definition in [[Commutative Algebra] §Localization, ⁋Definition 10](/en/math/commutative_algebra/localization#def10), we obtain

$$\kappa(x)=\mathscr{O}_{X,x}/\mathfrak{m}_x\cong A_{\mathfrak{p}_x}/\mathfrak{p}_xA_{\mathfrak{p}_x}=\kappa(\mathfrak{p}_x)$$

On the other hand, since localization and quotient commute ([[Commutative Algebra] §Properties of Localization, ⁋Proposition 2](/en/math/commutative_algebra/properties_of_localization#prop2)), we obtain

$$\kappa(x)\cong A_{\mathfrak{p}_x}/\mathfrak{p}_xA_{\mathfrak{p}_x}\cong \Frac(A/\mathfrak{p}_x)$$

and thus we see that [Definition 5](#def5) generalizes the notion of function values on affine schemes well. Then from this expression, for any $$f\in \mathscr{O}_X(X)$$, let us define

$$X_f=\{x\in X\mid\text{$f_x\neq 0$ in $\kappa(x)$}\}=\{x\in X\mid f_x\not\in \mathfrak{m}_x\}$$

Then for any $$x\in X_f$$, choosing an affine open neighborhood $$U=\Spec A$$ containing $$x$$, we know from the above discussion that $$U\cap X_f$$ equals the open subset $$D(f\vert_{U\cap X_f})$$, and thus by [§Spectra, ⁋Lemma 11](/en/math/scheme_theory/spectrums#lem11) we see that $$X_f$$ is an open subset of $$X$$.

Meanwhile, at a point $$x\in X$$, a function $$f$$ can be examined not only by its value but also by using its germ. Let us define the following.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def6">**Definition 6**</ins> For a scheme $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$ and $$f\in \mathscr{O}_X(X)$$, the *support* of $$f$$ is given by the formula

$$\supp(f)=\{x\in X\mid f_x\neq 0\text{ in $\mathscr{O}_{X,x}$}\}$$

Here $$f_x$$ denotes the stalk of $$f$$ at $$x$$.

</div>

Then if the stalk of $$f$$ at $$x\in X$$ is $$0$$, we can choose a suitable open neighborhood $$U=\Spec A$$ of $$x$$ so that $$f$$ is identically $$0$$ on $$U$$, and thus we see that $$\supp(f)$$ is a closed subset of $$X$$. On the other hand, if $$f_x=0$$ in $$\mathscr{O}_{X,x}$$, then it is obvious that $$f_x$$ is $$0$$ in $$\kappa(x)$$, so the inclusion

$$X\setminus \supp(f)\subseteq X\setminus X_f\iff X_f\subseteq \supp(f)$$

holds.

## Examples of Schemes

For convenience, from now on we shall denote a scheme by simply $$X$$ instead of $$(X, \mathscr{O}_X)$$, omitting the structure sheaf, and denote a scheme morphism by simply $$\varphi$$ instead of $$(\varphi, \varphi^\sharp)$$.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex7">**Example 7**</ins> In [§Spectra, §§Classical algebraic geometry](/en/math/scheme_theory/spectrums#classical-algebraic-geometry), we defined the (classical) affine $$n$$-space as

$$Z(\x(\x-1))=\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\mathrm{classical}}^n=\{(x_1,\ldots, x_n)\mid x_i\in \mathbb{K}\}=\mSpec \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$

Generalizing this, we shall from now on define the affine $$n$$-space over $$\mathbb{K}$$ as

$$\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^n=\Spec \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$

More generally, replacing the field $$\mathbb{K}$$ by an arbitrary ring $$A$$, we obtain the affine $$n$$-space over $$A$$

$$\mathbb{A}_A^n=\Spec A[\x_1,\ldots, \x_n]$$

Moving from the maximal spectrum $$\mSpec A$$ to $$\Spec A$$ not only makes algebra easier, but is also geometrically meaningful. Now each point of $$\mathbb{A}^n$$ means not only the points of the $$n$$-space $$\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{K},\mathrm{classical}}^n$$ in the traditional sense, but also treats the closed subsets appearing as zero loci of functions on this space as points.

</div>

Earlier we mentioned that an open subset of an affine scheme need not be an affine scheme; the following example shows this.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex8">**Example 8**</ins> Consider the affine plane $$\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2=\Spec \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2]$$. Then

$$\{(0,0)\}=Z(\x_1)\cap Z(\x_2)$$

so $$\{(0,0)\}$$ is a closed set, and therefore

$$U=\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2\setminus\{0\}=D(\x_1)\cup D(\x_2)$$

is an open set. Meanwhile, functions defined on $$D(\x_1)$$ and $$D(\x_2)$$ are of the forms

$$\mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2}(D(\x_1))\cong \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2]_{\x_1}=\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2, \x_1^{-1}],\qquad \mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2}(D(\x_2))\cong \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2]_{\x_2}=\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2, \x_2^{-1}]$$

Now functions on $$D(\x_1)\cup D(\x_2)$$ are those obtained by gluing these functions, and such functions must agree when restricted to the intersection $$D(\x_1)\cap D(\x_2)=D(\x_1\x_2)$$. Now comparing elements of $$\mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2}(D(\x_1))$$ and $$\mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2}(D(\x_2))$$ in

$$\mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2}(D(\x_1\x_2))=\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2]_{\x_1\x_2}$$

we see that such functions are only polynomial functions. That is,

$$\mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2}(U)=\mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2]$$

If $$(U, \mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^2}\vert_U)$$ were affine, then $$U$$ would have to be isomorphic to $$\Spec \mathbb{K}[\x_1,\x_2]$$ obtained by taking the global sections, but considering the point in $$U$$ corresponding to the prime ideal $$(\x_1,\x_2)$$,

$$Z(\x_1,\x_2)=Z(\x_1)\cap Z(\x_2)=\emptyset\qquad\text{in $U$}$$

so this is impossible.

</div>

By definition a scheme is made by gluing affine schemes, and in the above example too, to compute sections over $$U$$ we understood it as gluing $$D(\x_1)$$ and $$D(\x_2)$$. Before looking at more examples, let us first examine the following lemma, which will be helpful in dealing with various examples.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem9">**Lemma 9**</ins> Fix an index set $$I$$, and suppose the following data are given.

- Schemes $$X_i$$,
- Open subschemes $$X_{ij}$$ of $$X_i$$,
- For each $$i,j\in I$$, isomorphisms $$\varphi_{ij}:X_{ij} \rightarrow X_{ji}$$

Here we define $$X_{ii}=X_i$$ and $$\varphi_{ii}=\id_{X_i}$$. Then if these data satisfy the *cocycle condition*

$$\varphi_{ik}\vert_{X_{ij}\cap X_{ik}}=\varphi_{jk}\vert_{X_{ji}\cap X_{jk}}\circ \varphi_{ij}\vert_{X_{ij}\cap X_{ik}}\qquad\text{for all $i,j,k\in I$}$$

there exist a unique scheme $$X$$ and open subschemes $$U_i$$ of $$X$$ such that $$U_i\cong X_i$$ and $$U_i\cap U_j\cong X_{ij}$$.

</div>

The proof of this is as follows: first, considering the topological space $$\coprod X_i$$, we can define a relation $$\sim$$ on it by

$$(x_i, X_i)\sim (x_j,X_j) \iff \varphi_{ij}(x_i)=x_j$$

and using the cocycle condition we can prove that this is an equivalence relation. Thus we define the topological space as $$X=\coprod_{i\in X_i}X_i\big/{\sim}$$, and then it suffices to check that sheaves can be glued in the same way as in [[Topology] §Presheaves, ⁋Lemma 1](/en/math/topology/presheaves#lem1). Using this, let us see the following example.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex10">**Example 10**</ins> Suppose two affine lines $$X_0=\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^1=\Spec \mathbb{K}[\x_0]$$, $$X_1=\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{K}^1=\Spec \mathbb{K}[\x_1]$$, and their open subsets

$$U_0=X_0\setminus \{(\x_0)\}=D(\x_0),\quad U_1=X_1\setminus \{(\x_1)\}=D(\x_1)$$

are given. Then by definition

$$\mathscr{O}_{X_0}(U_0)\cong \mathbb{K}[\x_0]_{\x_0}=\mathbb{K}[\x_0,1/\x_0]$$

and similarly $$\mathscr{O}_{X_1}(U_1)\cong \mathbb{K}[\x_1,1/\x_1]$$. In this example, we examine two different ways of gluing $$X_0$$ and $$X_1$$ concretely.

First, consider the case where an isomorphism $$\varphi:(U_0, \mathscr{O}_{X_0}\vert_{U_0})\rightarrow (U_1, \mathscr{O}_{X_1}\vert_{U_1})$$ comes from an isomorphism $$k[\x_0,1/\x_0]\rightarrow k[\x_1,1/\x_1]$$ identifying $$\x_0$$ with $$\x_1$$. Then as a topological space $$X=X_0\cup_\varphi X_1$$ is the line with double origin, and the structure sheaf is defined for any open subset $$U\subseteq X$$ by the formula

$$\mathscr{O}_X(U)=\mathscr{O}_{X_0}(U\cap X_0)\times_{\mathscr{O}_{X_0}(U\cap U_0)\cong \mathscr{O}_{X_1}(U\cap U_1)} \mathscr{O}_{X_1}(U\cap X_1)$$

In particular, the global sections are

$$\Gamma(X, \mathscr{O}_X)=\mathscr{O}_{X_0}(X_0)\times_{\mathscr{O}_{X_0}(U_0)\cong \mathscr{O}_{X_1}(U_1)} \mathscr{O}_{X_1}(X_1)=\mathbb{K}[\x_0]\times_{\mathbb{K}[\x_0,1/\x_0]\cong \mathbb{K}[\x_1,1/\x_1]}\mathbb{K}[\x_1]\cong \mathbb{K}[\x_0]$$

![line_with_two_origins](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Varieties/Schemes-2.png){:style="width:242.7px" class="invert" .align-center}

Meanwhile, this time consider the case where the isomorphism $$\varphi$$ comes from an isomorphism $$\mathbb{K}[\x_0,1/\x_0]\rightarrow \mathbb{K}[\x_1,1/\x_1]$$ identifying $$\x_0$$ with $$1/\x_1$$. Then in particular, the closed point $$(\x_0-\alpha)$$ of $$U_0$$ corresponds to the closed point $$(\x_1-1/\alpha)$$ of $$X_1$$, and the closed point $$(\x_1-\beta)$$ of $$U_1$$ corresponds to the closed point $$(\x_0-1/\beta)$$ of $$U_0$$. That is, the space obtained from this is the projective space $$\mathbb{P}^1$$.

![projective_line](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Varieties/Schemes-3.png){:style="width:127.8px" class="invert" .align-center}

Thinking of the global sections in this case,

$$\Gamma(\mathbb{P}^1, \mathscr{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1})=\mathbb{K}[\x_0]\times_{\mathbb{K}[\x_0,1/\x_0]\cong \mathbb{K}[\x_1,1/\x_1]} \mathbb{K}[\x_1]\cong \mathbb{K}$$

only. The last isomorphism means, in everyday language, that among the elements of $$\mathbb{K}[\x_0]$$, only the constant functions remain elements of $$\mathbb{K}[\x_1]$$ when $$\x_0$$ is replaced by $$1/\x_1$$.

</div>

The last calculation in [Example 10](#ex10) above shows that $$\mathbb{P}^1$$ is not an affine scheme. If $$\mathbb{P}^1$$ were an affine scheme, it would necessarily have to be $$\Spec \mathbb{K}$$, because $$\Spec \mathbb{K}$$ is a scheme consisting of a single point.
