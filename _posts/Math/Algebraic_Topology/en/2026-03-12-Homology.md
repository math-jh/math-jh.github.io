## Simplices

We begin by introducing simplices, which provide intuitive guidance in developing homology theory.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> For any natural number $$k$$, suppose $$k+1$$ points $$v_0,\ldots, v_k\in\mathbb{R}^d$$ are given in general position. Then the *$$k$$-simplex* formed by these points is the smallest convex set containing $$\{v_0,\ldots, v_k\}$$.

</div>

Here, saying that the $$k+1$$ vertices are in general position means that they are not contained in any hyperplane of dimension less than $$k$$. Equivalently, the $$k$$ vectors

$$v_1-v_0,\ldots, v_k-v_0$$

are linearly independent. For example, a $$0$$-simplex is a point, a $$1$$-simplex is a line segment joining two vertices, a $$2$$-simplex is a triangle joining three points, and a $$3$$-simplex is a tetrahedron.

![standard_simplices](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-1.png){:style="width:24em" class="invert" .align-center}

As shown in the figure above, the $$n$$-simplex in $$\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$$ formed by the $$n+1$$ vertices

$$(1,0,\ldots, 0),\qquad\cdots,\qquad (0,0,\ldots,1)$$

is called the standard simplex. Naturally, these simplices themselves are not our primary objects of interest; rather, we aim to use them to compute invariants of manifolds. To this end, we must define a $$\Delta$$-complex structure on a manifold. Let us consider the following simple and intuitive example.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex2">**Example 2**</ins> A standard example we will often consider is the (2-dimensional) torus $$T^2$$. In the simplest terms, this is the product manifold $$S^1\times S^1$$, but to see intuitively that this product is a torus, consider the following figure.

![torus_2D](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-2.png){:style="width:20%" class="invert" .align-center}

In this figure, imagine gluing the edges of each color in the direction of the arrows "without twisting." For instance, if we first glue the horizontal edges to form a cylinder, and then glue the remaining edges to identify the top and bottom of the cylinder, we obtain the following.

![torus_3D](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-3.png){:style="width:40%" class="invert" .align-center}

If the square above were a square in the coordinate plane passing through the four points $$(0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1)$$, then this is the quotient space obtained by imposing the equivalence relation

$$(x,0)\sim (x,1),\qquad (0,y)\sim(1,y)$$

and considering that $$S^1$$ is obtained from the interval $$[0,1]$$ on the line by identifying $$0$$ and $$1$$, we see that this agrees with the definition $$T^2=S^1\times S^1$$. On the other hand, if we take the same square, reverse the direction of one edge, and glue only the horizontal edges, we obtain the following quotient space:

![mobius_2D](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-4.png){:style="width:20%" class="invert" .align-center}

The resulting space is not a cylinder but a Möbius strip:

![mobius_3D](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-5.png){:style="width:60%" class="invert" .align-center}

</div>

In the above example, if we divide the squares drawn on the plane along a diagonal into two triangles, we may regard these squares as two $$2$$-simplices joined together. Transferring this to the quotient space in the same manner, we can understand the spaces in [Example 2](#ex2) as being built by gluing simplices. As this example illustrates, when gluing $$2$$-simplices the orientation of edges is important (more generally, the orientation of $$(n-1)$$-simplices when gluing $$n$$-simplices), and this is determined by specifying a total order on the vertices. For instance, if we declare the ordering $$v_0,\ldots, v_k$$ of the vertices of a $$k$$-simplex according to their indices to be the positive orientation, then applying an odd permutation to obtain $$v_1,v_0,v_2,\ldots,v_k$$ yields the negative orientation. We write a simplex with orientation given by the index order as $$[v_0,\ldots, v_k]$$. Under this notation, whether we view a $$(k-1)$$-simplex obtained by forgetting one vertex of a $$k$$-simplex as a face of the original $$k$$-simplex, or as the following $$(k-1)$$-simplex

$$[v_0,\ldots,\hat{v}_i,\ldots, v_k]=[v_0,\ldots, v_{i-1},v_{i+1},\ldots, v_k]$$

the orientation is the same.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def3">**Definition 3**</ins> For a topological space $$X$$, a *$$\Delta$$-complex structure* on $$X$$ is a collection of maps $$\sigma_\alpha:\Delta^{n(\alpha)}\rightarrow X$$ satisfying the following conditions.

1. The restriction of $$\sigma_\alpha$$ to $$\interior(\Delta^{n(\alpha)})$$ is injective, and for every point $$x\in X$$ there exists exactly one $$\alpha$$ such that $$x\in \sigma_\alpha(\interior(\Delta^{n(\alpha)}))$$.
2. The restriction $$\sigma_\alpha\vert_{\Delta^{n(\alpha)-1}}:\Delta^{n(\alpha)-1}\rightarrow X$$ of $$\sigma_\alpha$$ to a face of $$\Delta^{n(\alpha)}$$ also belongs to this collection.
3. A subset $$A\subseteq X$$ is open in $$X$$ if and only if $$\sigma_\alpha^{-1}(A)$$ is open in $$\Delta^{n(\alpha)}$$ for every $$\alpha$$.

</div>

For instance, the standard simplex $$\Delta^2$$ trivially carries a $$\Delta$$-complex structure; explicitly, the maps defining this structure are

$$\operatorname{id}_{\Delta^2}:\Delta^2\rightarrow\Delta^2$$

together with three maps $$\sigma^1_1,\sigma^1_2,\sigma^1_3$$ sending the $$1$$-simplex $$\Delta^1$$ to each edge, and three maps $$\sigma_1^0,\sigma_2^0,\sigma_3^0$$ sending the $$0$$-simplex $$\Delta^0$$ to each vertex.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex4">**Example 4**</ins> For example, the 2-dimensional torus $$T^2$$ can be represented as shown in the following figure:

![Torus_simplicial_decomposition](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-6.png){:style="width:90%" class="invert" .align-center}

This figure simultaneously gives $$T^2$$ a $$\Delta$$-complex structure.

</div>

## Simplicial Homology

We now define invariants of topological spaces using the $$\Delta$$-complex structure introduced above. More concretely, these will be defined via groups of formal sums of simplices. However, a subtle issue arises here: for this to be an invariant of the topological space $$X$$, it must not depend on the choice of $$\Delta$$-complex structure.

![finer_torus](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-7.png){:style="width:25%" class="invert" .align-center}

For example, if we subdivide the square in [Example 2](#ex2) more finely to create additional 2-simplices as shown above, the newly created gray $$1$$-simplices must cancel each other out in some fashion. That is, when choosing a $$\Delta$$-complex we must attach them with compatible orientations, but when computing invariants we must add them with opposite signs. Keeping this in mind will make the following construction more transparent.

Let $$X$$ be a topological space with a given $$\Delta$$-complex structure, and let $$C^\Delta_k(X)$$ be the free abelian group generated by the $$k$$-simplices. That is,

$$C^\Delta_k(X)=\{\sigma_\alpha:\Delta^{n(\alpha)}\rightarrow X\text{ $k$-simplex}\mid n(\alpha)=k\}\cdot\mathbb{Z}$$

Following the convention for abelian groups, we regard the operation in $$C^\Delta_k(X)$$ as addition. As we saw earlier, the boundary of a $$k$$-simplex $$[v_0,\ldots, v_k]$$ consists of the following $$(k-1)$$-simplices:

$$[v_1,v_2,\ldots, v_k],\quad[v_0,v_2,\ldots, v_k],\quad\cdots,[v_0,v_1,\ldots\hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_k],\quad\cdots,\quad[v_0,v_1,\ldots, v_{k-1}]$$

If we think of the boundary of $$[v_0,\ldots, v_k]$$ as the sum of these, we obtain a map from $$C^\Delta_k(X)$$ to $$\Delta_{k-1}(X)$$. However, if we define the boundary map $$\partial_k$$ not as a simple sum of these simplices, but by the formula

$$\partial_k(\sigma_\alpha|_{[v_0,\ldots,v_k]})=\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^i\sigma_\alpha|_{[v_0,\ldots, \hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_k]}\tag{1}$$

then it is well known that $$(C^\Delta_k(X),\partial_k)$$ forms a chain complex. The signs on the right-hand side are arranged, as noted above, so that adjacent simplices in $$X$$ cancel each other.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop5">**Proposition 5**</ins> $$(C^\Delta_k(X),\partial_k)$$ is a chain complex. ([\[Category Theory\] §Abelian Categories, ⁋Definition 4](/en/math/category_theory/abelian_categories#def4))

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

For any $$\sigma_\alpha\in C^\Delta_k(X)$$,

$$\partial_k(\sigma_\alpha|_{[v_0,\ldots,v_k]})=\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^i\sigma_\alpha|_{[v_0,\ldots, \hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_k]}$$

so

$$\partial_{k-1}\partial_k(\sigma_\alpha|_{[v_0,\ldots,v_k]})=\sum_{j < i}(-1)^{i+j}\sigma_\alpha|_{[v_0,\ldots, \hat{v}_j,\ldots\hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_k]}+\sum_{j > i}(-1)^{i+j-1}\sigma_\alpha|_{[v_0,\ldots, \hat{v}_i,\ldots\hat{v}_j,\ldots,v_k]} $$

and therefore the first and second sums cancel each other.

</details>

The $$n$$th homology of the chain complex $$(C^\Delta_k(X),\partial_k)$$ obtained in this way is called the *$$n$$th simplicial homology* and is denoted $$H_n^\Delta(X)$$. ([\[Homological Algebra\] §Homology, ⁋Definition 2](/en/math/homological_algebra/homology#def2))

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex6">**Example 6**</ins> Represent the 2-dimensional torus $$T^2$$ as in [Example 4](#ex4) above, and label the two $$2$$-simplices as $$L$$ and $$U$$ in the order listed on the right, the three $$1$$-simplices as $$a,b,c$$, and the $$0$$-simplex as $$p$$. Since the two $$1$$-simplices $$b,c$$ already carry given orientations, for these to be simplices $$a$$ must be oriented from the lower left to the upper right. Now, given the vertices $$v_0,v_1,v_2$$ of $$U$$ as shown in

![Torus_upper](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-8.png){:style="width:12em" class="invert" .align-center}

we may write

$$a=[v_0,v_2],\quad b=[v_1,v_2],\quad c=[v_0,v_1]$$

Similarly, given the vertices $$v_0,v_1,v_2$$ of $$L$$ as shown in the following figure:

![Torus_lower](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-9.png){:style="width:12em" class="invert" .align-center}

for $$L$$ we may take

$$a=[v_0,v_2],\quad b=[v_0,v_1],\quad c=[v_1,v_2]$$

Now considering the boundary map $$\partial_2:C^\Delta_2(T^2)\rightarrow C^\Delta_1(T^2)$$, we have

$$\begin{aligned}\partial_2(U)&=[v_1,v_2]-[v_0,v_2]+[v_0,v_1]=b-a+c,\\ \partial_2(L)&=[v_1,v_2]-[v_0,v_2]+[v_0,v_1]=c-a+b\end{aligned}$$

Also, considering $$\partial_1:C^\Delta_1(T^2)\rightarrow C^\Delta_0(T^2)$$, since all these vertices correspond to the same point $$p$$ in $$T^2$$,

$$\partial_1(a)=\partial_1(b)=\partial_1(c)=p-p=0$$

and therefore in the complex

$$\cdots\overset{\partial_3}{\longrightarrow}C^\Delta_2(T^2)=\langle L,U\rangle\overset{\partial_2}{\longrightarrow}C^\Delta_1(T^2)=\langle a,b,c\rangle\overset{\partial_1}{\longrightarrow}C^\Delta_0(T^2)=\langle p\rangle\overset{\partial_0}{\longrightarrow}0$$

we have

$$\ker\partial_2=\langle L-U\rangle,\qquad\ker\partial_1=C^\Delta_1(T^2),\qquad\ker\partial_0=C^\Delta_0(T^2)$$

and

$$\im\partial_3=0,\qquad\im\partial_2=\langle a-b-c\rangle,\qquad \im\partial_1=0$$

so

$$H_2^\Delta(T^2)=\ker\partial_2/\im\partial_3\cong\mathbb{Z},\quad H_1^\Delta(T^2)=\ker\partial_1/\im\partial_2\cong \mathbb{Z}\oplus\mathbb{Z},\quad H_0^\Delta(T^2)=\ker\partial_0/\im\partial_1\cong\mathbb{Z}$$

and the remaining homology groups are all $$0$$.

</div>

Meanwhile, in [\[Homological Algebra\] §Homology, ⁋Definition 2](/en/math/homological_algebra/homology#def2) we called the elements of $$Z_n(C)=\ker\partial_n$$ *$$n$$-cycles* and the elements of $$B_n(C)=\im\partial_{n+1}$$ *$$n$$-boundaries*; their names are now intuitively transparent. That is, in this setting the boundary maps literally compute the boundary of a simplex, and $$n$$-cycles are those whose boundaries cancel out—for instance, in [Example 6](#ex6), the closed curves formed by $$a,b$$ (and $$c$$) in the original space $$T^2$$—while $$n$$-boundaries are precisely those $$n$$-simplices that appear as the boundary of some $$(n+1)$$-simplex.

## Singular Homology

The simplicial homology defined above has an intuitively clear meaning, but its limitation is equally clear: to compute the homology of an arbitrary topological space $$X$$, we must endow it with a $$\Delta$$-complex structure. Even if $$X$$ is a topological manifold, it is well known that if its dimension is $$4$$ or higher, it may be impossible to impose a $$\Delta$$-complex structure on $$X$$.

Therefore, we relax this condition and define a new homology.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def7">**Definition 7**</ins> A *singular $$k$$-simplex* in $$X$$ is a continuous map $$\sigma:\Delta^k\rightarrow X$$.

</div>

Unlike the $$\Delta$$-complex structure defined previously, singular $$k$$-simplices need not preserve the shape of a $$k$$-simplex in $$X$$ in any way. For example, a constant map sending all points of $$\Delta^k$$ to a single point is also a singular $$k$$-simplex.

Now let $$C_k(X)$$ be the free abelian group generated by all singular $$k$$-simplices, and define $$\partial_k:C_k(X)\rightarrow C_{k-1}(X)$$ in the same manner as equation (1) above. Then one can verify in exactly the same way as in [Proposition 5](#prop5) that $$(C_k(X), \partial_k)$$ forms a chain complex; the resulting homology groups are called *singular homology* and denoted $$H_n(X)$$.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex8">**Example 8**</ins> Computing singular homology directly from the definition is not advisable, but for the sake of intuition let us carry out some (non-rigorous) calculations.

By definition, a singular $$0$$-simplex on any topological space $$X$$ is a continuous map from $$\Delta^0$$ to $$X$$. Since $$\Delta^0$$ is just a single point, $$C_0(X)$$ is the free abelian group generated by the points of $$X$$. Similarly, identifying $$\Delta^1$$ with the interval $$I=[0,1]$$, we see that $$C_1(X)$$ is simply the free abelian group generated by paths in $$X$$, where we also allow the constant path $$[0,1]\rightarrow X$$—and it is for this reason that we call it a *singular* $$1$$-simplex. Likewise, after continuous deformation, $$C_2(X)$$ becomes the free abelian group generated by disks contained in $$X$$.

Then the boundary of a path $$\sigma:[0,1]\rightarrow X$$ is given by $$\partial_1\sigma=\sigma(1)-\sigma(0)$$ according to equation (1), whence

$$Z_1(X)=\ker\partial_1=\left\{\sigma:[0,1]\rightarrow X\mid \sigma(1)=\sigma(0)\right\}$$

Intuitively, $$Z_1(X)$$ can be thought of as the subgroup generated by closed curves in $$X$$. Similarly, if we assign geometric meaning to $$B_1(X)=\im\partial_2$$, it consists of those closed curves in $$X$$ that bound some disk; therefore the first homology

$$H_1(X)=\frac{Z_1(X)}{B_1(X)}$$

measures how many closed curves in $$X$$ fail to bound a disk. For example, the first homology of the subset

$$D^2=\left\{(x,y)\in \mathbb{R}^2\mid x^2+y^2\leq 1\right\}$$

of $$\mathbb{R}^2$$ is $$0$$, because any closed curve in $$D^2$$ can trivially be filled in.

![cycle_in_D2](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-10.png){:style="width:32em" class="invert" .align-center}

On the other hand, the first homology of the space $$D^2\setminus {(0,0)}$$ is not $$0$$. For instance, considering the following closed curve:

![cycle_in_punctured_disk](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-11.png){:style="width:18em" class="invert" .align-center}

there is no way to fill its interior (continuously) to form a disk. However, for the punctured space

$$D^3\setminus \left\{(0,0,0)\right\}=\left\{(x,y,z)\in \mathbb{R}^3\mid 0< x^2+y^2+z^2\leq 1\right\}$$

the first homology is $$0$$, because even if we are given a closed curve "encircling the hole"

$$S^1=\left\{(x,y,0)\in \mathbb{R}^3\mid x^2+y^2=1\right\}$$

we can view it as the boundary of a disk as shown below:

![boundary_in_punctured_D3](/assets/images/Math/Algebraic_Topology/Homology-12.png){:style="width:32em" class="invert" .align-center}

Instead, the second homology of this space will not be $$0$$.

</div>

There is a minor gap in this calculation. For example, a constant map sending all points of $$\Delta^1$$ to a fixed $$x\in X$$ is by definition a singular $$1$$-simplex, but when we claim that any closed curve in $$D^2$$ can be filled, we did not account for paths of this (constant) form. However, what we ultimately need to show is that by applying $$\partial$$ to a suitable singular $$2$$-simplex $$\Delta^2 \rightarrow X$$, this singular $$1$$-simplex appears. Thus we may simply take the $$2$$-simplex sending all points of $$\Delta^2$$ to the fixed $$x\in X$$; the boundary of this simplex is exactly the desired $$\Delta^1 \rightarrow X$$. Generalizing, for any $$n$$, a singular $$n$$-simplex sending all points of $$\Delta^n$$ to a fixed $$x\in X$$ is the boundary of a singular $$(n+1)$$-simplex sending all points of $$\Delta^{n+1}$$ to the same fixed $$x\in X$$. In other words, constant maps always become trivial in $$H_n(X)$$.

Gaps of this kind can be closed with a little care as above. In fact, for any space $$X$$ admitting a $$\Delta$$-complex structure, one can show that singular homology $$H_n(X)$$ and simplicial homology $$H_n^\Delta(X)$$ always agree. Roughly speaking, singular maps such as constant $$\Delta^k \rightarrow X$$ become boundaries of similarly singular $$\Delta^{k+1}\rightarrow X$$, so that when comparing the two quotients

$$H_n^\Delta(X)=\frac{\ker\partial_n^\Delta}{\im\partial_{n+1}^\Delta},\qquad H_n(X)=\frac{\ker\partial_n}{\im\partial_{n+1}}$$

the enlargement of $$\ker \partial_n$$ relative to $$\ker\partial_n^\Delta$$, caused by allowing singular $$\Delta^k \rightarrow X$$, is matched by a corresponding enlargement of $$\im\partial_{n+1}$$, so the two quotients coincide.

A more fundamental issue is that this calculation relies entirely on our geometric intuition. To compute the homology of more complicated spaces, we must study more general properties of homology.

## Properties of Homology

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop9">**Proposition 9**</ins> When a topological space $$X$$ is expressed as a disjoint union of path-components $$X=\coprod X_i$$, the following isomorphism holds:

$$H_n(X)\cong \bigoplus_{i\in I} H_n(X_i)$$

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, since the continuous image of the path-connected space $$\Delta^k$$ is path-connected, the images of singular simplices lie entirely within the $$X_i$$'s. Hence $$C_n(X)\cong \bigoplus_{i\in I} C_n(X_i)$$. For the same reason, $$\partial$$ preserves this decomposition, and since direct sums preserve kernels and images of such maps, we obtain the desired result.

</details>

Thus computing the homology of an arbitrary topological space reduces to computing the homology of path-connected spaces. Nevertheless, this remains a difficult problem in general. We cannot carry out the computation in full generality, but the case $$n=0$$ admits a geometric interpretation.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop10">**Proposition 10**</ins> For a non-empty path-connected space $$X$$, we have $$H_0(X)\cong \mathbb{Z}$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, since $$\partial_0=0$$,

$$H_0(X)=\ker\partial_0/\im\partial_1=C_0/\im\partial_1$$

To construct an isomorphism $$H_0(X)\rightarrow\mathbb{Z}$$, define a homomorphism $$\varepsilon:C_0(X)\rightarrow\mathbb{Z}$$ by

$$\varepsilon\left(\sum n_i\sigma_i\right)=\sum_i n_i$$

Since $$X$$ is non-empty, $$\varepsilon$$ is surjective. Therefore, by the first isomorphism theorem, it suffices to show $$\ker\varepsilon=\im\partial_1$$. The inclusion $$\im\partial_1\subseteq\ker\varepsilon$$ is immediate from the definition of $$\partial_1$$, so we need only prove the reverse. Assume $$\varepsilon\left(\sum n_i\sigma_i\right)=0$$, and for each $$i$$ let $$x_i$$ be the image of the $$0$$-simplex $$\sigma_i$$. By path-connectedness of $$X$$, choose a point $$x$$ and paths from $$x$$ to each $$x_i$$; these determine $$1$$-simplices in the corresponding directions. Denoting these by $$\tau_i$$, we have $$\partial \tau_i=\sigma_i-\sigma$$, and therefore

$$\partial\left(\sum_i n_i\tau_i\right)=\sum n_i\sigma_i-\sum n_i \sigma=0$$

which yields the desired result.

</details>

The proof is somewhat lengthy for the sake of rigor, but the essential idea is simple: in a path-connected space $$X$$, any two points can be joined by a path, and viewing this path as a $$1$$-simplex makes the two points the boundary of that $$1$$-simplex; hence with respect to $$B_0(X)=\im\partial_1$$ we may regard the two points as identical.

Conversely, there are cases where we can compute the homology for all $$n$$, namely when $$X$$ is a single point. In this case, regardless of $$k$$, the singular $$k$$-simplex $$\sigma_k:\Delta^k \rightarrow X$$ is uniquely determined (that is, it is the constant map), and considering equation (1), $$\partial_k$$ is $$0$$ when $$k$$ is odd and sends $$\sigma_k$$ to $$\sigma_{k-1}$$ when $$k$$ is even. Hence the chain complex of singular simplices is

$$\cdots\rightarrow \mathbb{Z} \overset{0}{\longrightarrow}\mathbb{Z}\overset{\approx}{\longrightarrow} \mathbb{Z}\overset{0}{\longrightarrow}\mathbb{Z}\rightarrow0$$

and we obtain the following.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop11">**Proposition 11**</ins> For a one-point space $$X$$, we have $$H_0(X)\cong \mathbb{Z}$$ and $$H_k(X)\cong 0$$ for all $$k>0$$.

</div>

However, the most important property is arguably functoriality. We already know that in the category $$\Ch_{\geq 0}(\Ab)$$ of chain complexes in $$\Ab$$, the $$n$$th homology $$H_n:\Ch_{\geq 0}(\Ab)\rightarrow \Ab$$ is a functor for each $$n$$. Therefore, to show that the composition

$$\Top \rightarrow \Ch_{\geq 0}(\Ab)\rightarrow \Ab$$

is a functor, it suffices to show that $$\Top \rightarrow \Ch_{\geq 0}(\Ab)$$ is a functor.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop12">**Proposition 12**</ins> $$\Top\rightarrow\Ch_{\geq 0}(\Ab)$$ is a functor.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

That is, we must show that for any continuous map $$f:X\rightarrow Y$$, there exists a chain map $$C_\bullet(f):C_\bullet(X)\rightarrow C_\bullet(Y)$$. Naturally, we define $$C_\bullet(f)$$ by

$$C_\bullet(f):\sigma\mapsto f\circ\sigma$$

and the key point is to verify that this is a chain map. For any $$\sigma:\Delta^n \rightarrow X$$,

$$\begin{aligned}(C_\bullet(f)\circ\partial^X_n)(\sigma)&=C_\bullet(f)\left(\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^i\sigma\vert_{[v_0,\ldots,\hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_n]}\right)=\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^iC_\bullet(f)(\sigma\vert_{[v_0,\ldots,\hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_n]})\\&=\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^i f\circ(\sigma\vert_{[v_0,\ldots,\hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_n]})=\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^i (f\circ\sigma)\vert_{[v_0,\ldots,\hat{v}_i,\ldots,v_n]}\\&=\partial_n^Y(f\circ\sigma)=(\partial_n^Y\circ C_\bullet(f))(\sigma)\end{aligned}$$

which completes the proof.

</details>

Finally, we make the following definition.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def13">**Definition 13**</ins> If $$H_n(X)\cong H_n(Y)$$ holds for all $$n$$, we say that the two spaces $$X,Y$$ are *homologous*.

</div>
