In the posts of this category, we study fields. In particular, our goal is to introduce Galois theory.

## Prime Fields

First, let us recall the following definition.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def1">**Definition 1**</ins> A commutative division ring is called a *field*. ([\[Algebraic Structures\] §Field of Fractions, ⁋Definition 3](/en/math/algebraic_structures/field_of_fractions#def3))

</div>

Fields are commonly denoted by symbols such as $$\mathbb{K}$$. Since a field is a ring satisfying certain conditions, we can collect only these to form a subcategory $$\Field$$ of $$\Ring$$. That is, $$\Field$$ is a full subcategory of $$\Ring$$.

Previously, we defined every object of $$\Ring$$ to have a $$1$$, and every ring homomorphism to preserve $$1$$. ([\[Algebraic Structures\] §Definition of a Ring, ⁋Definition 3](/en/math/algebraic_structures/rings#def3)) Therefore, any subring of a ring $$A$$ also has a multiplicative identity, which is exactly the multiplicative identity $$1$$ of $$A$$. From this we also know that $$\mathbb{Z}$$ is the initial object of $$\Ring$$.

The following proposition, though obvious, is quite interesting: it shows that a ring homomorphism between fields is either an inclusion or the zero map.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop2">**Proposition 2**</ins> Let $$\mathbb{K},\mathbb{K}'$$ be arbitrary fields, and consider a ring homomorphism $$f:\mathbb{K} \rightarrow \mathbb{K}'$$. Then $$f$$ is either the zero map or an inclusion.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

$$\ker f$$ is an ideal of $$\mathbb{K}$$. Now see [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Field of Fractions, ⁋Proposition 4](/en/math/algebraic_structures/field_of_fractions#prop4).

</details>

Meanwhile, in the post [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Field of Fractions](/en/math/algebraic_structures/field_of_fractions) we obtained $$\mathbb{Q}$$ as the total field of fractions $$\Frac(\mathbb{Z})$$ of $$\mathbb{Z}$$. The following example exhibits a field different from $$\mathbb{Q}$$.

<div class="example" markdown="1">

<ins id="ex3">**Example 3**</ins> Fix a prime $$p$$. Then the quotient ring $$\mathbb{F}_p := \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$$ of $$\mathbb{Z}$$ is a field.

To verify this, let an arbitrary nonzero $$a+p\mathbb{Z}\in \mathbb{F}_p$$ be given. Then $$\gcd(a,p)=1$$, so by [\[Ring Theory\] §Integral Domains, ⁋Corollary 9](/en/math/ring_theory/integral_domains#cor9)

$$1=ax+bp$$

for some $$x, b\in \mathbb{Z}$$, and it suffices to reduce both sides modulo $$p$$.

</div>

Collectively, we call these *prime fields*. Then the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm4">**Theorem 4**</ins> Let $$A$$ be a ring having some subfield. Then $$A$$ has a unique prime subfield $$\mathbb{P}$$. In this case, $$\mathbb{K}$$ belongs to the center of $$A$$ and is contained in every subfield of $$A$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $$\mathbb{K}$$ be a subfield of $$A$$. Let $$C$$ be the center of $$A$$; then $$\mathbb{K}':= \mathbb{K} \cap C$$ is also a subfield of $$A$$.

Consider the unique ring homomorphism $$f: \mathbb{Z} \to A$$ and let its kernel be $$\mathfrak{p}$$. Then every subring of $$A$$ contains $$\im(f)$$; in particular, $$\mathbb{K}'$$ also contains $$\im(f)$$. Now $$\mathbb{K}'$$ is a field, hence an integral domain, and thus $$(0)$$ is a prime ideal of $$\mathbb{K}'$$; therefore by [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Field of Fractions, ⁋Proposition 9](/en/math/algebraic_structures/field_of_fractions#prop9), either $$\mathfrak{p} = (0)$$ or $$\mathfrak{p} = (p)$$ for some prime $$p$$.

If $$\mathfrak{p}=(0)$$, then $$f$$ is an embedding from $$\mathbb{Z}$$ into $$\mathbb{K}'$$, and therefore $$\mathbb{Q}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{K}'$$ is induced from [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Field of Fractions, ⁋Theorem 1](/en/math/algebraic_structures/field_of_fractions#thm1).

If $$\mathfrak{p} = (p)$$, then by the first isomorphism theorem a homomorphic image of $$\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{F}_p$$ defines a subfield of $$\mathbb{K}'$$.

Thus, in both cases we know that a prime field $$\mathbb{P}$$ exists inside $$\mathbb{K}'$$, and this $$\mathbb{P}$$ is a subfield of $$A$$ lying in the center $$C$$.

Now consider another subfield $$L$$ of $$A$$. $$\mathbb{P} \cap L$$ is a subfield of $$A$$ and a subset of $$\mathbb{P}$$, so $$\mathbb{P} \cap L = \mathbb{P}$$, and therefore $$\mathbb{P} \subseteq L$$. That is, every subfield contains $$\mathbb{P}$$, so $$\mathbb{P}$$ is contained in every subfield of $$A$$, and is unique as a prime field.

</details>

Since we are mostly interested in the case where the ring $$A$$ is itself a field, the hypothesis of the above theorem is not very restrictive. For instance, we obtain the following corollaries.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="cor5">**Corollary 5**</ins> A field $$\mathbb{K}$$ has a unique prime subfield $$\mathbb{P}$$, and $$\mathbb{P}$$ is the smallest among the subfields of $$\mathbb{K}$$.

</div>

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="cor6">**Corollary 6**</ins> A field $$\mathbb{K}$$ is a prime field if and only if it has no subfields other than itself.

</div>

From this we now know that whenever an arbitrary field $$\mathbb{K}$$ is given, there exist a prime field $$\mathbb{P}$$, defined in a unique way, and an injection $$\mathbb{P}\hookrightarrow \mathbb{K}$$. In general, we call an injective ring homomorphism $$f:A \rightarrow B$$ an *extension*, so this can be rewritten as the following slogan:

> Every field is an extension of a prime field in a unique way.

## Characteristic

Using prime fields, we can define the characteristic of a ring. Assume that a ring $$A$$ has some subfield. Then by [Theorem 4](#thm4), this $$A$$ has a unique prime subfield $$\mathbb{P}$$. On the other hand, examining the proof of [Theorem 4](#thm4), the method of obtaining this $$\mathbb{P}$$ is also given quite explicitly; since $$\mathbb{P}$$ must contain the multiplicative identity $$1$$ of $$A$$, from this

$$n.x=\underbrace{1+1+\cdots+1}_\text{\scriptsize$n$ times}$$

must also belong to $$\mathbb{P}$$, and if this ever becomes $$0$$, then $$\mathbb{P}$$ is isomorphic to $$\mathbb{F}_p$$; otherwise $$\mathbb{P}$$ is $$\mathbb{Q}$$. Taking this as motivation, we define the following.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def7">**Definition 7**</ins> Assume that a ring $$A$$ has a subfield. Then, in the proof of [Theorem 4](#thm4), we define the positive integer $$n$$ generating the kernel of $$f:\mathbb{Z} \rightarrow A$$ as the *characteristic* of the ring $$A$$, and write it as $$\ch(A)$$.

</div>

That is, if $$A$$ has a subfield and hence a prime subfield $$\mathbb{P}$$, then $$\ch A=0$$ is equivalent to $$\mathbb{P}\cong \mathbb{Q}$$, and $$\ch A=p$$ is equivalent to $$\mathbb{P}\cong \mathbb{F}_p$$; no other cases exist.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop8">**Proposition 8**</ins> Let $$A$$ be a nonzero ring. Then the following hold:

1. $$A$$ has characteristic $$0$$ if and only if for every $$n\neq 0$$, the map $$x\mapsto n.x$$ is a bijection from $$A$$ to $$A$$.
2. For a prime $$p$$, $$\ch(A)=p$$ if and only if $$p.x=0$$ for all $$x\in A$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

This follows because, for any integer $$n$$ and element $$x\in A$$, considering the unique ring homomorphism $$f$$ from $$\mathbb{Z}$$ to $$A$$, the formula

$$n.x=\underbrace{1+1+\cdots+1}_\text{\scriptsize$n$ times}=f(n)x$$

holds.

</details>

The objects we are familiar with are of course those with $$\ch(A)=0$$. If $$\ch(A)=p$$, several interesting results hold. To see this, let us first prove the following lemma.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="lem9">**Lemma 9**</ins> Fix a prime $$p$$. Then for an integer $$i$$ with $$1 \leq i \leq p-1$$, the binomial coefficient $$\binom{p}{i}$$ is divisible by $$p$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

We prove this by induction on $$i$$. For $$i = 1$$, we have $$\binom{p}{1} = p$$, so there is nothing more to prove. Now assume for $$2 \leq i \leq p-1$$ that $$\binom{p}{i-1}$$ is divisible by $$p$$. Then from the identity

$$i \cdot \binom{p}{i} = (p - i + 1) \cdot \binom{p}{i-1}$$

the right-hand side is a multiple of $$p$$ by the induction hypothesis. On the other hand, since $$i$$ is not divisible by $$p$$, it follows that $$\binom{p}{i}$$ must be divisible by $$p$$.

</details>

Now the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm10">**Theorem 10**</ins> Let $$A$$ be a commutative ring of characteristic $$p>0$$. Then the function

$$\Frob_p: A \rightarrow A;\qquad a \mapsto a^p$$

is an endomorphism of $$A$$. That is, the two identities

$$(a + b)^p = a^p + b^p,\qquad (ab)^p = a^p b^p$$

hold for all $$a,b\in A$$.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

The multiplicative identity is immediate since $$A$$ is commutative. For addition, by the binomial expansion

$$(a + b)^p = a^p + b^p + \sum_{i = 1}^{p-1} \binom{p}{i}. a^i b^{p-i}$$

we see that by [Lemma 9](#lem9), for all $$i$$ with $$1 \leq i \leq p-1$$ we have $$\binom{p}{i} \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$$, and therefore by [Proposition 8](#prop8)

$$\binom{p}{i}. a^i b^{p-i} = 0$$

holds for all $$1\leq i\leq p-1$$. From this we obtain the desired equality.

</details>

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def11">**Definition 11**</ins> When a commutative ring $$A$$ has characteristic $$p$$, the function in [Theorem 10](#thm10)

$$A \to A,\qquad a \mapsto a^p$$

is called the *Frobenius endomorphism*.

</div>

Then the $$f$$-fold composition $$\Frob_p^f$$ of the Frobenius endomorphism is given by the formula

$$a\mapsto a^{p^f}$$

and this is still an endomorphism of $$A$$, so in particular

$$(a_1+\cdots+a_n)^{p^f}=a_1^{p^f}+\cdots+a_n^{p^f}$$

always holds and each $$a_k^{p^f}$$ is an element of $$A$$. More generally, for a subset $$S$$ of $$A$$, we write $$S^{p^f}$$ for the image of $$S$$ under the $$f$$-fold composition of the Frobenius endomorphism.

We introduce a few more notations. For a subring $$K$$ of $$A$$ and an arbitrary subset $$S\subset A$$, we denote by $$K[S]$$ the subring of $$A$$ generated by $$K\cup S$$. If $$A$$ is a field, then by [\[Algebraic Structures\] §Field of Fractions, ⁋Theorem 1](/en/math/algebraic_structures/field_of_fractions#thm1), from $$K[S]\hookrightarrow A$$ a ring homomorphism

$$\Frac(K[S]) \rightarrow A$$

is induced, and through this we can regard $$\Frac(K[S])$$ as a subfield of $$A$$. We shall write this as $$K(S)$$. Then $$K(S)$$ is, by definition, the smallest subfield of $$A$$ containing $$K\cup S$$.

Then under this notation the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop12">**Proposition 12**</ins> Fix a commutative ring $$A$$ of characteristic $$p>0$$, a subring $$K$$ of $$A$$, a subset $$S$$ of $$A$$, and a positive integer $$f$$.

1. The equality $$K[S]^{p^f} = K^{p^f}[S^{p^f}]$$ holds. If $$A$$ is a field, then $$K(S)^{p^f} = K^{p^f}(S^{p^f})$$ also holds.
2. If the set $$\{a_i\}_{i \in I}$$ generates $$K[S]$$ as a $$K$$-module, then the set $$\{a_i^{p^f}\}_{i \in I}$$ generates $$K[S]^{p^f}$$ as a $$K^{p^f}$$-module.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. Since the left-hand side of the equality is $$\Frob_p^f(K[S])$$, the claim amounts to saying that the subring of $$A$$ generated by $$K^{p^f}\cup S^{p^f}=\Frob_p^f(K)\cup\Frob_p^f(S)$$ equals $$\Frob_p^f(K[S])$$, which is obvious.
2. Since $$K[S]$$ is generated by $$K$$-linear combinations of the $$a_i$$, we see from the binomial expansion and [Theorem 10](#thm10) that $$K[S]^{p^f}$$ is generated by $$K^{p^f}$$-linear combinations of the $$a_i^{p^f}$$.

</details>

## Perfect Rings

We now define the following.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def13">**Definition 13**</ins> Consider a ring $$A$$ of characteristic $$p \neq 0$$ and the Frobenius map $$\Frob_p : A \to A$$.

1. If $$\Frob_p$$ is surjective, we call $$A$$ a *semi-perfect ring*.
2. If $$\Frob_p$$ is bijective, we call $$A$$ a *perfect ring*.

</div>

If $$A$$ is a perfect ring, then $$\Frob_p^{-1}$$ is also an (auto)morphism from $$A$$ to $$A$$, so it is appropriate to write this as $$a\mapsto a^{p^{-f}}$$ or $$a^{1/p^f}$$. As before, for a subset $$S$$ of $$A$$, we write $$S^{1/p^f}$$ for the image of $$S$$ under $$(\Frob_p^{-1})^f$$.

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def14">**Definition 14**</ins> For a commutative ring $$A$$ of characteristic $$p \ne 0$$, a *perfect closure* is a pair $$(A^{1/p^\infty}, \phi)$$ satisfying the following two conditions.

- $$A^{1/p^\infty}$$ is a perfect ring of characteristic $$p$$.
- $$\phi : A \to A^{1/p^\infty}$$ is a ring homomorphism satisfying the following universal property.
    >
    > Whenever a perfect ring $$B$$ of characteristic $$p$$ and a ring homomorphism $$f:A \rightarrow B$$ are given, there exists a unique ring homomorphism $$\hat{f}: A^{1/p^\infty} \rightarrow B$$ such that $$f=\hat{f}\circ\phi$$.

</div>

If the perfect closure of $$A$$ exists, it is uniquely determined by definition. The following theorem shows that every commutative ring of characteristic $$p\neq 0$$ has a perfect closure.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="thm15">**Theorem 15**</ins> For a commutative ring $$A$$ of characteristic $$p \ne 0$$, the perfect closure $$(A^{1/p^\infty}, \phi)$$ of $$A$$ exists. Furthermore, the following hold.

1. The kernel of $$\phi$$ is the set of all nilpotent elements of $$A$$.
2. For any $$x \in A^{1/p^\infty}$$, there exists some $$n > 0$$ such that $$x^{p^n} \in \phi(A)$$.

</div>

Instead of a proof, we briefly sketch the idea. Intuitively, the condition that $$\Frob_p$$ is bijective means that every element of $$A$$ has a (unique) $$p$$-th root. Therefore, the perfect closure of $$A$$ is obtained by adjoining $$p$$-th roots of all elements of $$A$$.

For this purpose we consider the directed system

$$A_0=A\overset{\Frob_p}{\longrightarrow} A_1=A\overset{\Frob_p}{\longrightarrow} A_2=A\rightarrow\cdots$$

The direct limit of this directed system

$$\varinjlim A=\left(\coprod A\right)\bigg/{\sim},\qquad \text{$a_i\sim a_j\iff \Frob_p^{k-i}(a_i)=\Frob_p^{k-j}(a_j)$ for some $k\geq i,j$}$$

is considered; then an arbitrary element of $$\varinjlim A$$ is represented by suitable $$i$$ and $$a_i\in A_i$$. We write this as

$$[a_i\in A_i]$$

and define the canonical morphisms as

$$\phi_i: A_i \rightarrow \varinjlim A;\qquad a_i\mapsto [a_i\in A_i]$$

Our claim is that $$A^{1/p^\infty}=\varinjlim A$$ and $$\phi=\phi_0$$ satisfy the desired conditions. This is consistent with our intuition if we write out the multiplication in $$\varinjlim A$$ explicitly. By definition, the product of two elements $$[a_i\in A_i]$$ and $$[a_j\in A_j]$$ in $$\varinjlim A$$ is, assuming without loss of generality that $$j\geq i$$, given by the formula

$$[\Frob_p^{j-i}(a_j)\in A_j]=[a_i^{p^{j-i}}a_j\in A_j]$$

Our intuition is that $$[a_j\in A_j]$$ corresponds to $$a_j^{1/p^j}$$ in $$\varinjlim A$$, which means

$$[a_j\in A_0]=[a_j\in A_j]^{p^j}$$

Then the right-hand side is $$[a_j^{p^j}\in A_j]=[\Frob_p^j(a_j)]$$, so the above formula holds.

It should be noted that, as can be seen from the first result of the theorem, $$\phi: a\mapsto [a\in A_0]$$ is not injective, and this is obvious from a short calculation.

For unified notation, it is sometimes convenient to set the case $$\ch A=0$$ to be $$1$$. To this end, we define the *characteristic exponent* of a field $$\mathbb{K}$$ to be $$1$$ if $$\ch A=0$$, and $$p$$ if $$\ch A=p>0$$.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop16">**Proposition 16**</ins> Consider a field $$\mathbb{K}$$ of characteristic exponent $$q$$. Then for each $$f\geq 0$$, the map $$x\mapsto x^{q^f}$$ induces an isomorphism from $$\mathbb{K}$$ onto some subfield $$\mathbb{K}^{q^f}$$ of $$\mathbb{K}$$.

</div>
<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $$p=0$$, then $$q=1$$, and thus the given function is the identity map, so there is nothing to prove. If $$q \ne 1$$, then $$q$$ is a positive prime, so the characteristic of $$K$$ is a positive prime $$p$$, and $$q = p$$. Then the given function is the $$f$$-fold composition of the Frobenius endomorphism and is a surjective ring homomorphism from $$\mathbb{K}$$ onto $$\mathbb{K}^{q^f}$$. That this is injective is obvious because $$\mathbb{K}$$ is a field and therefore has no nilpotent elements.

</details>

<div class="definition" markdown="1">

<ins id="def17">**Definition 17**</ins> For a field $$\mathbb{K}$$ of characteristic exponent $$q$$, if the function in [Proposition 16](#prop16) is an isomorphism from $$\mathbb{K}$$ onto $$\mathbb{K}$$, we call $$\mathbb{K}$$ a *perfect field*. Otherwise, we call it an *imperfect field*.

</div>

That is, for a field $$\mathbb{K}$$ to be perfect, either (1) its characteristic exponent is $$1$$, or (2) its characteristic exponent is $$q$$ and it is a perfect ring in the sense of [Definition 14](#def14).

Then the following holds.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop18">**Proposition 18**</ins> For a field $$\mathbb{K}$$, the following hold.

1. If $$\ch(\mathbb{K})=0$$, then $$\mathbb{K}$$ is perfect.
2. If $$\mathbb{K}$$ is a finite set, then $$\mathbb{K}$$ is perfect.

</div>

<details class="proof" markdown="1">
<summary>Proof</summary>

It suffices to show the second claim; we already saw in the proof of that proposition that the function $$\mathbb{K} \rightarrow \mathbb{K}^{q^f}\subset \mathbb{K}$$ in [Proposition 16](#prop16) is always injective without any condition on $$\mathbb{K}$$. Now if $$\mathbb{K}$$ is a finite set, then by cardinality we must have $$\mathbb{K}^{q^f}=\mathbb{K}$$.

</details>

Meanwhile, we have previously examined the module $$\Omega_{E/A}$$ of Kähler differentials for an arbitrary commutative ring $$A$$ and its polynomial algebra $$E=A[\x_i]_{i\in I}$$. ([\[Multilinear Algebra\] §Differential Modules, ⁋Example 10](/en/math/multilinear_algebra/differential_modules#ex10)) According to this structure, for any polynomial $$f\in E$$, the differential $$df\in\Omega_{E/A}$$ is given by the formula

$$df=\sum_{i\in I} \frac{\partial f}{\partial\x_i}\mathop{d\x_i}$$

Therefore, if $$A$$ has characteristic $$p$$, the image of the polynomial $$\x_i^p$$ in $$\Omega_{E/A}$$ will be $$0$$. The following proposition refines this observation more completely.

<div class="proposition" markdown="1">

<ins id="prop19">**Proposition 19**</ins> Let $$A$$ be a commutative ring and $$E=A[\x_i]_{i\in I}$$ its polynomial algebra, and let $$S$$ be the subset of $$E$$ consisting of polynomials $$f$$ satisfying $$df=0$$. Then the following hold.

1. If $$\ch(A)=0$$, then $$S=A$$.
2. If $$\ch(A)=p$$, then $$S=A[\x_i^p]_{i\in I}$$, and furthermore if $$A$$ is perfect, then $$A=E^p$$ holds.

</div>
